1. ACUTE CORONARY SYNDROME (ACS)

STEMI ‚Ä¢ NSTEMI ‚Ä¢ Unstable Angina
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: Substernal chest pain/pressure >20 minutes, radiation to jaw/left arm/back, diaphoresis, nausea, dyspnea. Pain not relieved by rest or nitroglycerin.

Alternate Presentations:

  • Diabetics/Elderly/Women: Atypical symptoms - dyspnea alone, weakness, epigastric pain, syncope
  • Silent MI: No chest pain (30% of diabetics)
  • RV Infarction: Hypotension with clear lungs, JVD, Kussmaul sign
  • Posterior MI: Isolated ST depression V1-V3, need posterior leads (V7-V9)
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Immediate: ECG within 10 minutes, troponin (repeat at 3 and 6 hours if initial negative), CBC, BMP, PT/INR, lipid panel, CXR

STEMI Criteria: ST elevation ‚â•1mm in 2+ contiguous leads OR new LBBB OR ST depression V1-V3 (posterior MI)

Calculations: HEART Score, TIMI STEMI, TIMI NSTEMI, GRACE Score

üßÆ HEART Score Calculator
Use: Risk stratification for chest pain patients to determine if safe for outpatient evaluation. Score 0-3 = low risk (outpatient), 4-6 = moderate risk (observe), 7-10 = high risk (admit).
üßÆ TIMI Risk Score - STEMI
Use: Predicts 30-day mortality in STEMI patients. Score 0-2 = 2% mortality, 3-4 = 5%, 5-6 = 12%, >6 = 36%.
üßÆ TIMI Risk Score - NSTEMI/UA
Use: Risk of death, MI, or urgent revascularization within 14 days. Score 0-2 = 5% risk (low), 3-4 = 13% (moderate), 5-7 = 41% (high).
üßÆ GRACE Score Calculator
Use: Estimates in-hospital and 6-month mortality in ACS. Low risk <109, intermediate 109-140, high >140. More accurate than TIMI for mortality prediction.
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Initial Management (MONA-BAH):

  • Morphine: 2-4mg IV PRN (use cautiously, may worsen outcomes)
  • Oxygen: Only if SpO2 <90%
  • Nitroglycerin: 0.4mg SL q5min √ó 3 (avoid if RV infarct, SBP <90, recent PDE5 inhibitor)
  • Aspirin: 325mg PO chewed immediately
  • Œ≤-Blocker: Metoprolol 25-50mg PO (avoid if HF, bradycardia, hypotension)
  • Anticoagulation: Heparin bolus + infusion or enoxaparin
  • High-intensity statin: Atorvastatin 80mg

P2Y12 Inhibitor: Ticagrelor 180mg load (preferred) or clopidogrel 600mg load

STEMI Specific: Door-to-balloon <90 minutes. Call cath lab immediately. If PCI unavailable within 120 min, consider fibrinolytics (tPA, reteplase, tenecteplase)

NSTEMI Specific: Risk stratify with TIMI/GRACE. High-risk ‚Üí early invasive strategy (<24hr). Low-risk ‚Üí medical management, stress test before discharge

üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • Sgarbossa Criteria: Diagnose STEMI with LBBB: ST elevation ‚â•1mm concordant with QRS (5 points), ST depression ‚â•1mm V1-V3 (3 points), ST elevation ‚â•5mm discordant (2 points). Score ‚â•3 = STEMI
  • Wellens Syndrome: Biphasic or deep inverted T waves V2-V3 in pain-free patient = critical LAD stenosis, high risk of anterior STEMI. No stress test! Go to cath.
  • RV Infarction Pearl: If inferior STEMI with hypotension, always get right-sided leads (V4R). Treat with aggressive IVF, avoid nitrates/diuretics
  • Troponin timing: Can be negative first 2-4 hours. Serial troponins essential if high suspicion
  • Don't forget: Type 2 MI (supply-demand mismatch from anemia, sepsis, tachyarrhythmia) - treat underlying cause, not necessarily cath
‚ö†Ô∏è ESCALATE TO ICU IF:
  • Hemodynamic instability (SBP <90, shock)
  • Malignant arrhythmias (VT/VF, high-grade AV block)
  • Mechanical complications (papillary rupture, VSD, free wall rupture)
  • Acute heart failure/cardiogenic shock
  • Need for pressors or mechanical support (IABP, Impella)
  • Post-cardiac arrest
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • Hemodynamically stable >24 hours
  • No recurrent chest pain
  • No arrhythmias requiring intervention
  • LVEF assessed (echo)
  • Successful revascularization (if indicated)
  • On DAPT, statin, Œ≤-blocker, ACE-I (if EF <40%)
  • Cardiac rehab referral arranged
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

Early (hours-days): Arrhythmias (VF, VT, bradycardia), cardiogenic shock, mechanical complications (papillary muscle rupture, VSD, free wall rupture), acute MR

Late (days-weeks): Heart failure, LV thrombus, pericarditis (early: Dressler's syndrome 2-10 weeks), recurrent ischemia

2. ACUTE DECOMPENSATED HEART FAILURE

HFrEF ‚Ä¢ HFpEF ‚Ä¢ Acute Exacerbation
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: Progressive dyspnea, orthopnea, PND, lower extremity edema, weight gain. Physical exam: JVD, bibasilar rales, S3 gallop, peripheral edema, hepatomegaly.

Alternate Presentations:

  • Flash Pulmonary Edema: Acute onset, often with HTN emergency, minimal peripheral edema
  • High Output HF: Warm extremities, bounding pulses (thyrotoxicosis, AV fistula, severe anemia)
  • RV Failure: Isolated lower extremity edema, ascites, hepatic congestion without pulmonary edema
  • Elderly: Confusion, fatigue, decreased appetite may predominate over dyspnea
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Labs: BNP/NT-proBNP (BNP >400 or NT-proBNP >900 suggests HF), troponin (r/o ACS), BMP (assess renal function), CBC, Mg, TSH, LFTs

Imaging: CXR (cardiomegaly, pulmonary edema, pleural effusions), Echo (assess EF, valves, wall motion)

ECG: Look for ischemia, LVH, arrhythmias

Calculations: NYHA Class, EF%, fluid balance (daily weights), creatinine clearance

Classify Hemodynamics: Warm vs cold (perfusion), wet vs dry (volume status)

TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Initial Management:

  • O2: Maintain SpO2 >90%. Consider NIPPV (BiPAP/CPAP) if respiratory distress
  • Diuresis (Wet): IV furosemide 40mg (or 2√ó home dose) bolus, then assess response. Consider continuous infusion if poor response. Goal negative 1-2L/day
  • Vasodilators (if SBP >90): Nitroglycerin IV for afterload reduction, especially if hypertensive
  • Sodium/Fluid Restriction: <2g sodium/day, <2L fluid/day
  • Daily weights: Essential for monitoring

Cold & Wet (Low Output): Consider inotropes (dobutamine, milrinone) with invasive monitoring

Chronic HF Management (Initiate/Optimize):

  • HFrEF (EF <40%): Guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT)
  • ACE-I/ARB or ARNI (sacubitril-valsartan)
  • Œ≤-blocker (carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, bisoprolol) - start low when stable
  • Aldosterone antagonist (spironolactone, eplerenone) if EF <35%
  • SGLT2 inhibitor (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin) - proven mortality benefit
  • HFpEF (EF ‚â•50%): Treat HTN, AF, ischemia. SGLT2i now indicated
üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • BNP Pearls: Obesity lowers BNP. Renal failure raises it. Age-adjust NT-proBNP: <50yo: >450, 50-75yo: >900, >75yo: >1800
  • Diuretic Resistance: If poor response to IV lasix, try: (1) Increase dose, (2) Continuous infusion, (3) Add thiazide (metolazone 2.5-5mg 30min before lasix), (4) Add acetazolamide
  • Cardiorenal Syndrome: Cr bump with diuresis is often acceptable if congestion improving. Monitor BUN:Cr ratio (>20:1 suggests prerenal)
  • Don't stop GDMT: Avoid stopping ACE-I/Œ≤-blocker acutely unless cardiogenic shock. Can hold during diuresis, restart before discharge
  • B-type natriuretic peptide diuretics: Nesiritide (BNP analog) has no mortality benefit and increases hypotension - rarely used
‚ö†Ô∏è ESCALATE TO ICU IF:
  • Cardiogenic shock (SBP <90, cool extremities, AMS, oliguria)
  • Need for inotropes or mechanical support
  • Acute respiratory failure requiring intubation
  • Refractory pulmonary edema
  • Malignant arrhythmias
  • ACS with hemodynamic instability
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • Euvolemic (at or near dry weight)
  • No dyspnea at rest
  • Stable vital signs, O2 sat on room air
  • Transition to PO diuretics established
  • On optimized GDMT (HFrEF)
  • BMP stable, Cr at acceptable baseline
  • Patient education completed
  • Close follow-up arranged (within 7 days)
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

During Admission: Cardiorenal syndrome, electrolyte abnormalities (hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, hyponatremia), arrhythmias (AF most common), worsening renal function

Long-term: Recurrent admissions (50% at 6 months), progressive renal dysfunction, sudden cardiac death, need for advanced therapies (LVAD, transplant)

3. ATRIAL FIBRILLATION

RVR ‚Ä¢ New-Onset ‚Ä¢ Chronic AF Management
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: Palpitations, irregular pulse, dyspnea, chest discomfort, fatigue, lightheadedness. Irregularly irregular rhythm on exam.

Alternate Presentations:

  • Asymptomatic: 30-40% discovered incidentally on exam or ECG
  • Embolic stroke: First presentation in some patients
  • Tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy: Chronic RVR leading to heart failure
  • Hyperthyroid AF: Lone AF in young patient, check TSH
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Labs: TSH (always), BMP, Mg, CBC, troponin (if chest pain), digoxin level (if on digoxin)

Imaging: ECG (confirm AF, assess rate, look for ischemia/WPW), CXR, TTE (assess for structural disease, valve disease, LV function, LA size)

TEE: Before cardioversion if AF >48hr or unknown duration (r/o LA thrombus), unless patient on therapeutic anticoagulation ‚â•3 weeks

Calculations:

  • CHA‚ÇÇDS‚ÇÇ-VASc: Stroke risk (CHF, HTN, Age ‚â•75 [2pts], DM, Stroke/TIA [2pts], Vascular disease, Age 65-74, Sex [female]). Score ‚â•2 (‚ôÇ) or ‚â•3 (‚ôÄ) ‚Üí anticoagulate
  • HAS-BLED: Bleeding risk (HTN, Abnormal renal/liver, Stroke, Bleeding, Labile INR, Elderly, Drugs/alcohol). Score ‚â•3 = high bleeding risk
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Rate Control (First-line for most):

  • Œ≤-Blockers: Metoprolol 25-50mg PO q6h or 2.5-5mg IV, esmolol gtt (ICU). Goal HR <110 at rest
  • Non-dihydropyridine CCB: Diltiazem 0.25mg/kg IV over 2min, then 5-15mg/hr gtt OR 30-60mg PO q6h
  • Digoxin: 0.25mg IV/PO (less effective, use if HF or sedentary patients). Loading: 0.5mg ‚Üí 0.25mg q6h √ó 2
  • Avoid: Œ≤-blockers and CCBs together (risk of heart block), rate control in WPW (can precipitate VF)

Rhythm Control:

  • Indications: Hemodynamic instability, highly symptomatic despite rate control, first episode, young patient preference
  • Cardioversion: If AF <48hr OR on anticoagulation ‚â•3 weeks OR TEE negative
  • Synchronized DC cardioversion: 120-200J biphasic (under sedation)
  • Chemical: Flecainide 300mg PO (if no CAD), ibutilide 1mg IV over 10min (monitor QT)
  • Antiarrhythmics: Amiodarone (safest in structural disease), sotalol, dofetilide (in-hospital initiation)

Anticoagulation:

  • CHA‚ÇÇDS‚ÇÇ-VASc ‚â•2 (‚ôÇ) or ‚â•3 (‚ôÄ): Anticoagulate (DOACs preferred over warfarin)
  • DOACs: Apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban, dabigatran (avoid if mechanical valve or severe MS)
  • Post-cardioversion: Anticoagulate √ó 4 weeks minimum, longer if stroke risk factors
üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • WPW + AF = Emergency: Irregular wide-complex tachycardia. DO NOT use AV nodal blockers (can precipitate VF). Cardiovert or procainamide
  • Rate vs Rhythm: AFFIRM/RACE trials show no mortality difference. Most patients do fine with rate control alone
  • Anticoagulation paradox: HAS-BLED ‚â•3 means high bleeding risk, but often same patients who need anticoagulation most. Don't withhold if CHA‚ÇÇDS‚ÇÇ-VASc high
  • Holiday Heart: Alcohol-induced AF. Often converts spontaneously with abstinence
  • TEE before cardioversion: Even if on anticoagulation <3 weeks, consider TEE strategy to expedite cardioversion safely
‚ö†Ô∏è ESCALATE TO ICU IF:
  • Hemodynamic instability (SBP <90, shock)
  • Acute heart failure/pulmonary edema with RVR
  • Acute MI with RVR
  • Need for continuous IV rate control infusions
  • Post-cardioversion complications
  • RVR refractory to multiple agents
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • Rate controlled (HR <110 at rest, <120 with activity)
  • Hemodynamically stable ‚â•24 hours
  • Transitioned to oral rate control meds
  • Anticoagulation started (if indicated)
  • No signs of heart failure
  • Follow-up arranged (cardiology if new-onset)
  • If cardioverted: Stable rhythm ‚â•24hr
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

Acute: Stroke/TIA (most feared), hemodynamic compromise, heart failure, ACS (demand ischemia from RVR)

Chronic: Tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy (EF improves with rate/rhythm control), thromboembolism, bleeding (if anticoagulated)

4. HYPERTENSIVE EMERGENCY

End-Organ Damage ‚Ä¢ ICU Management
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Definition: Severe hypertension (typically SBP >180 or DBP >120) WITH acute end-organ damage

Classic: Severe headache, visual changes, chest pain, dyspnea, altered mental status, focal neurologic deficits

Alternate Presentations:

  • Neurologic: Hypertensive encephalopathy (HA, confusion, seizures), stroke (ischemic/hemorrhagic), PRES
  • Cardiac: Acute MI, acute pulmonary edema, aortic dissection
  • Renal: Acute kidney injury, hematuria, proteinuria
  • Microangiopathic: Hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia

Hypertensive Urgency: Severe HTN WITHOUT end-organ damage. Managed outpatient with PO meds

DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Labs: BMP, troponin, CBC, UA, peripheral smear if hemolysis suspected, BNP if APE concern

Imaging: ECG, CXR, Head CT if neuro symptoms, CT chest if dissection suspected

Fundoscopic: Look for papilledema, hemorrhages, exudates

Calculate Target BP: Initial reduction 10-20% in 1st hour, then 5-15% over next 23 hours. Avoid >25% reduction in first 24hr

TREATMENT PROTOCOL

General: ICU admission, continuous BP monitoring (arterial line often needed), IV antihypertensives

IV Agents:

  • Nicardipine: 5mg/hr, titrate by 2.5mg/hr q5-15min (max 15mg/hr). First-line
  • Labetalol: 20mg IV bolus, then 20-80mg q10min OR 0.5-2mg/min infusion
  • Clevidipine: 1-2mg/hr, double q90sec (max 21mg/hr). Short-acting
  • Esmolol: Œ≤-blocker for dissection. 500mcg/kg load, then 50-300mcg/kg/min
  • Nitroprusside: 0.25-10mcg/kg/min. For APE. Watch cyanide toxicity

Specific Scenarios:

  • Aortic Dissection: SBP <120 in 20min. Esmolol FIRST, then nicardipine
  • Ischemic Stroke: Permissive HTN! Allow SBP up to 220 (or 185 if tPA)
  • Hemorrhagic Stroke: Goal SBP 140-160
  • Eclampsia: Mag sulfate + hydralazine/labetalol. Deliver baby!
üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • Urgency vs Emergency: Only EMERGENCY needs ICU/IV meds. Urgency = oral meds over hours-days
  • Don't drop too fast: Cerebral autoregulation reset in chronic HTN. Rapid drop ‚Üí stroke/MI
  • Ischemic stroke exception: Brain needs perfusion! Only lower if SBP >220 or giving tPA
  • PRES: Headache, confusion, seizures, vision loss + posterior white matter edema. Usually reversible
  • Transition to PO: Start long-acting oral agents before stopping IV to prevent rebound
‚ö†Ô∏è ESCALATE TO ICU IF:
  • ALL hypertensive emergencies require ICU
  • Need for continuous IV infusion
  • Acute end-organ damage
  • Altered mental status
  • Arterial line needed
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • BP controlled on oral regimen ‚â•24hr
  • End-organ damage stabilized
  • Off IV medications
  • Patient educated on compliance
  • Close follow-up arranged (within 1 week)
  • Secondary causes evaluated if indicated
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

From Hypertension: Stroke, MI, aortic dissection, acute renal failure, retinopathy, heart failure

From Treatment: Hypotension, stroke from rapid BP drop, rebound HTN, cyanide toxicity (nitroprusside)

5. COMMUNITY-ACQUIRED PNEUMONIA (CAP)

Outpatient ‚Ä¢ Inpatient ‚Ä¢ ICU Management
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: Fever, cough (productive/dry), dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain. Exam: Tachypnea, rales/crackles, bronchial breath sounds, dullness, egophony

Alternate Presentations:

  • Elderly: Minimal fever, confusion/delirium, weakness, falls. May lack respiratory symptoms
  • Atypical Organisms: Dry cough, headache, myalgias, diarrhea (Legionella, Mycoplasma)
  • Aspiration: Witnessed event, right lower lobe/posterior segments, foul sputum
  • Immunocompromised: PCP, CMV, TB
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Imaging: CXR (PA and lateral) - infiltrate confirms diagnosis

Labs (if admitted): CBC, BMP, procalcitonin, blood cultures √ó 2, sputum Gram stain/culture, ABG if hypoxic

Special: Legionella urinary Ag, Strep pneumo urinary Ag (if severe), respiratory viral panel, HIV if risk factors

Severity Scores:

  • CURB-65: Confusion, Urea >20, RR ‚â•30, BP (SBP <90 or DBP ‚â§60), Age ‚â•65. Score 0-1: outpatient, 2: admit, ‚â•3: ICU
  • PSI/PORT: More complex. Classes I-II outpatient, III consider admit, IV-V admit
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Outpatient (CURB-65 0-1):

  • Previously healthy: Amoxicillin 1g TID √ó 5d OR doxycycline 100mg BID √ó 5d
  • With comorbidities: Amoxicillin-clavulanate + macrolide OR levofloxacin 750mg daily

Inpatient Non-ICU:

  • Standard: Ceftriaxone 1-2g IV daily + azithromycin 500mg IV/PO daily
  • Alternative: Levofloxacin 750mg daily or moxifloxacin 400mg daily
  • Duration: Minimum 5 days if afebrile √ó 48-72hr and stable

ICU (Severe CAP):

  • Standard: Ceftriaxone 2g IV daily + azithromycin 500mg IV daily
  • MRSA Risk: Add vancomycin or linezolid
  • Pseudomonas Risk: Pip-tazo or cefepime + fluoroquinolone

Supportive: O2 to SpO2 >90%, IV fluids, VTE prophylaxis

üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • Legionella clues: Hyponatremia, elevated LFTs, GI symptoms, relative bradycardia. Urinary antigen test. Need 10-14d abx
  • PCP Pneumonia: Subacute, dry cough, exertional dyspnea in HIV. Bilateral interstitial infiltrates. LDH >500. TMP-SMX + prednisone if PaO2 <70
  • Step-down criteria: HR <100, RR <24, SBP >90, SpO2 >90%, temp <37.8¬∞C, able to take PO
  • Macrolide resistance: S. pneumoniae resistance ~30-40%. Always pair with Œ≤-lactam for inpatients
  • Vaccines: PPSV23 and PCV20 after recovery per CDC schedule
‚ö†Ô∏è ESCALATE TO ICU IF:
  • Severe CAP (CURB-65 ‚â•3)
  • Need for mechanical ventilation
  • Septic shock requiring pressors
  • Multilobar infiltrates
  • PaO2/FiO2 <250
  • Confusion, AMS
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • Afebrile √ó 48-72 hours
  • Hemodynamically stable
  • O2 sat >90% on room air/baseline
  • Able to tolerate PO
  • Completed minimum 5 days antibiotics
  • Follow-up CXR arranged (6 weeks)
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

Early: Respiratory failure, sepsis/septic shock, parapneumonic effusion, empyema, lung abscess

Late: Organizing pneumonia, fibrosis, recurrent pneumonia (consider malignancy)

6. COPD EXACERBATION

AECOPD ‚Ä¢ Acute on Chronic
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: Increased dyspnea, cough, sputum production/purulence beyond baseline. Wheezing, accessory muscle use, pursed-lip breathing

Anthonisen Criteria: (1) Increased dyspnea, (2) Increased sputum volume, (3) Increased sputum purulence. Type I = all 3, Type II = 2 of 3, Type III = 1 symptom

Alternate Presentations:

  • Hypercapnic Encephalopathy: Confusion, asterixis, somnolence from CO2 retention
  • Right Heart Failure: Peripheral edema, JVD from cor pulmonale
  • Elderly: Fatigue, decreased functional status, falls
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Labs: ABG (assess hypercapnia, acidosis), CBC, BMP, BNP if HF concern, sputum culture if severe/recurrent

Imaging: CXR (r/o pneumonia, PTX, CHF), consider CT if recurrent or PE concern

ECG: Evaluate for MI, arrhythmia, right heart strain

Calculations: A-a gradient, FiO2 requirements

Triggers: Infection (most common), medication noncompliance, PE, pneumothorax, MI, pneumonia

TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Bronchodilators:

  • SABA: Albuterol 2.5-5mg nebulized q4h or continuous
  • SAMA: Ipratropium 0.5mg nebulized q6h OR combined albuterol-ipratropium (DuoNeb)
  • Note: Ipratropium added to albuterol shows additive benefit in COPD (unlike asthma)

Systemic Corticosteroids:

  • Dose: Prednisone 40mg PO daily √ó 5 days (or methylprednisolone 125mg IV if severe/unable PO)
  • Evidence: 5-7 days as effective as longer courses with fewer side effects

Antibiotics (if ‚â•2 cardinal symptoms or purulent sputum):

  • First-line: Azithromycin 500mg daily √ó 5d OR doxycycline 100mg BID √ó 5-7d
  • Second-line: Amoxicillin-clavulanate, respiratory fluoroquinolone, cephalosporin
  • Pseudomonas risk: (FEV1 <50%, recent hospitalization, >4 exacerbations/year) ‚Üí fluoroquinolone or cephalosporin

Oxygen Therapy: Goal SpO2 88-92% (avoid higher - can worsen hypercapnia). Titrate carefully in CO2 retainers

Ventilatory Support:

  • NIPPV (BiPAP): First-line for hypercapnic respiratory failure (pH 7.25-7.35). Reduces intubation rate and mortality
  • Intubation: If unable to tolerate NIPPV, worsening acidosis (pH <7.25), decreased mental status
üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • O2-induced hypercapnia: High O2 in COPD ‚Üí worsens V/Q mismatch and Haldane effect ‚Üí CO2 retention. Keep SpO2 88-92%
  • BiPAP is magic: NIPPV reduces intubation by 65%, mortality by 55% in AECOPD with respiratory acidosis. Use early!
  • Steroid duration: REDUCE trial showed 5 days = 14 days for outcomes. Don't give longer courses
  • Antibiotics debate: Only benefit if purulent sputum or ‚â•2 cardinal symptoms. Type III (1 symptom) may not need abx
  • Home oxygen criteria: Resting PaO2 <55 or SpO2 <88% OR PaO2 56-59 with cor pulmonale/polycythemia
  • Prevention: Vaccinations (flu, pneumococcal), smoking cessation, maintenance inhalers, pulmonary rehab
‚ö†Ô∏è ESCALATE TO ICU IF:
  • Severe respiratory acidosis (pH <7.30)
  • Need for intubation/mechanical ventilation
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Altered mental status despite treatment
  • Failed NIPPV trial on floor
  • Life-threatening comorbidity (MI, sepsis)
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • Return to baseline dyspnea/function
  • Tolerating SABA q4h or less
  • SpO2 >90% on room air or baseline O2
  • Able to sleep and walk without severe dyspnea
  • pH >7.35 if acidotic on admission
  • On maintenance inhalers (LABA/LAMA combo)
  • Inhaler technique verified
  • Pulmonary follow-up within 1 month
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

Acute: Respiratory failure requiring intubation, pneumothorax (bullous disease), pneumonia, arrhythmias, myocardial ischemia

Chronic: Cor pulmonale, progressive lung function decline, long-term oxygen need, recurrent exacerbations

7. ASTHMA EXACERBATION

Acute Bronchospasm ‚Ä¢ Status Asthmaticus
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: Dyspnea, wheezing, chest tightness, cough, accessory muscle use, prolonged expiration

Alternate Presentations:

  • Silent Chest: Severe obstruction with minimal air movement - ominous sign!
  • Cough-Variant Asthma: Persistent cough without wheezing
  • Exercise-Induced: Symptoms during/after exercise
  • Children: May present with only cough or difficulty feeding
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Immediate: Peak flow, O2 saturation, vital signs

If Severe: CXR (r/o pneumothorax, infiltrate), ABG if critically ill, VBG acceptable

Calculations: Peak flow % predicted (compare to personal best), FEV1 if available

Severity Assessment:

  • Mild: Speaks full sentences, HR <100, PEF >70%
  • Moderate: Speaks phrases, HR 100-120, PEF 40-70%
  • Severe: Speaks words, HR >120, PEF <40%, accessory muscles
  • Life-threatening: Unable to speak, altered mental status, silent chest, bradycardia, hypotension
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Bronchodilators:

  • Albuterol: 2.5-5mg nebulized q20min √ó 3, then reassess. Consider continuous nebulization if severe

  • Methylprednisolone: 125mg IV if unable to take PO or severe

  • Terbutaline: 0.25mg subQ (use with caution - cardiac side effects)
  • Heliox: May reduce work of breathing (helium-oxygen mixture)

  • Normal PCO2 is concerning: Asthma patients should be hypocapnic (from hyperventilation). Normal/high CO2 = respiratory fatigue

  • Altered mental status, confusion, lethargy
  • Silent chest
  • PCO2 >45 (respiratory fatigue)
  • Need for continuous nebulization
  • Hemodynamic instability
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA

  • Prescribed albuterol + oral steroids √ó 5 days

8. PULMONARY EMBOLISM (PE)

CLINICAL PRESENTATION

  • Massive PE: Hypotension, syncope, cardiac arrest (obstructive shock)

  • Small PE: May be asymptomatic or minimal symptoms
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Calculate Pre-test Probability: Wells Score or PERC Rule (see calculators below)

Labs: D-dimer (if low probability - high sensitivity, low specificity), troponin, BNP (assess RV strain), ABG (may show hypoxemia, A-a gradient)

  • CTPA (CT Pulmonary Angiography): Gold standard, shows filling defects
  • V/Q Scan: If contrast contraindicated
  • Lower Extremity Doppler US: Look for DVT source
  • Echo: Assess RV function, strain (McConnell's sign = RV dysfunction with apical sparing)

  • Respiratory failure
  • Cardiac arrest

  • On therapeutic anticoagulation
  • Safe for outpatient management (PESI score)

Labs: Blood cultures √ó 2 (different sites, before antibiotics), CBC, CMP, lactate (repeat q2-4h), procalcitonin, coagulation studies

  • Time is tissue: Every hour delay in antibiotics increases mortality by 7.6%. Door-to-antibiotic <1 hour!

  • Oliguria/anuria despite fluids
  • Multiorgan dysfunction
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • Afebrile √ó 24-48 hours

10. ACUTE KIDNEY INJURY (AKI)

Prerenal ‚Ä¢ Intrinsic ‚Ä¢ Postrenal
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Definition: Increase in Cr ‚â•0.3 mg/dL within 48hr OR increase to ‚â•1.5√ó baseline OR urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hr for 6hr

Classic: Oliguria, fluid overload, edema, uremic symptoms (N/V, confusion, asterixis, pericarditis)

Alternate Presentations:

  • Non-oliguric AKI: Cr rising but urine output preserved (often ATN from nephrotoxins)
  • Hyperkalemia: May be presenting sign, can be life-threatening
  • Metabolic Acidosis: High anion gap from uremia
  • Elderly: Minimal symptoms despite significant Cr elevation
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Labs: BMP (Cr, BUN, K+), baseline Cr for comparison, UA with microscopy, urine electrolytes (Na+, Cr, osmolality)

Imaging: Renal US (assess for obstruction, kidney size, hydronephrosis)

Consider: Renal biopsy if diagnosis unclear or concern for glomerulonephritis

Categorize AKI:

  • Prerenal (60-70%): Decreased renal perfusion - volume depletion, heart failure, cirrhosis, NSAIDs
  • Intrinsic (25-40%): Acute tubular necrosis (ischemia, nephrotoxins), acute interstitial nephritis, glomerulonephritis
  • Postrenal (5-10%): Obstruction - BPH, kidney stones, malignancy, neurogenic bladder

Use: Differentiate prerenal AKI (<1%) from ATN (>2%). FENa 1-2% is indeterminate. If on diuretics, use FEUrea instead.
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

General Principles:

  • Treat underlying cause
  • Stop nephrotoxins (NSAIDs, ACE-I/ARB if AKI severe, aminoglycosides, contrast)
  • Adjust all medication doses for GFR
  • Avoid further insults (hypotension, contrast, nephrotoxic drugs)

Prerenal AKI:

  • Volume Depletion: IV fluids (LR or NS), correct hypotension
  • Cardiorenal: Diuresis if volume overloaded, optimize cardiac output
  • Hepatorenal: Albumin + midodrine + octreotide, or terlipressin

Intrinsic AKI:

  • ATN: Supportive care, avoid nephrotoxins, optimize hemodynamics. Usually resolves in 1-3 weeks
  • AIN (Acute Interstitial Nephritis): Stop offending drug, consider steroids if severe/not improving
  • Glomerulonephritis: Nephrology consult, may need steroids/immunosuppression ¬± plasmapheresis

Postrenal AKI:

  • Relieve Obstruction: Foley catheter (if bladder outlet obstruction), nephrostomy tubes, ureteral stents
  • Watch for post-obstructive diuresis - may need aggressive fluid replacement

Indications for Dialysis (AEIOU):

  • Acidosis - severe metabolic acidosis (pH <7.1) refractory to medical management
  • Electrolytes - hyperkalemia refractory to medical therapy
  • Ingestions - toxic alcohols, lithium, salicylates
  • Overload - volume overload refractory to diuretics, pulmonary edema
  • Uremia - uremic pericarditis, encephalopathy, bleeding
üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • FENa limitations: Not valid if on diuretics (falsely elevated), chronic kidney disease, or contrast nephropathy. Use FEUrea if on diuretics (prerenal <35%)
  • BUN:Cr ratio: >20:1 suggests prerenal (BUN reabsorbed more than Cr). <15:1 suggests intrinsic renal disease
  • Muddy brown casts: Pathognomonic for ATN on urine microscopy
  • Triple whammy: ACE-I/ARB + diuretic + NSAID = high risk for AKI. Avoid this combination!
  • Contrast-induced AKI: Peak Cr at 3-5 days. Prevent with IV fluids before/after contrast. No evidence for NAC or bicarb
  • Post-obstructive diuresis: Can lose massive amounts of fluid after obstruction relieved. Replace half of UOP with 0.45% NS

  • Hyperkalemia >6.5 with ECG changes
  • Severe metabolic acidosis (pH <7.2)
  • Volume overload with respiratory failure
  • Uremic encephalopathy/pericarditis
  • Need for emergent dialysis
  • Hemodynamic instability
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • Cr downtrending or stable
  • No dialysis-requiring complications
  • Electrolytes controlled (K+ <5.5)
  • Volume status optimized
  • Cause identified and addressed
  • Nephrotoxins stopped
  • Close nephrology follow-up if Cr not at baseline
  • Patient educated on medication adjustments
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

Acute: Hyperkalemia, metabolic acidosis, volume overload, uremia, need for dialysis, death

Chronic: Chronic kidney disease, progression to ESRD, recurrent AKI episodes

11. HYPERKALEMIA

K+ >5.5 mEq/L ‚Ä¢ Life-Threatening Arrhythmias
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: Often asymptomatic until severe. Weakness, paresthesias, palpitations

ECG Changes (Progressive):

  • Mild (5.5-6.5): Peaked T waves (tall, narrow, symmetric)
  • Moderate (6.5-8): PR prolongation, P wave flattening/loss, QRS widening
  • Severe (>8): Sine wave pattern, ventricular fibrillation, asystole

Alternate Presentations:

  • Pseudohyperkalemia: Hemolysis during blood draw, severe leukocytosis/thrombocytosis, fist clenching
  • Acute on Chronic: Patients on ACE-I/ARB with CKD who take NSAIDs or get dehydrated
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Labs: Repeat K+ to confirm (rule out pseudohyperkalemia), BMP, Mg, Ca, VBG, consider digoxin level if on digoxin

ECG: CRITICAL - assess for cardiotoxicity. ECG changes indicate true emergency

Identify Cause:

  • Increased Intake: K+ supplements, salt substitutes, IV K+
  • Transcellular Shift: Acidosis, insulin deficiency, Œ≤-blocker, digoxin toxicity, succinylcholine, tumor lysis
  • Decreased Excretion: AKI/CKD, hypoaldosteronism, ACE-I/ARB, K+-sparing diuretics, NSAIDs, heparin, TMP-SMX
TREATMENT PROTOCOL (Sequence Matters!)

Step 1: Cardiac Membrane Stabilization (if ECG changes or K >6.5):

  • Calcium Gluconate: 1-2 amps (10-20 mL of 10% solution) IV over 2-3 min
  • OR Calcium Chloride: 0.5-1g (5-10 mL of 10%) IV via central line (more potent, sclerosing)
  • Effect: Immediate (1-3 min), duration 30-60 min
  • Does NOT lower K+, only protects heart
  • Can repeat dose if persistent ECG changes

Step 2: Shift K+ Intracellularly (Temporary):

  • Insulin + Dextrose: Regular insulin 10 units IV + D50 50mL (25g dextrose) IV push
    • If glucose >250: give insulin alone, no dextrose
    • Effect: 15-30 min, duration 4-6 hours
    • Lowers K+ by 0.5-1.5 mEq/L
    • Check glucose in 1hr (risk hypoglycemia)
  • Albuterol: 10-20mg nebulized (or 0.5mg IV)
    • Effect: 30 min, duration 2-4 hours
    • Lowers K+ by 0.5-1 mEq/L
    • Additive with insulin
  • Sodium Bicarbonate: 50-100 mEq (1-2 amps) IV over 5 min (only if concurrent metabolic acidosis)
    • Controversial efficacy if pH normal

Step 3: Remove K+ from Body (Definitive):

  • Loop Diuretic: Furosemide 40-80mg IV (if adequate renal function)
  • Sodium Polystyrene Sulfonate (Kayexalate): 15-30g PO/PR
    • Effect: Hours, lowers K+ by 0.5-1 mEq/L
    • Avoid if ileus (risk colonic necrosis)
  • Patiromer or Sodium Zirconium: Newer K+ binders, better tolerated but slower onset
  • Hemodialysis: Most effective. Indications:
    • K+ >6.5 with ECG changes refractory to medical management
    • Severe AKI/ESRD
    • Ongoing tissue breakdown (rhabdo, tumor lysis)

Step 4: Stop Exacerbating Factors: Hold ACE-I/ARB, K+-sparing diuretics, NSAIDs, K+ supplements

üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • ECG trumps K+ level: If ECG shows changes, treat aggressively regardless of exact K+ value. ECG changes = cardiac emergency!
  • Calcium first! If ECG changes, give calcium before anything else. Protects heart immediately while you arrange other therapies
  • Insulin hypoglycemia: Very common! Check glucose 30min and 1hr after insulin. Have D50 ready. Consider dextrose infusion
  • Kayexalate controversy: Limited evidence for efficacy, risk of colonic necrosis esp with sorbitol. Consider newer agents
  • Repeat K+ frequently: Check K+ q2-4h during acute management. Temporizing measures wear off!
  • Chronic management: Low K+ diet, loop diuretics, K+ binders. Avoid "hyperkalemia cocktail" (ACE-I + spironolactone + NSAIDs)
‚ö†Ô∏è ESCALATE TO ICU IF:
  • K+ >6.5 with ECG changes
  • Hemodynamically significant arrhythmias
  • Need for emergent dialysis
  • Rapidly rising K+ despite treatment
  • Cardiac arrest from hyperkalemia
  • Requires continuous cardiac monitoring
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • K+ <5.5 mEq/L and downtrending
  • ECG normalized (no peaked T waves)
  • Cause identified and addressed
  • K+-raising medications stopped/adjusted
  • Patient educated on low K+ diet
  • Close follow-up with repeat K+ within 2-3 days
  • If CKD: nephrology follow-up arranged
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

Cardiac: Ventricular arrhythmias (VT, VF), heart block, asystole, sudden cardiac death

From Treatment: Hypoglycemia (insulin), hypokalemia rebound, volume overload (from IVF), GI complications (kayexalate)

12. HYPONATREMIA

CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: Nausea, headache, confusion, lethargy, seizures (if severe/acute)

Severity by Symptoms:

  • Mild (Na 130-135): Often asymptomatic or mild nausea
  • Moderate (125-129): Nausea, confusion, headache
  • Severe (<125): Vomiting, seizures, coma, respiratory arrest

Alternate Presentations:

  • Elderly: Falls, gait instability, cognitive impairment
  • Athletes: Exercise-associated hyponatremia from excessive free water intake
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Step 1 - Check Serum Osmolality:

  • Isotonic (280-295): Pseudohyponatremia (hyperglycemia, hyperlipidemia, hyperproteinemia)
  • Hypertonic (>295): Hyperglycemia, mannitol
  • Hypotonic (<280): True hyponatremia - proceed to step 2

Step 2 - Assess Volume Status:

  • Hypovolemic: Dry mucous membranes, ‚Üìskin turgor, orthostatic hypotension
    • Urine Na <20: GI losses, third-spacing, skin losses
    • Urine Na >40: Diuretics, salt-wasting nephropathy, adrenal insufficiency
  • Euvolemic: Normal volume status
    • SIADH (most common), hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency, psychogenic polydipsia
  • Hypervolemic: Edema, elevated JVP, ascites
    • CHF, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome, advanced CKD

TREATMENT PROTOCOL

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL: Correct slowly! Maximum 8-10 mEq/L in first 24 hours. Faster correction ‚Üí osmotic demyelination syndrome

Acute Symptomatic (<48hr onset with seizures/coma):

  • 3% Hypertonic Saline: 100 mL IV bolus over 10 min, can repeat √ó 2 if needed
  • Goal: Raise Na by 4-6 mEq/L (enough to stop seizures), then slow correction
  • Check Na q2-4h

Chronic or Asymptomatic:

  • Hypovolemic: NS at 0.5-1 mL/kg/hr. Na will auto-correct with volume repletion
  • Euvolemic (SIADH):
    • Fluid restriction (800-1000 mL/day)
    • Treat underlying cause
    • Salt tabs + loop diuretic if needed
    • Tolvaptan (vasopressin antagonist) for refractory cases
  • Hypervolemic: Fluid + sodium restriction, diuretics, treat underlying (CHF, cirrhosis)

Correction Rate Calculation:

  • Target: 6-8 mEq/L in 24 hours, 12-14 mEq/L in 48 hours
  • High-risk patients (chronic, malnourished, cirrhosis, K+ <3): Limit to 6 mEq/L in 24hr
üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • Osmotic demyelination (ODS): From overly rapid correction. Presents 2-6 days later with dysarthria, dysphagia, quadriplegia, locked-in syndrome. PREVENT by slow correction!
  • SIADH criteria: Hyponatremia + low serum osm (<280) + inappropriately concentrated urine (>100) + euvolemic + normal thyroid/adrenal
  • Beer potomania: Hyponatremia from excessive beer intake with poor solute intake. Na can rise dangerously fast when solute repleted!
  • Tea and toast diet: Similar to beer potomania - inadequate solute intake in elderly
  • Exercise-associated: From drinking too much water during endurance events. Treat with hypertonic saline, NOT more water!
  • Hyperglycemia correction: For every 100 mg/dL glucose >100, add 1.6 mEq/L to measured Na to get "true" Na

  • Na <120 mEq/L
  • Acute symptomatic hyponatremia
  • Need for 3% hypertonic saline
  • Rapid changes requiring q2h monitoring
  • Respiratory compromise
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • Na >125 mEq/L and stable/improving
  • Asymptomatic or symptoms resolved
  • Underlying cause identified/treated
  • Can comply with fluid restriction if needed
  • Follow-up arranged with Na check in 2-3 days
  • Patient educated on risks

13. DIABETIC KETOACIDOSIS (DKA)

Hyperglycemia ‚Ä¢ Acidosis ‚Ä¢ Ketones
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic Triad: (1) Hyperglycemia (>250), (2) Anion gap metabolic acidosis, (3) Ketones

Classic Symptoms: Polyuria, polydipsia, N/V, abdominal pain, Kussmaul respirations (deep, rapid), fruity breath odor, altered mental status

Precipitants (5 I's):

  • Infection: Most common (UTI, pneumonia, etc)
  • Insulin: Non-compliance or inadequate dosing
  • Ischemia: MI, stroke
  • Intoxication: Alcohol, drugs
  • Infant: Pregnancy
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

DKA Criteria:

  • Glucose >250 mg/dL (or known diabetic)
  • pH <7.3 (arterial) or HCO3 <18
  • Anion gap >10
  • Positive serum/urine ketones

Labs: VBG (pH, HCO3), BMP, anion gap, Œ≤-hydroxybutyrate (preferred over urine ketones), phosphate, Mg, CBC, blood cultures, UA/CXR if infection suspected

Calculate: Anion gap = Na - (Cl + HCO3). Normal 8-12. Also calculate osmolality if altered mental status

Severity:

  • Mild: pH 7.25-7.30, HCO3 15-18
  • Moderate: pH 7.0-7.24, HCO3 10-14
  • Severe: pH <7.0, HCO3 <10
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

1. Fluids (MOST IMPORTANT):

  • Initial: NS 1-1.5 L over first hour (15-20 mL/kg/hr)
  • Subsequent:
    • If Na normal/high: 0.45% NS at 250-500 mL/hr
    • If Na low: NS at 250-500 mL/hr
  • Once glucose <250: Switch to D5 0.45% NS to prevent hypoglycemia while continuing insulin
  • Total deficit usually 5-10 L - replace over 24-48 hours

2. Insulin:

  • Regular insulin IV infusion: 0.1 units/kg/hr (usually 5-10 units/hr)
  • Optional bolus: 0.1 units/kg IV if severe DKA
  • Goal: Decrease glucose by 50-75 mg/dL/hr
  • Do NOT stop insulin when glucose reaches 250! Switch to D5-containing fluids and continue insulin until anion gap closes
  • Once anion gap closed: Transition to subQ insulin, overlap by 1-2 hours before stopping gtt

3. Potassium Repletion (CRITICAL):

  • If K+ <3.3: Hold insulin, give K+ 20-30 mEq/hr until K >3.3
  • If K+ 3.3-5.2: Add 20-30 mEq K+ to each liter of fluids
  • If K+ >5.2: No K+ initially, check q2-4h (will drop with insulin)
  • Goal: K+ 4-5 mEq/L

4. Phosphate (if <1.0 mg/dL): K-phos 20-30 mEq/L in fluids

5. Bicarbonate (controversial, only if pH <6.9): 100 mEq in 400 mL H2O + 20 mEq KCl over 2 hours

6. Treat Precipitant: Antibiotics if infection, adjust insulin regimen, etc

üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • DKA resolution: Glucose <200 AND anion gap <12 AND HCO3 >18 AND pH >7.3. Don't stop insulin just because glucose normalized!
  • Two-bag system: Run two IV bags (one NS, one D5-NS) through Y-connector. Adjust ratio to maintain glucose 150-200 while continuing insulin
  • Euglycemic DKA: Can occur with SGLT2 inhibitors. Check ketones even if glucose <250!
  • K+ shifts: Total body K+ depleted despite normal/high initial K+. With insulin, K+ shifts into cells ‚Üí life-threatening hypokalemia. Monitor closely!
  • Cerebral edema: Rare but fatal. More common in kids. From too-rapid correction. Symptoms: Headache, AMS, bradycardia. Give mannitol/3% saline

14. HYPEROSMOLAR HYPERGLYCEMIC STATE (HHS)

CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Key Differences from DKA: More extreme hyperglycemia (>600), higher osmolality (>320), NO significant ketoacidosis, more profound dehydration

Classic: Altered mental status, severe dehydration, hypotension. Often insidious onset over days-weeks

Typical Patient: Elderly Type 2 diabetic with infection or medication non-compliance

DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

HHS Criteria:

  • Glucose >600 mg/dL
  • Effective serum osmolality >320 mOsm/kg
  • pH >7.30
  • HCO3 >15
  • Minimal ketones (can have trace)

Calculate Osmolality: 2(Na) + glucose/18 + BUN/2.8. Normal 280-295

Labs: Same as DKA - VBG, BMP, identify precipitant

TREATMENT PROTOCOL

1. Aggressive Fluid Resuscitation (MORE than DKA):

  • Initial: NS 1-1.5 L first hour
  • Subsequent: 250-500 mL/hr based on volume status
  • Total deficit often 8-12 L (more than DKA)
  • Goal: Replace half of deficit in first 12-24 hours

2. Insulin (LOWER rates than DKA):

  • Wait 1-2 hours, give fluids first to avoid vascular collapse
  • Then start insulin 0.05-0.1 units/kg/hr (lower than DKA)
  • Goal: Decrease glucose by 50-75 mg/dL/hr
  • Once glucose <300: Add dextrose to fluids, continue insulin

3. Potassium: Same principles as DKA

4. Correct Slowly: Osmolality should decrease by <3 mOsm/kg/hr to avoid cerebral edema

  • HHS vs DKA: HHS = Type 2 DM, more dehydration, higher glucose (>600), no ketosis. DKA = Type 1, ketoacidosis
  • Why no ketosis? Type 2 patients have enough insulin to prevent lipolysis but not enough to control glucose
  • Mortality higher: HHS mortality 10-20% (vs 1-5% for DKA) - sicker, older patients
  • Fluids first! Give 1-2 L NS before starting insulin to prevent cardiovascular collapse
  • Slower correction: Osmolality built up over longer time, correct more slowly to avoid cerebral edema

  • Osmolality >350
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Need for insulin infusion
  • Severe electrolyte abnormalities
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • Glucose <250 and stable
  • Mental status at baseline
  • Osmolality <310
  • Taking PO
  • On appropriate diabetes regimen
  • Precipitant treated

16. CIRRHOSIS WITH ASCITES

Classic: Abdominal distension, weight gain, peripheral edema, early satiety, dyspnea from diaphragm elevation

Alternate Presentations:

  • SBP: Fever, abdominal pain/tenderness, altered mental status without obvious peritonitis
  • Hepatic Hydrothorax: Right-sided pleural effusion (usually) without cardiopulmonary disease
  • Tense Ascites: Respiratory compromise, severe discomfort, umbilical/inguinal hernias
  • Hepatorenal Syndrome: Progressive AKI despite volume resuscitation
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Imaging: Ultrasound (detects >100mL ascites, evaluates liver texture, portal vein), CT abdomen if unclear etiology or concern for HCC

Diagnostic Paracentesis (ALWAYS on first presentation OR any clinical deterioration):

  • Cell count with differential: PMN >250 = SBP
  • Albumin: For SAAG calculation
  • Total protein: <1g/dL = high SBP risk
  • Gram stain and culture: Inoculate blood culture bottles at bedside (improves yield)
  • Consider: Glucose, LDH, cytology (if malignancy suspected), TB studies, amylase (if pancreatitis concern)

SAAG (Serum-Ascites Albumin Gradient):

  • Calculate: Serum albumin - Ascitic fluid albumin
  • ‚â•1.1 g/dL: Portal hypertension (97% accuracy) - cirrhosis, cardiac ascites, Budd-Chiari syndrome, sinusoidal obstruction syndrome
  • <1.1 g/dL: Non-portal causes - peritoneal carcinomatosis, TB peritonitis, pancreatic ascites, nephrotic syndrome, serositis
  • SAAG unaffected by diuretics or albumin infusions

Labs: CBC, CMP, albumin, PT/INR, hepatitis panel (HBsAg, anti-HCV), AFP (HCC screening), lipid panel

TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Sodium Restriction: <2g/day (88 mEq/day). This is THE most important intervention! Effective in 10-20% as monotherapy

Diuretic Therapy:

  • Initial regimen: Spironolactone 100mg PO daily + Furosemide 40mg PO daily (100:40 ratio)
  • Titration: Increase both medications together every 3-5 days maintaining the 100:40 ratio
    • Step 2: Spironolactone 200mg + Furosemide 80mg
    • Step 3: Spironolactone 300mg + Furosemide 120mg
    • Maximum: Spironolactone 400mg + Furosemide 160mg
  • Goal weight loss: 0.5kg/day if ascites alone, 1kg/day if peripheral edema present
  • Monitoring: Daily weights, BMP at least 2√ó/week initially (watch Na, K, Cr)
  • Hold diuretics if: Na <120, K >6, Cr rise >2mg/dL above baseline, encephalopathy worsens, severe hypovolemia

Large Volume Paracentesis (LVP):

  • Indications: Tense/refractory ascites, respiratory compromise, severe discomfort, new-onset ascites for diagnosis
  • Safe to remove 10-12 liters in single session
  • Albumin replacement: 6-8g IV per liter removed if >5 liters removed (prevents post-paracentesis circulatory dysfunction)
  • Can use 20% or 25% albumin (20% is more cost-effective)
  • Complications rare: bleeding <1%, infection <1%, bowel perforation <0.1%

Refractory Ascites:

  • Definition: Unresponsive to maximum diuretics + sodium restriction OR development of diuretic-induced complications
  • Management options:
    • Serial large volume paracentesis q2-4 weeks + albumin
    • TIPS (transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt) - if appropriate candidate (MELD <18, no encephalopathy)
    • Liver transplant evaluation

Fluid Restriction: Generally NOT needed unless Na <120-125 mEq/L

üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • 100:40 ratio maintains normokalemia: Spironolactone (K-sparing) + furosemide (K-wasting) balance each other
  • SAAG is gospel: ‚â•1.1 = portal hypertension with 97% accuracy. Unaffected by diuretics, paracentesis, or albumin
  • Paracentesis is safe: Can tap with INR 2-3, platelets >20k. Prophylactic FFP/platelets NOT needed. Bleeding risk <1%
  • SBP prophylaxis indications: Primary (never had SBP): ascitic protein <1.5 g/dL + Child-Pugh B/C OR GI bleeding. Secondary (prior SBP): lifelong
  • Medications to AVOID: NSAIDs (precipitate HRS, worsen ascites), ACE-I/ARBs (hypotension, HRS), aminoglycosides (nephrotoxic)
  • TIPS considerations: Reduces ascites and variceal bleeding but increases hepatic encephalopathy. Best for refractory ascites with MELD <18
  • Hepatorenal syndrome triggers: SBP, GI bleeding, large volume paracentesis without albumin, overdiuresis
‚ö†Ô∏è ESCALATE TO ICU IF:
  • Hemodynamic instability/shock
  • Respiratory failure from tense ascites
  • HRS type 1 (rapidly progressive AKI)
  • Grade 3-4 hepatic encephalopathy
  • Variceal bleeding
  • Severe SBP with sepsis
  • Need for mechanical ventilation
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • Ascites improved/controlled on oral diuretics
  • No evidence of SBP (if tapped)
  • Renal function stable (Cr not rising)
  • Electrolytes acceptable (Na >125, K 3.5-5.5)
  • Encephalopathy grade 0-1
  • Patient/family educated on 2g sodium diet
  • Daily weight monitoring arranged
  • Hepatology follow-up within 1-2 weeks
  • Transplant evaluation if MELD ‚â•15

From Disease: SBP, hepatorenal syndrome, hepatic hydrothorax, umbilical/inguinal hernia rupture, respiratory compromise, hepatic encephalopathy

  • Day 3: 1.0 g/kg IV

    • Norfloxacin 400mg PO daily (first choice) OR
    • Ciprofloxacin 750mg PO weekly OR

18. HEPATIC ENCEPHALOPATHY

Altered Mental Status in Cirrhosis
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

West Haven Grading:

  • Grade 0 (Minimal): Normal mental status, subtle psychometric abnormalities
  • Grade 1: Trivial confusion, euphoria/anxiety, shortened attention span, impaired addition/subtraction
  • Grade 2: Lethargy, disorientation to time, obvious personality change, inappropriate behavior, asterixis
  • Grade 3: Somnolent but arousable, gross disorientation, bizarre behavior
  • Grade 4: Coma, unresponsive to painful stimuli

Physical Exam Findings:

  • Asterixis: "Flapping tremor" - extend arms with dorsiflexed wrists, observe for irregular flapping. Present in grades 2-3
  • Hyperreflexia: Increased deep tendon reflexes
  • Fetor hepaticus: Sweet, musty breath odor
  • Stigmata of cirrhosis: Jaundice, spider angiomas, palmar erythema, ascites

Alternate Presentations:

  • Acute Change: Sudden worsening in previously stable patient suggests precipitant
  • Post-TIPS: Can develop weeks after TIPS procedure
  • Subclinical: Patient/family report subtle changes not apparent to clinicians
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Diagnosis of Exclusion: Rule out other causes of altered mental status first!

Essential Studies:

  • Labs: CBC, CMP, ammonia level (correlates poorly but >200 suggests HE), venous pH/lactate
  • Infection workup: UA, CXR, diagnostic paracentesis if ascites present (rule out SBP!)
  • Toxicology screen: Especially in acute change
  • Head CT: If focal neurologic signs, trauma, first episode, or atypical presentation

Identify Precipitants (The "5 I's" + more):

  • Infection: SBP (most common), UTI, pneumonia, any infection
  • GI bleed: Protein load from blood
  • Constipation: Increased colonic transit time ‚Üí more ammonia absorption
  • Medications: Benzos, opioids, diuretic-induced hypokalemia/alkalosis
  • Dehydration/Azotemia: From overdiuresis
  • Electrolyte abnormalities: Hyponatremia, hypokalemia
  • TIPS: Increased porto-systemic shunting
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma: Tumor burden
  • Dietary indiscretion: Large protein meal
  • Non-compliance: Stopping lactulose
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

1. Identify and Treat Precipitant (MOST IMPORTANT!):

  • Tap ascites (rule out SBP)
  • Check for GI bleeding
  • Treat infections
  • Review medication list - stop sedatives/opioids
  • Correct electrolyte abnormalities

2. Lactulose (First-line Therapy):

  • Initial dose: 30mL (20g) PO q1-2 hours until first bowel movement
  • Maintenance: 30mL PO TID, titrate to 2-3 soft bowel movements per day
  • Goal: Acidify colon, decrease ammonia production/absorption
  • If cannot take PO: 300mL lactulose in 700mL water as retention enema, hold 30-60 min
  • Watch for: Dehydration, hypernatremia (diarrhea), cramping

3. Rifaximin (Add-on Therapy for Recurrent/Chronic HE):

  • Dose: 550mg PO BID
  • Mechanism: Non-absorbable antibiotic, reduces ammonia-producing gut bacteria
  • Evidence: Reduces HE recurrence by 58% when added to lactulose
  • Cost: Expensive (~$2000/month), often requires prior auth
  • Use for patients with ‚â•2 HE episodes requiring hospitalization

4. Zinc Supplementation:

  • Dose: Zinc sulfate 220mg PO daily
  • Many cirrhotics are zinc deficient
  • Zinc is cofactor for urea cycle enzymes

5. Supportive Care:

  • Airway protection if grade 3-4 (consider intubation)
  • Avoid sedatives (worsen encephalopathy)
  • If agitated: low-dose haloperidol 0.5-2mg (avoid benzos)
  • Nutrition: Don't restrict protein! Give 1.2-1.5 g/kg/day. Protein restriction is OUTDATED

What NOT to Do:

  • Do NOT restrict protein: Outdated practice, worsens malnutrition
  • Do NOT use neomycin: Nephrotoxic, ototoxic. Rifaximin is better
  • Ammonia level: Don't follow serial levels - correlates poorly with clinical status
üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • Always find the precipitant: HE rarely occurs without a trigger. Treat the cause, not just the symptom
  • Ammonia level controversy: Poor correlation with severity. Level >200 supports diagnosis but normal level doesn't exclude HE
  • Protein restriction is WRONG: Old dogma. Cirrhotics need ADEQUATE protein (1.2-1.5g/kg/day) to prevent sarcopenia
  • Vegetable protein better than animal: May be better tolerated but don't eliminate meat entirely
  • Lactulose goals: 2-3 soft BMs/day. Too much = dehydration. Too little = constipation worsens HE
  • Rifaximin not for acute: Add only for chronic/recurrent HE. Takes days to work. Not for initial episodes
  • Post-TIPS HE: Occurs in 30-50%. May need TIPS revision or consider transplant if refractory
  • Asterixis is NOT specific: Also seen in uremia, hypercapnia, other metabolic encephalopathies

  • Inability to protect airway
  • Rapidly progressive deterioration
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Severe concurrent illness (sepsis, GI bleed)
  • Need for mechanical ventilation

  • Precipitant identified and treated
  • Established bowel regimen (2-3 BM/day)

  • Transplant evaluation if not listed

19. ACUTE PANCREATITIS

  • Gallstones (40%)
  • Ethanol (30%)
  • Trauma
  • Steroids
  • Mumps/other infections
  • Autoimmune (IgG4)
  • Scorpion/snake bites

  • 2. Serum lipase >3√ó upper limit normal (more specific than amylase)
  • 3. Imaging findings

  • CT abdomen with IV contrast: If diagnosis unclear OR severe disease. Delay 48-72hr if possible (pancreatic necrosis takes time to develop)
  • MRCP: If concern for choledocholithiasis

  • BISAP Score: Simpler. 1 point each for: BUN >25, Impaired mental status, SIRS, Age >60, Pleural effusion. Score ‚â•3 = severe
  • APACHE-II: Complex but accurate

  • Aggressive IVF: Lactated Ringer's preferred over NS (reduces SIRS, mortality)
  • Rate: 250-500 mL/hr initially (15-20 mL/kg bolus, then 250-500 mL/hr)
  • Goal: UOP >0.5 mL/kg/hr, HR <120, MAP 65-85
  • Caution: Watch for volume overload in elderly/cardiac disease
  • Early aggressive hydration (within 24hr) reduces organ failure and mortality

Nutrition:

  • Early oral feeding: Start within 24 hours if tolerated (even in severe pancreatitis!)
  • Diet: Low-fat solid diet as tolerated. No need for clear liquids first
  • If cannot tolerate PO: Enteral nutrition via NG/NJ tube preferred over TPN
  • NPO only if: Intractable vomiting, ileus, or altered mental status

  • IV opioids PRN (morphine, hydromorphone)
  • Myth: Opioids don't worsen pancreatitis via sphincter of Oddi spasm

  • Indications: Cholangitis OR biliary obstruction with bilirubin rising
  • NOT indicated: Mild gallstone pancreatitis without obstruction (just do cholecystectomy later)

Antibiotics:

  • NOT routinely indicated for prophylaxis
  • Give only if: Infected necrosis (proven by aspiration or gas on CT), cholangitis, or extrapancreatic infection
  • If infected necrosis: Carbapenems or fluoroquinolone + metronidazole

  • Timing: During SAME admission once acute inflammation resolves (pancreatic enzymes normalizing)
  • Usually safe after 48-72 hours if improving
  • Recurrence rate 30-60% if not done

  • No prophylactic antibiotics: Don't reduce infection or mortality. Only treat proven infection
  • CT timing: Necrosis takes 48-72hr to develop. Early CT often non-diagnostic
  • Lipase > Amylase: Lipase more sensitive/specific. Can skip amylase entirely
  • Hypertriglyceridemia: If TG >1000, this can CAUSE pancreatitis. Treat with insulin gtt + fibrates

20. CELLULITIS

Skin/Soft Tissue Infection
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: Acute onset of erythema, warmth, edema, and tenderness in affected area. Usually unilateral lower extremity. Pain typically present. Systemic symptoms: fever, chills, malaise in 40-60% of hospitalized patients

Physical Exam: Ill-defined borders spreading centrifugally (vs erysipelas which has raised sharp borders), no fluctuance (vs abscess), lymphangitic streaking in 15-20%, regional lymphadenopathy. Measure and mark borders with pen. Look for portal of entry (trauma, tinea pedis, fissures)

Common Organisms: Œ≤-hemolytic Streptococcus (Group A Strep, most common in non-purulent), Staphylococcus aureus (especially MRSA if purulent/abscess present)

Risk Factors: Obesity, diabetes mellitus, venous insufficiency, chronic lymphedema, tinea pedis (toe web intertrigo), immunosuppression, prior cellulitis (20-30% recurrence rate), skin barrier disruption

Alternate Presentations:

  • Erysipelas: Superficial cellulitis with sharply demarcated, raised, indurated borders. "Orange peel" appearance. Almost always Group A Strep. Face and legs most common
  • Periorbital Cellulitis: Eyelid edema, erythema WITHOUT vision changes or pain with eye movement (vs orbital cellulitis which needs emergent CT/ENT). Can progress to orbital cellulitis
  • Facial Cellulitis: Consider dental source (odontogenic), sinusitis. Higher risk of cavernous sinus thrombosis if near nasolabial triangle
  • Diabetic Patients: More likely polymicrobial, higher risk of progression to necrotizing infection. Check for gas on imaging
  • Immunocompromised: Consider atypical organisms (fungi, mycobacteria, Pseudomonas). May lack fever/leukocytosis despite severe infection
  • Post-Surgical: If within 48 hours, consider Group A Strep (rapidly progressive, very painful). If days later, S. aureus more likely
  • Water Exposure: Vibrio vulnificus (salt water), Aeromonas (fresh water) - both can cause necrotizing fasciitis. Ask about water exposure!
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Clinical Diagnosis: Based on appearance - culture/biopsy rarely needed for typical cellulitis. Positive predictive value of clinical diagnosis >70% by experienced clinicians

Labs (Obtain if systemic symptoms or severe):

  • CBC: Leukocytosis common (WBC >12k in 50%), left shift. Leukopenia concerning for overwhelming sepsis
  • Blood Cultures: Low yield (<5% positive) but obtain if: fever >38.5¬∞C, hypotension, immunocompromised, suspected bacteremia, failed outpatient therapy
  • ESR/CRP: Elevated but non-specific. Can be useful if diagnosis uncertain or to follow treatment response
  • BMP: If systemic toxicity (assess renal function before vancomycin/aminoglycosides)
  • Lactate: If concern for sepsis or necrotizing infection

Imaging (NOT routinely needed):

  • Ultrasound: Can differentiate cellulitis from abscess. "Cobblestoning" of subcutaneous tissue seen in cellulitis. Can identify fluid collections needing drainage
  • CT with IV contrast: If necrotizing fasciitis suspected (gas in tissues, fascial involvement), deep abscess, osteomyelitis concern
  • MRI: Gold standard for osteomyelitis, deep space infections, necrotizing fasciitis. Use if diagnosis unclear and high clinical suspicion
  • X-ray: Can identify foreign body, subcutaneous gas (necrotizing infection), osteomyelitis (after 2 weeks)

Culture/Biopsy (Consider if):

  • Purulent drainage present ‚Üí Send for culture & sensitivity (guides antibiotic de-escalation)
  • Failed initial therapy
  • Immunocompromised (atypical organisms)
  • Penetrating trauma or water exposure (atypical organisms)
  • Punch biopsy/aspiration: Low yield (20-40% positive), painful, generally not recommended for uncomplicated cellulitis

Mark Borders: Draw around erythema with marker, date/time. Essential for tracking progression/regression. Take photo for documentation

Differential Diagnosis to Consider:

  • Stasis Dermatitis: Bilateral, chronic, hyperpigmentation, varicose veins. Often misdiagnosed as cellulitis
  • DVT: Asymmetric edema but typically no erythema. Get duplex if uncertain
  • Contact Dermatitis: Pruritic, vesicular, bilateral distribution, exposure history
  • Necrotizing Fasciitis: Pain out of proportion, hemorrhagic bullae, crepitus, systemic toxicity, rapid progression
  • Gout/Pseudogout: Acute monoarticular arthritis, usually joint-based
  • Lipodermatosclerosis: Chronic, indurated, inverted champagne bottle appearance of lower leg
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Key Decision: Purulent vs Non-Purulent

  • Purulent: Abscess, furuncle, carbuncle, purulent drainage ‚Üí MUST cover MRSA

    • Cephalexin 500mg PO QID √ó 5-10 days (first-line)

    • Cefazolin 1-2g IV q8h (excellent Strep/MSSA coverage)
    • Ceftriaxone 1-2g IV q24h (good for once-daily dosing)
  • PCN Allergy (Non-anaphylaxis):
    • Cephalosporins safe if non-IgE mediated reaction (~98% safe)
  • Severe PCN Allergy (Anaphylaxis):
    • Clindamycin 600mg IV q8h OR 300-450mg PO TID (covers Strep/MSSA, not MRSA reliably)

  • Cirrhosis: Higher risk of Vibrio if shellfish exposure. Consider ceftriaxone + doxycycline

  • Bite Wounds:

  • Elevation: Keep affected limb elevated above heart level. CRITICAL for reducing edema and speeding recovery
  • Analgesia: NSAIDs (ibuprofen 600mg q6h) or acetaminophen 1g q6h. NSAIDs have anti-inflammatory benefit
  • Treat Underlying Conditions:

  • Wound Care: Keep clean and dry. Gentle cleansing with soap and water. No need for topical antibiotics

  • Severe: 10-14 days if slow response, extensive disease, or immunocompromised

  • Response Time: Expect improvement within 48-72 hours. Erythema may initially expand first 24-48hr despite appropriate antibiotics (inflammatory response). If not improving by 72hr, re-evaluate

  • Patient education: Early recognition and treatment at first sign reduces hospitalizations

21. URINARY TRACT INFECTION / PYELONEPHRITIS

Cystitis ‚Ä¢ Pyelonephritis
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Cystitis (Lower UTI): Acute onset of dysuria, urinary frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain/pressure, hematuria (gross or microscopic). NO fever (Temp <38¬∞C) or flank pain. Symptoms typically develop over hours to 1-2 days

Pyelonephritis (Upper UTI): Fever >38.5¬∞C, rigors, unilateral flank/back pain, costovertebral angle (CVA) tenderness on exam, nausea/vomiting. Often accompanied by dysuria and frequency. Can progress to sepsis

Complicated UTI Definition: Structural/functional abnormality of GU tract, instrumentation, immunosuppression, pregnancy, males, indwelling catheter, recent antimicrobial use, diabetes, renal transplant, hospital-acquired. Higher risk of treatment failure and complications

Alternate Presentations:

  • Elderly Patients: May present with altered mental status, confusion, falls, functional decline, or generalized weakness WITHOUT classic UTI symptoms. Fever often absent or blunted. Dysuria may not be reported. High index of suspicion needed
  • Diabetic Patients: Higher risk of emphysematous pyelonephritis (gas-forming infection - E. coli, Klebsiella), papillary necrosis, perinephric abscess. Worse outcomes, longer treatment needed. May have poor glucose control as only manifestation
  • Pregnancy: 20-30% of untreated asymptomatic bacteriuria progresses to pyelonephritis. Pyelonephritis can trigger preterm labor, low birth weight. Must screen ALL pregnant women and treat asymptomatic bacteriuria. Higher risk of recurrence during pregnancy
  • Men (Any Age): Almost always complicated UTI. Assume prostatic involvement until proven otherwise. Need 7-14 days of treatment. Evaluate for structural abnormalities, BPH, prostatitis. First UTI in young man = red flag for anatomic defect
  • Catheter-Associated (CAUTI): New fever, flank pain, suprapubic pain, altered mental status in catheterized patient. Often polymicrobial. Pyuria alone is NOT diagnostic (50% of catheterized patients have asymptomatic pyuria). Need symptoms to treat
  • Perinephric/Renal Abscess: Persistent fever despite 48-72 hours appropriate antibiotics. Unilateral flank pain, palpable flank mass. Risk factors: diabetes, IV drug use, renal stones, obstruction. CT diagnostic. May need percutaneous or surgical drainage
  • Xanthogranulomatous Pyelonephritis: Rare chronic infection causing granulomatous destruction of kidney. Mimics renal mass on imaging. Presents with persistent flank pain, fever, weight loss. Kidney non-functional, requires nephrectomy
  • Emphysematous Pyelonephritis: Life-threatening gas-forming infection. 90% diabetics. Presents with severe sepsis, CVA tenderness. CT shows gas in renal parenchyma. High mortality (20-40%). Requires IV antibiotics + possible nephrectomy
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Urinalysis (Essential for ALL):

  • Pyuria: >10 WBC/hpf highly suggestive. >5 WBC/hpf = abnormal. Absence of pyuria makes UTI unlikely (95% NPV)
  • Bacteriuria: Any bacteria on unspun urine microscopy = ‚â•10^5 CFU/mL
  • Leukocyte Esterase: Sensitive (75-96%) but less specific. Produced by WBCs. Can be false positive with contamination
  • Nitrites: Highly specific (90-100%) but only 50% sensitive. Many uropathogens (Enterococcus, Staph saprophyticus, Pseudomonas) don't produce nitrites. Requires bacteria in bladder ‚â•4 hours
  • Hematuria: Present in 40-60% of UTIs but non-specific
  • WBC Casts: Pathognomonic for pyelonephritis (indicates renal parenchymal involvement). Differentiates from cystitis

Urine Culture & Sensitivity (When to Send):

  • ALWAYS send before antibiotics if: Pyelonephritis, complicated UTI, pregnant, recent antibiotic use, healthcare-associated, symptoms >7 days, recurrent UTI, immunocompromised
  • NOT needed for: Uncomplicated cystitis in young women (treat empirically)
  • Collection: Mid-stream clean catch (not catheter unless catheter present <2 hours). First morning void best
  • Interpretation:
    • ‚â•10^5 CFU/mL = diagnostic for cystitis
    • ‚â•10^4 CFU/mL = diagnostic for pyelonephritis
    • ‚â•10^2 CFU/mL = diagnostic if catheter specimen
    • Multiple organisms = contamination (repeat culture)
  • Common Organisms: E. coli (75-95%), Klebsiella pneumoniae (5-10%), Proteus mirabilis (5%), Enterococcus (5%), Staphylococcus saprophyticus (5-15% in young women)

Blood Cultures:

  • Obtain if: Pyelonephritis with sepsis/hemodynamic instability, immunocompromised, persistent fever despite 48-72hr of antibiotics, concern for bacteremia
  • Positive in 15-25% of hospitalized pyelonephritis patients
  • Usually same organism as urine culture

Additional Labs:

  • CBC: Leukocytosis common in pyelonephritis (WBC often 12-20k with left shift). Leukopenia suggests severe sepsis
  • BMP: Assess renal function (baseline Cr), check for AKI. Evaluate electrolytes
  • Pregnancy test: ALL women of childbearing age (ectopic can present as flank pain, UTI changes management in pregnancy)
  • Lactate: If sepsis suspected (goal <2, elevated suggests tissue hypoperfusion)

Imaging (NOT routine, but obtain if):

  • CT Abdomen/Pelvis WITH IV Contrast (Test of Choice):
    • No clinical improvement after 48-72 hours of appropriate antibiotics
    • Sepsis/septic shock
    • Immunocompromised (evaluate for abscess)
    • Concern for urinary obstruction (hydronephrosis, stones)
    • Recurrent pyelonephritis (>2 episodes)
    • Men with pyelonephritis (evaluate for anatomic abnormality)
    • Suspected emphysematous pyelonephritis (gas in tissues)
    • Suspected perinephric/renal abscess
  • Renal Ultrasound: First-line if pregnant or contraindication to IV contrast. Can detect hydronephrosis (obstruction) but less sensitive for abscess than CT. Good for assessing kidney size/echogenicity
  • KUB X-ray: Limited utility. Can identify radiopaque stones (calcium) but misses 10-20% of stones

Special Diagnostic Considerations:

  • Asymptomatic Bacteriuria (ASB): Positive culture (‚â•10^5 CFU/mL) WITHOUT symptoms. Very common in elderly, catheterized patients. DO NOT TREAT except in pregnancy or before urologic procedures. Treatment causes antibiotic resistance without benefit
  • Sterile Pyuria: WBCs in urine but negative culture. Differential: TB (send AFB culture √ó 3), fungal infection, interstitial nephritis, appendicitis, recent antibiotic use, urethritis (chlamydia, gonorrhea), kidney stones
  • Catheter-Associated UTI Diagnosis: Catheterized patient with fever (>38¬∞C) OR new onset suprapubic pain OR flank pain OR altered mental status WITHOUT other source PLUS positive culture ‚â•10^3 CFU/mL. Pyuria alone is NOT diagnostic

  • Pregnancy ASB MUST be treated: 30% of untreated ASB in pregnancy progresses to pyelonephritis ‚Üí preterm labor. Screen ALL pregnant women at 12-16 weeks and treat positive cultures even if asymptomatic. This is the ONLY population where ASB treatment is indicated

22. C. DIFFICILE INFECTION (CDI)

CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: ‚â•3 unformed (loose or watery) stools in 24 hours in setting of recent antibiotic exposure (typically within past 3 months). Crampy abdominal pain, low-grade fever. Diarrhea is NOT bloody in most cases. Leukocytosis often pronounced (WBC 15-30k)

Physical Exam: Diffuse abdominal tenderness (worse in left lower quadrant), abdominal distension, hyperactive bowel sounds. If peritoneal signs present ‚Üí concerning for toxic megacolon or perforation

Risk Factors:

  • Antibiotic exposure: #1 risk factor. Highest risk: clindamycin, fluoroquinolones, cephalosporins, carbapenems. Even single dose can trigger. Usually within 3 months but can be up to 3 years
  • Healthcare exposure: Hospitalization, nursing home, recent admission
  • Advanced age: >65 years (decreased immunity, increased exposure)
  • Medications: PPIs (impair gastric acid barrier), H2 blockers, chemotherapy
  • Immunosuppression: IBD, transplant, malignancy, HIV
  • GI surgery/manipulation: Recent abdominal surgery, NG tube, PEG tube

Alternate Presentations:

  • Severe CDI: WBC ‚â•15k, Cr ‚â•1.5√ó baseline. Presents with high fever (>38.5¬∞C), severe abdominal pain, hypotension. May have minimal diarrhea (ileus develops)
  • Fulminant CDI (2-3% of cases): Hypotension requiring pressors, shock, ileus, megacolon, perforation, multi-organ failure. WBC >35k or <2k (leukemoid reaction or leukopenia both bad signs). High mortality (30-50%)
  • Toxic Megacolon: Abdominal distension, absent bowel sounds, peritoneal signs. Colon >6cm on imaging. Life-threatening - requires surgical evaluation
  • Elderly Patients: May present with only altered mental status, functional decline, or minimal diarrhea. Lower threshold for testing
  • IBD Patients: Difficult to distinguish from IBD flare. C. diff superinfection in 5-10% of IBD hospitalizations. Always test IBD patients with worsening symptoms
  • Community-Acquired CDI: No recent healthcare or antibiotic exposure (15-25% of cases). Often younger, healthier patients. Associated with PPI use, outpatient antibiotics
  • Asymptomatic Carriage: 3-15% of healthy adults, 20-40% of hospitalized patients colonized without symptoms. Do NOT test or treat asymptomatic patients (even if formed stool sent for testing comes back positive)
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

When to Test: ONLY test patients with ‚â•3 unformed stools in 24 hours. Do NOT test formed stools or asymptomatic patients (high false positive rate from colonization)

Diagnostic Testing (Multi-Step Algorithm Preferred):

  • NAAT (Nucleic Acid Amplification Test/PCR):
    • Detects toxin genes (tcdB). Most sensitive test (>95%)
    • Problem: Can't distinguish active infection from colonization
    • If positive ‚Üí need clinical correlation (symptoms + risk factors)
  • EIA for Toxins A/B:
    • Detects actual toxins in stool. High specificity (>95%) but poor sensitivity (60-80%)
    • If positive ‚Üí definite infection (can start treatment)
    • If negative ‚Üí doesn't rule out (may need NAAT or repeat testing)
  • GDH (Glutamate Dehydrogenase) Antigen:
    • Screening test. Sensitive but not specific (detects organism, not toxin)
    • If negative ‚Üí C. diff ruled out
    • If positive ‚Üí need confirmatory toxin testing
  • Recommended 2-Step Algorithm:
    • Step 1: GDH + Toxin EIA
    • If both positive ‚Üí Treat
    • If GDH positive but Toxin negative ‚Üí Send NAAT (if high suspicion, can empirically treat while awaiting)
    • If both negative ‚Üí C. diff excluded
  • Do NOT send repeat testing: If initially negative but high clinical suspicion persists, can repeat once after 24-48 hours. More than 2 tests in 7 days is excessive
  • Test of cure NOT recommended: Patients can shed toxin for weeks. Treat symptoms, not test results

Labs (Assess Severity):

  • CBC: Leukocytosis common (15-30k). WBC >15k = severe. WBC >35k or <2k = very high risk for complications/death
  • BMP: Cr >1.5√ó baseline = severe. Check for AKI, electrolyte abnormalities (hypokalemia from diarrhea)
  • Albumin: Hypoalbuminemia from protein-losing enteropathy
  • Lactate: Elevated if sepsis/shock

    • Indications: Severe CDI, fulminant presentation, concern for megacolon/perforation, ileus, peritoneal signs
    • Findings: Colonic wall thickening (>4mm), pericolonic stranding, "accordion sign" (contrast trapped in thickened colonic folds), ascites, megacolon (>6cm), pneumatosis
    • Helps determine need for surgery
  • KUB X-ray: Can identify megacolon, free air (perforation), but CT preferred if available
  • Colonoscopy: Generally NOT needed. Consider if diagnosis uncertain or refractory to treatment. Pseudomembranes (yellow-white plaques) are pathognomonic but only present in 50% of cases. Risk of perforation - avoid if severe colitis

  • Non-Severe (Mild-Moderate): WBC <15k, Cr <1.5 mg/dL
  • Severe: WBC ‚â•15k OR Cr ‚â•1.5√ó baseline
  • Fulminant: Hypotension, shock, ileus, megacolon, perforation, ICU admission, WBC >35k or <2k

General Principles:

  • Discontinue PPIs/H2 blockers if possible
  • Contact isolation: Private room, gloves and gown for all contact. Soap and water hand hygiene (alcohol doesn't kill spores)
  • Avoid anti-motility agents: Loperamide, opiates can precipitate toxic megacolon. Contraindicated
  • Avoid unnecessary antibiotics: But continue life-saving antibiotics if needed (C. diff treatment will work alongside)

Initial Episode - Non-Severe/Severe:

    • Higher doses (500mg QID) do NOT improve outcomes for non-fulminant disease
  • ALTERNATIVE: Fidaxomicin 200mg PO BID √ó 10 days

    • Lower recurrence rate than vancomycin (15% vs 25%)
    • Very expensive - reserve for: recurrent CDI, high risk for recurrence, immunocompromised
  • If Oral Vanc/Fidaxomicin Unavailable: Metronidazole 500mg PO TID √ó 10 days

40. SYNCOPE

Vasovagal ‚Ä¢ Cardiac ‚Ä¢ Orthostatic ‚Ä¢ Neurologic
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Definition: Transient loss of consciousness (LOC) with loss of postural tone, followed by spontaneous complete recovery. Caused by global cerebral hypoperfusion. Duration typically <20 seconds

Classic: Sudden LOC, fall, rapid spontaneous recovery without intervention. Patient recalls events before and after (vs seizure with postictal confusion)

Key Historical Questions:

  • Prodrome: Nausea, diaphoresis, tunnel vision, lightheadedness = vasovagal. No warning = cardiac
  • Position: Standing ‚Üí orthostatic or vasovagal. Supine ‚Üí cardiac (very concerning!)
  • Activity: Exertional syncope = cardiac until proven otherwise (HOCM, AS, arrhythmia, PE)
  • Triggers: Pain, emotion, standing, prolonged standing, hot environment = vasovagal. Post-micturition/defecation = situational
  • Recovery: Immediate = syncope. Confusion/lethargy = seizure or stroke
  • Witnesses: Tonic-clonic movements? (can occur in syncope but brief <15sec, vs prolonged in seizure). Tongue biting/incontinence more typical of seizure

Alternate Presentations by Etiology:

  • Vasovagal (Neurocardiogenic): Most common (40-50%). Prodrome of nausea, diaphoresis, visual changes. Triggered by emotional stress, pain, prolonged standing, hot environment. Young patients. Benign
  • Orthostatic Hypotension: Syncope upon standing from sitting/supine. Elderly, autonomic dysfunction, medications (Œ±-blockers, diuretics, vasodilators), volume depletion. BP drop ‚â•20/10 mmHg within 3 minutes of standing
  • Cardiac Arrhythmia: No warning, sudden collapse. Palpitations before/after. High-risk: age >60, exertional, supine, no prodrome, heart disease. Bradyarrhythmias (sick sinus, AV block) or tachyarrhythmias (VT, SVT)
  • Structural Cardiac: Aortic stenosis (exertional syncope, systolic murmur), HOCM (young athletes, exertional, family history sudden death), cardiac tamponade, myxoma, PE (dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain)
  • Neurologic (RARE cause): Vertebrobasilar TIA (diplopia, ataxia, vertigo), subclavian steal, seizure (postictal confusion differentiates from syncope)
  • Situational: Specific triggers - micturition (post-void), defecation, cough, swallow. Vasovagal-mediated
  • Carotid Sinus Hypersensitivity: Elderly men, triggered by tight collar, head turning, shaving. Bradycardia/hypotension with carotid massage
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

ALL Patients Must Have:

  • Detailed History: Events before/during/after, position, activity, prodrome, recovery time, witnesses, prior episodes, medications, cardiac history
  • Physical Exam: Complete cardiac exam (murmurs, JVD, edema), neurologic exam (focal deficits?), orthostatic vitals (supine ‚Üí standing at 1 and 3 min)
  • ECG (MANDATORY for ALL): Look for: prolonged QT (>480ms = high risk for Torsades), Brugada pattern (ST elevation V1-V3), pre-excitation (WPW = short PR, delta wave), epsilon wave (ARVC), pathologic Q waves (old MI), heart block, SVT/VT

Risk Stratification - San Francisco Syncope Rule (HIGH RISK if ANY):

  • History of CHF
  • ECG abnormal (any non-sinus rhythm or new changes)
  • Anemia (Hct <30%)
  • Respiratory - Shortness of breath
  • Triage systolic BP <90 mmHg
  • If ANY present ‚Üí 7-10% risk of serious outcome at 30 days ‚Üí ADMIT

Labs (Based on Clinical Suspicion):

  • Basic: CBC (anemia?), BMP (electrolytes - hypokalemia can cause QT prolongation), glucose (hypoglycemia?)
  • If cardiac suspected: Troponin (ACS?), BNP (heart failure?), D-dimer if PE suspected
  • If volume depletion suspected: BUN/Cr ratio, orthostatic vitals
  • Pregnancy test: All women of childbearing age (ectopic pregnancy can present as syncope)

Advanced Testing (If Indicated):

  • Echocardiography: If structural heart disease suspected (murmur, abnormal ECG, exertional syncope, known heart disease). Assess for AS, HOCM, RV strain (PE), tamponade, LV function
  • Telemetry/Holter Monitor: If arrhythmia suspected. Admit for telemetry if high-risk features. Outpatient Holter (24-48hr) or event monitor (30 days) for recurrent syncope
  • Exercise Stress Test: If exertional syncope (evaluate for ischemia, arrhythmias). Contraindicated if severe AS suspected
  • Tilt Table Test: If recurrent syncope, no cardiac cause found. Reproduces vasovagal response. Positive = BP drop >25mmHg or HR <60 with symptoms
  • Electrophysiology Study (EP Study): For unexplained syncope with structural heart disease or high suspicion for arrhythmia. Can induce/document VT, assess sinus/AV node function
  • CT Head: Only if focal neurologic deficits, head trauma, persistent altered mental status. Syncope alone does NOT require head CT
  • Carotid Ultrasound: Generally low yield. Consider if carotid bruit or vertebrobasilar symptoms
  • CT PE Protocol: If dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain, hypoxia, tachycardia, RV strain on ECG

Orthostatic Vitals Technique:

  • Supine √ó 5 minutes ‚Üí measure BP/HR
  • Stand immediately ‚Üí measure at 1 minute and 3 minutes
  • Positive: SBP drop ‚â•20 mmHg OR DBP drop ‚â•10 mmHg OR HR increase ‚â•30 bpm (or HR >120)
  • Symptoms (lightheadedness, presyncope) with standing = clinical orthostasis even if BP criteria not met
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

General Principles:

  • Treatment depends on underlying cause
  • Admit if high-risk features (see San Francisco Syncope Rule, cardiac cause suspected)
  • Discharge if low-risk and clear benign etiology (young, vasovagal, normal ECG, normal exam)

Vasovagal Syncope (Reflex/Neurocardiogenic):

  • Reassurance: Benign condition, excellent prognosis. Explain pathophysiology
  • Trigger Avoidance: Avoid prolonged standing, hot environments, dehydration, emotional stress. Sit/lie down at first prodromal symptom
  • Hydration: Increase fluid intake to 2-3 L/day. Liberal salt intake (6-10g/day) if no contraindications
  • Physical Counterpressure Maneuvers: At first sign of prodrome: leg crossing + tensing, squatting, arm tensing. Can abort episode
  • Tilt Training: Gradual exposure to prolonged standing (start 5 min, increase to 30+ min)
  • Pharmacologic (Refractory cases only):
    • Midodrine 5-10mg TID (Œ±-agonist, increases BP) - first-line medication
    • Fludrocortisone 0.1-0.2mg daily (volume expansion)
    • Œ≤-blockers (metoprolol) - controversial, limited evidence
  • Pacemaker: Very rarely indicated. Only if severe cardioinhibitory response (asystole >3 sec) on tilt table and failed medical therapy

Orthostatic Hypotension:

  • Identify and Address Causes:
    • Volume depletion ‚Üí IV fluids, oral hydration
    • Medications ‚Üí stop/reduce Œ±-blockers, diuretics, vasodilators, antihypertensives, antidepressants
    • Autonomic dysfunction ‚Üí treat underlying (diabetes, Parkinson's, amyloidosis)
  • Non-Pharmacologic:
    • Rise slowly from supine/sitting (sit at edge of bed √ó 1 min before standing)
    • Increase fluid intake (2-3 L/day) and salt (6-10g/day if no CHF)
    • Compression stockings (waist-high, 30-40 mmHg) - wear before rising
    • Elevate head of bed 10-20 degrees at night (reduces nocturnal diuresis)
    • Avoid large meals (can cause postprandial hypotension)
    • Physical counterpressure maneuvers
  • Pharmacologic:
    • Midodrine 2.5-10mg TID (last dose before 6 PM to avoid supine hypertension)
    • Fludrocortisone 0.1-0.3mg daily (watch for hypokalemia, edema, supine hypertension)
    • Droxidopa 100-600mg TID (norepinephrine precursor, for neurogenic OH)
    • Pyridostigmine 60mg TID (if autonomic failure)

Cardiac Syncope - ADMIT for Telemetry:

  • Arrhythmia Management:
    • Bradycardia (sick sinus, AV block) ‚Üí Cardiology consult, consider pacemaker
    • Tachyarrhythmia (VT, SVT) ‚Üí Antiarrhythmics, ablation, ICD evaluation
    • Long QT syndrome ‚Üí Remove offending drugs, electrolyte repletion (Mg, K), Œ≤-blockers, ICD if high risk
    • Brugada, ARVC ‚Üí EP study, ICD placement
  • Structural Disease:
    • Aortic stenosis ‚Üí Cardiology/CT surgery referral for valve replacement if severe + symptomatic
    • HOCM ‚Üí Œ≤-blockers, avoid volume depletion/vasodilators. ICD if high risk. Septal reduction if refractory
    • Pulmonary embolism ‚Üí Anticoagulation, thrombolytics if massive PE
    • Cardiac tamponade ‚Üí Pericardiocentesis
  • Ischemia: If syncope due to ACS ‚Üí treat per ACS protocol (aspirin, heparin, cath lab)

Situational Syncope:

  • Micturition syncope ‚Üí Sit to void, avoid straining, stay hydrated
  • Defecation syncope ‚Üí Avoid straining (stool softeners), adequate hydration
  • Cough/swallow syncope ‚Üí Treat underlying cause (GERD, cough), maneuvers to reduce triggering

Neurologic Syncope (Rare):

  • Vertebrobasilar TIA ‚Üí Neurology consult, antiplatelet therapy, imaging (MRA/CTA neck)
  • Subclavian steal ‚Üí Vascular surgery referral
  • Seizure (if postictal confusion suggests) ‚Üí Neurology consult, antiepileptics, EEG
üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • ECG is MANDATORY for ALL syncope patients: Single most important test. Can diagnose Brugada, long QT, WPW, ARVC, heart block, old MI. Never discharge syncope patient without ECG
  • Syncope while supine = cardiac until proven otherwise: Vasovagal almost never occurs supine. If patient syncopizes lying down, think arrhythmia and admit for telemetry
  • Exertional syncope = high-risk: Think HOCM (young athletes), AS (elderly), VT, coronary ischemia. These patients need echo, stress test, and cardiology evaluation
  • Red flags for cardiac syncope: Age >60, exertional, supine position, no prodrome, known heart disease, abnormal ECG, family history sudden cardiac death. Admit these patients
  • Vasovagal has a prodrome, cardiac doesn't: If patient describes nausea, diaphoresis, tunnel vision before passing out = vasovagal (low risk). No warning, sudden collapse = cardiac (high risk)
  • Don't confuse syncope with seizure: Syncope = brief LOC (<20 sec), rapid complete recovery, may have brief myoclonic jerks (<15 sec). Seizure = prolonged LOC, tonic-clonic movements, tongue biting, incontinence, postictal confusion/lethargy. But can be tough to distinguish!
  • Head CT rarely useful: Only if focal neurologic findings, head trauma, or persistent altered mental status. Syncope is due to global hypoperfusion, not focal brain lesion. Don't reflexively order head CT
  • San Francisco Syncope Rule is your friend: If none of the 5 criteria (CHF, abnormal ECG, Hct <30%, SOB, SBP <90), patient is low-risk (<1% serious outcome) and can likely be discharged with outpatient follow-up if vasovagal etiology clear
  • Orthostatic vitals technique matters: Must be supine √ó 5 min first, then check at 1 and 3 minutes standing. Immediate standing without adequate supine time can give false positives
  • Young healthy patient with classic vasovagal story needs minimal workup: Prodrome, triggered by standing/heat/emotion, quick recovery, normal ECG = can discharge with reassurance and anticipatory guidance. Don't over-test these patients
  • Medications are a common culprit: Œ±-blockers (BPH meds), diuretics, antihypertensives, TCAs, antipsychotics, PDE5 inhibitors. Always review medication list carefully
  • Carotid massage to diagnose carotid sinus hypersensitivity: Only perform if safe (no carotid bruits, no recent stroke/TIA). Massage one side √ó 5 seconds. Positive = >3 sec asystole or >50 mmHg BP drop. Never massage both sides simultaneously!

COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

From Syncope Episode: Trauma from fall (head injury, fractures, lacerations), motor vehicle accidents (if driving), sudden cardiac death (if malignant arrhythmia), prolonged cerebral hypoperfusion causing ischemia (rare)

From Underlying Cardiac Cause: Sudden cardiac death (VT/VF, complete heart block), acute MI/ACS, cardiac arrest, stroke from arrhythmia with thrombus formation, progression of structural heart disease (AS, HOCM)

From Recurrent Episodes: Injury from repeated falls, impaired quality of life, anxiety/depression, loss of driving privileges, loss of employment (especially if operate machinery), social isolation

From Treatment: Supine hypertension (midodrine, fludrocortisone - can cause nocturnal HTN), hypokalemia (fludrocortisone), edema (fludrocortisone), bradycardia (Œ≤-blockers), pacemaker complications (infection, lead malfunction), ICD shocks (appropriate or inappropriate)
