1. ACUTE CORONARY SYNDROME (ACS)

STEMI ‚Ä¢ NSTEMI ‚Ä¢ Unstable Angina
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: Substernal chest pain/pressure >20 minutes, radiation to jaw/left arm/back, diaphoresis, nausea, dyspnea. Pain not relieved by rest or nitroglycerin.

Alternate Presentations:

  • Diabetics/Elderly/Women: Atypical symptoms - dyspnea alone, weakness, epigastric pain, syncope
  • Silent MI: No chest pain (30% of diabetics)
  • RV Infarction: Hypotension with clear lungs, JVD, Kussmaul sign
  • Posterior MI: Isolated ST depression V1-V3, need posterior leads (V7-V9)
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Immediate: ECG within 10 minutes, troponin (repeat at 3 and 6 hours if initial negative), CBC, BMP, PT/INR, lipid panel, CXR

STEMI Criteria: ST elevation ‚â•1mm in 2+ contiguous leads OR new LBBB OR ST depression V1-V3 (posterior MI)

Calculations: HEART Score, TIMI STEMI, TIMI NSTEMI, GRACE Score

üßÆ HEART Score Calculator
Use: Risk stratification for chest pain patients to determine if safe for outpatient evaluation. Score 0-3 = low risk (outpatient), 4-6 = moderate risk (observe), 7-10 = high risk (admit).
üßÆ TIMI Risk Score - STEMI
Use: Predicts 30-day mortality in STEMI patients. Score 0-2 = 2% mortality, 3-4 = 5%, 5-6 = 12%, >6 = 36%.
üßÆ TIMI Risk Score - NSTEMI/UA
Use: Risk of death, MI, or urgent revascularization within 14 days. Score 0-2 = 5% risk (low), 3-4 = 13% (moderate), 5-7 = 41% (high).
üßÆ GRACE Score Calculator
Use: Estimates in-hospital and 6-month mortality in ACS. Low risk <109, intermediate 109-140, high >140. More accurate than TIMI for mortality prediction.
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Initial Management (MONA-BAH):

  • Morphine: 2-4mg IV PRN (use cautiously, may worsen outcomes)
  • Oxygen: Only if SpO2 <90%
  • Nitroglycerin: 0.4mg SL q5min √ó 3 (avoid if RV infarct, SBP <90, recent PDE5 inhibitor)
  • Aspirin: 325mg PO chewed immediately
  • Œ≤-Blocker: Metoprolol 25-50mg PO (avoid if HF, bradycardia, hypotension)
  • Anticoagulation: Heparin bolus + infusion or enoxaparin
  • High-intensity statin: Atorvastatin 80mg

P2Y12 Inhibitor: Ticagrelor 180mg load (preferred) or clopidogrel 600mg load

STEMI Specific: Door-to-balloon <90 minutes. Call cath lab immediately. If PCI unavailable within 120 min, consider fibrinolytics (tPA, reteplase, tenecteplase)

NSTEMI Specific: Risk stratify with TIMI/GRACE. High-risk ‚Üí early invasive strategy (<24hr). Low-risk ‚Üí medical management, stress test before discharge

Post-MI Arrhythmia Monitoring:

  • 48-Hour Standard: Continuous cardiac telemetry monitoring for minimum 48 hours is the standard post-MI observation period. Life-threatening arrhythmias (VF, VT, high-grade AV blocks) are most common in first 48 hours post-infarction, with peak risk in first 24 hours
  • Early Discharge Considerations: Hemodynamically stable, low-risk patients without complications may be considered for discharge after 24 hours. However, patients discharged before 48 hours remain at elevated risk for arrhythmias and require careful patient selection, thorough education about warning signs, and clear return precautions
  • Extended Monitoring (>48hr) indicated if: Arrhythmias detected, hemodynamic instability, large infarct, reduced EF (<40%), high-risk features, or persistent ischemia
  • Serial troponins: Peak at 12-24 hours, trend until downtrending
  • Daily ECG: Assess for evolution of infarct, new ischemia, conduction abnormalities
  • Echo before discharge: Assess LV function (EF), wall motion abnormalities, mechanical complications, LV thrombus
üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • 48-hour arrhythmia window: Standard telemetry monitoring is 48 hours post-MI. Low-risk patients MAY be discharged at 24 hours if stable, but they remain at elevated arrhythmia risk until 48 hours. Early discharge requires careful patient selection and thorough education about warning signs (palpitations, syncope, chest pain)
  • Sgarbossa Criteria: Diagnose STEMI with LBBB: ST elevation ‚â•1mm concordant with QRS (5 points), ST depression ‚â•1mm V1-V3 (3 points), ST elevation ‚â•5mm discordant (2 points). Score ‚â•3 = STEMI
  • Wellens Syndrome: Biphasic or deep inverted T waves V2-V3 in pain-free patient = critical LAD stenosis, high risk of anterior STEMI. No stress test! Go to cath.
  • RV Infarction Pearl: If inferior STEMI with hypotension, always get right-sided leads (V4R). Treat with aggressive IVF, avoid nitrates/diuretics
  • Troponin timing: Can be negative first 2-4 hours. Serial troponins essential if high suspicion
  • Don't forget: Type 2 MI (supply-demand mismatch from anemia, sepsis, tachyarrhythmia) - treat underlying cause, not necessarily cath
‚ö†Ô∏è ESCALATE TO ICU IF:
  • Hemodynamic instability (SBP <90, shock)
  • Malignant arrhythmias (VT/VF, high-grade AV block)
  • Mechanical complications (papillary rupture, VSD, free wall rupture)
  • Acute heart failure/cardiogenic shock
  • Need for pressors or mechanical support (IABP, Impella)
  • Post-cardiac arrest
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • Hemodynamically stable >24 hours (minimum for early discharge in low-risk patients; standard 48-hour telemetry monitoring preferred)
  • No recurrent chest pain or ischemic symptoms
  • No arrhythmias requiring intervention during monitoring period
  • LVEF assessed (echo)
  • Successful revascularization (if indicated)
  • On DAPT, statin, Œ≤-blocker, ACE-I (if EF <40%)
  • Cardiac rehab referral arranged
  • Patient educated on arrhythmia warning signs if discharged <48 hours
  • Clear return precautions given
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

Early (hours-days): Arrhythmias (VF, VT, bradycardia), cardiogenic shock, mechanical complications (papillary muscle rupture, VSD, free wall rupture), acute MR

Late (days-weeks): Heart failure, LV thrombus, pericarditis (early: Dressler's syndrome 2-10 weeks), recurrent ischemia

2. ACUTE DECOMPENSATED HEART FAILURE

HFrEF ‚Ä¢ HFpEF ‚Ä¢ Acute Exacerbation
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: Progressive dyspnea, orthopnea, PND, lower extremity edema, weight gain. Physical exam: JVD, bibasilar rales, S3 gallop, peripheral edema, hepatomegaly.

Alternate Presentations:

  • Flash Pulmonary Edema: Acute onset, often with HTN emergency, minimal peripheral edema
  • High Output HF: Warm extremities, bounding pulses (thyrotoxicosis, AV fistula, severe anemia)
  • RV Failure: Isolated lower extremity edema, ascites, hepatic congestion without pulmonary edema
  • Elderly: Confusion, fatigue, decreased appetite may predominate over dyspnea
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Labs: BNP/NT-proBNP (BNP >400 or NT-proBNP >900 suggests HF), troponin (r/o ACS), BMP (assess renal function), CBC, Mg, TSH, LFTs

Imaging: CXR (cardiomegaly, pulmonary edema, pleural effusions), Echo (assess EF, valves, wall motion)

ECG: Look for ischemia, LVH, arrhythmias

Calculations: NYHA Class, EF%, fluid balance (daily weights), creatinine clearance

Classify Hemodynamics: Warm vs cold (perfusion), wet vs dry (volume status)

TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Initial Management:

  • O2: Maintain SpO2 >90%. Consider NIPPV (BiPAP/CPAP) if respiratory distress
  • Diuresis (Wet): IV furosemide 40mg (or 2√ó home dose) bolus, then assess response. Consider continuous infusion if poor response. Goal negative 1-2L/day
  • Vasodilators (if SBP >90): Nitroglycerin IV for afterload reduction, especially if hypertensive
  • Sodium/Fluid Restriction: <2g sodium/day, <2L fluid/day
  • Daily weights: Essential for monitoring

Cold & Wet (Low Output): Consider inotropes (dobutamine, milrinone) with invasive monitoring

Chronic HF Management (Initiate/Optimize):

  • HFrEF (EF <40%): Guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT)
  • ACE-I/ARB or ARNI (sacubitril-valsartan)
  • Œ≤-blocker (carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, bisoprolol) - start low when stable
  • Aldosterone antagonist (spironolactone, eplerenone) if EF <35%
  • SGLT2 inhibitor (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin) - proven mortality benefit
  • HFpEF (EF ‚â•50%): Treat HTN, AF, ischemia. SGLT2i now indicated
üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • BNP Pearls: Obesity lowers BNP. Renal failure raises it. Age-adjust NT-proBNP: <50yo: >450, 50-75yo: >900, >75yo: >1800
  • Diuretic Resistance: If poor response to IV lasix, try: (1) Increase dose, (2) Continuous infusion, (3) Add thiazide (metolazone 2.5-5mg 30min before lasix), (4) Add acetazolamide
  • Cardiorenal Syndrome: Cr bump with diuresis is often acceptable if congestion improving. Monitor BUN:Cr ratio (>20:1 suggests prerenal)
  • Don't stop GDMT: Avoid stopping ACE-I/Œ≤-blocker acutely unless cardiogenic shock. Can hold during diuresis, restart before discharge
  • B-type natriuretic peptide diuretics: Nesiritide (BNP analog) has no mortality benefit and increases hypotension - rarely used
‚ö†Ô∏è ESCALATE TO ICU IF:
  • Cardiogenic shock (SBP <90, cool extremities, AMS, oliguria)
  • Need for inotropes or mechanical support
  • Acute respiratory failure requiring intubation
  • Refractory pulmonary edema
  • Malignant arrhythmias
  • ACS with hemodynamic instability
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • Euvolemic (at or near dry weight)
  • No dyspnea at rest
  • Stable vital signs, O2 sat on room air
  • Transition to PO diuretics established
  • On optimized GDMT (HFrEF)
  • BMP stable, Cr at acceptable baseline
  • Patient education completed
  • Close follow-up arranged (within 7 days)
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

During Admission: Cardiorenal syndrome, electrolyte abnormalities (hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, hyponatremia), arrhythmias (AF most common), worsening renal function

Long-term: Recurrent admissions (50% at 6 months), progressive renal dysfunction, sudden cardiac death, need for advanced therapies (LVAD, transplant)

3. ATRIAL FIBRILLATION

RVR ‚Ä¢ New-Onset ‚Ä¢ Chronic AF Management
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: Palpitations, irregular pulse, dyspnea, chest discomfort, fatigue, lightheadedness. Irregularly irregular rhythm on exam.

Alternate Presentations:

  • Asymptomatic: 30-40% discovered incidentally on exam or ECG
  • Embolic stroke: First presentation in some patients
  • Tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy: Chronic RVR leading to heart failure
  • Hyperthyroid AF: Lone AF in young patient, check TSH
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Labs: TSH (always), BMP, Mg, CBC, troponin (if chest pain), digoxin level (if on digoxin)

Imaging: ECG (confirm AF, assess rate, look for ischemia/WPW), CXR, TTE (assess for structural disease, valve disease, LV function, LA size)

TEE: Before cardioversion if AF >48hr or unknown duration (r/o LA thrombus), unless patient on therapeutic anticoagulation ‚â•3 weeks

Calculations:

  • CHA‚ÇÇDS‚ÇÇ-VASc: Stroke risk (CHF, HTN, Age ‚â•75 [2pts], DM, Stroke/TIA [2pts], Vascular disease, Age 65-74, Sex [female]). Score ‚â•2 (‚ôÇ) or ‚â•3 (‚ôÄ) ‚Üí anticoagulate
  • HAS-BLED: Bleeding risk (HTN, Abnormal renal/liver, Stroke, Bleeding, Labile INR, Elderly, Drugs/alcohol). Score ‚â•3 = high bleeding risk
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Rate Control (First-line for most):

  • Œ≤-Blockers: Metoprolol 25-50mg PO q6h or 2.5-5mg IV, esmolol gtt (ICU). Goal HR <110 at rest
  • Non-dihydropyridine CCB: Diltiazem 0.25mg/kg IV over 2min, then 5-15mg/hr gtt OR 30-60mg PO q6h
  • Digoxin: 0.25mg IV/PO (less effective, use if HF or sedentary patients). Loading: 0.5mg ‚Üí 0.25mg q6h √ó 2
  • Avoid: Œ≤-blockers and CCBs together (risk of heart block), rate control in WPW (can precipitate VF)

Rhythm Control:

  • Indications: Hemodynamic instability, highly symptomatic despite rate control, first episode, young patient preference
  • Cardioversion: If AF <48hr OR on anticoagulation ‚â•3 weeks OR TEE negative
  • Synchronized DC cardioversion: 120-200J biphasic (under sedation)
  • Chemical: Flecainide 300mg PO (if no CAD), ibutilide 1mg IV over 10min (monitor QT)
  • Antiarrhythmics: Amiodarone (safest in structural disease), sotalol, dofetilide (in-hospital initiation)

Anticoagulation:

  • CHA‚ÇÇDS‚ÇÇ-VASc ‚â•2 (‚ôÇ) or ‚â•3 (‚ôÄ): Anticoagulate (DOACs preferred over warfarin)
  • DOACs: Apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban, dabigatran (avoid if mechanical valve or severe MS)
  • Post-cardioversion: Anticoagulate √ó 4 weeks minimum, longer if stroke risk factors
üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • WPW + AF = Emergency: Irregular wide-complex tachycardia. DO NOT use AV nodal blockers (can precipitate VF). Cardiovert or procainamide
  • Rate vs Rhythm: AFFIRM/RACE trials show no mortality difference. Most patients do fine with rate control alone
  • Anticoagulation paradox: HAS-BLED ‚â•3 means high bleeding risk, but often same patients who need anticoagulation most. Don't withhold if CHA‚ÇÇDS‚ÇÇ-VASc high
  • Holiday Heart: Alcohol-induced AF. Often converts spontaneously with abstinence
  • TEE before cardioversion: Even if on anticoagulation <3 weeks, consider TEE strategy to expedite cardioversion safely
‚ö†Ô∏è ESCALATE TO ICU IF:
  • Hemodynamic instability (SBP <90, shock)
  • Acute heart failure/pulmonary edema with RVR
  • Acute MI with RVR
  • Need for continuous IV rate control infusions
  • Post-cardioversion complications
  • RVR refractory to multiple agents
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • Rate controlled (HR <110 at rest, <120 with activity)
  • Hemodynamically stable ‚â•24 hours
  • Transitioned to oral rate control meds
  • Anticoagulation started (if indicated)
  • No signs of heart failure
  • Follow-up arranged (cardiology if new-onset)
  • If cardioverted: Stable rhythm ‚â•24hr
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

Acute: Stroke/TIA (most feared), hemodynamic compromise, heart failure, ACS (demand ischemia from RVR)

Chronic: Tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy (EF improves with rate/rhythm control), thromboembolism, bleeding (if anticoagulated)

4. HYPERTENSIVE EMERGENCY

End-Organ Damage ‚Ä¢ ICU Management
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Definition: Severe hypertension (typically SBP >180 or DBP >120) WITH acute end-organ damage

Classic: Severe headache, visual changes, chest pain, dyspnea, altered mental status, focal neurologic deficits

Alternate Presentations:

  • Neurologic: Hypertensive encephalopathy (HA, confusion, seizures), stroke (ischemic/hemorrhagic), PRES
  • Cardiac: Acute MI, acute pulmonary edema, aortic dissection
  • Renal: Acute kidney injury, hematuria, proteinuria
  • Microangiopathic: Hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia

Hypertensive Urgency: Severe HTN WITHOUT end-organ damage. Managed outpatient with PO meds

DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Labs: BMP, troponin, CBC, UA, peripheral smear if hemolysis suspected, BNP if APE concern

Imaging: ECG, CXR, Head CT if neuro symptoms, CT chest if dissection suspected

Fundoscopic: Look for papilledema, hemorrhages, exudates

Calculate Target BP: Initial reduction 10-20% in 1st hour, then 5-15% over next 23 hours. Avoid >25% reduction in first 24hr

TREATMENT PROTOCOL

General: ICU admission, continuous BP monitoring (arterial line often needed), IV antihypertensives

IV Agents:

  • Nicardipine: 5mg/hr, titrate by 2.5mg/hr q5-15min (max 15mg/hr). First-line
  • Labetalol: 20mg IV bolus, then 20-80mg q10min OR 0.5-2mg/min infusion
  • Clevidipine: 1-2mg/hr, double q90sec (max 21mg/hr). Short-acting
  • Esmolol: Œ≤-blocker for dissection. 500mcg/kg load, then 50-300mcg/kg/min
  • Nitroprusside: 0.25-10mcg/kg/min. For APE. Watch cyanide toxicity

Specific Scenarios:

  • Aortic Dissection: SBP <120 in 20min. Esmolol FIRST, then nicardipine
  • Ischemic Stroke: Permissive HTN! Allow SBP up to 220 (or 185 if tPA)
  • Hemorrhagic Stroke: Goal SBP 140-160
  • Eclampsia: Mag sulfate + hydralazine/labetalol. Deliver baby!
üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • Urgency vs Emergency: Only EMERGENCY needs ICU/IV meds. Urgency = oral meds over hours-days
  • Don't drop too fast: Cerebral autoregulation reset in chronic HTN. Rapid drop ‚Üí stroke/MI
  • Ischemic stroke exception: Brain needs perfusion! Only lower if SBP >220 or giving tPA
  • PRES: Headache, confusion, seizures, vision loss + posterior white matter edema. Usually reversible
  • Transition to PO: Start long-acting oral agents before stopping IV to prevent rebound
‚ö†Ô∏è ESCALATE TO ICU IF:
  • ALL hypertensive emergencies require ICU
  • Need for continuous IV infusion
  • Acute end-organ damage
  • Altered mental status
  • Arterial line needed
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • BP controlled on oral regimen ‚â•24hr
  • End-organ damage stabilized
  • Off IV medications
  • Patient educated on compliance
  • Close follow-up arranged (within 1 week)
  • Secondary causes evaluated if indicated
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

From Hypertension: Stroke, MI, aortic dissection, acute renal failure, retinopathy, heart failure

From Treatment: Hypotension, stroke from rapid BP drop, rebound HTN, cyanide toxicity (nitroprusside)

5. COMMUNITY-ACQUIRED PNEUMONIA (CAP)

Outpatient ‚Ä¢ Inpatient ‚Ä¢ ICU Management
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: Fever, cough (productive/dry), dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain. Exam: Tachypnea, rales/crackles, bronchial breath sounds, dullness, egophony

Alternate Presentations:

  • Elderly: Minimal fever, confusion/delirium, weakness, falls. May lack respiratory symptoms
  • Atypical Organisms: Dry cough, headache, myalgias, diarrhea (Legionella, Mycoplasma)
  • Aspiration: Witnessed event, right lower lobe/posterior segments, foul sputum
  • Immunocompromised: PCP, CMV, TB
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Imaging: CXR (PA and lateral) - infiltrate confirms diagnosis

Labs (if admitted): CBC, BMP, procalcitonin, blood cultures √ó 2, sputum Gram stain/culture, ABG if hypoxic

Special: Legionella urinary Ag, Strep pneumo urinary Ag (if severe), respiratory viral panel, HIV if risk factors

Severity Scores:

  • CURB-65: Confusion, Urea >20, RR ‚â•30, BP (SBP <90 or DBP ‚â§60), Age ‚â•65. Score 0-1: outpatient, 2: admit, ‚â•3: ICU
  • PSI/PORT: More complex. Classes I-II outpatient, III consider admit, IV-V admit
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Outpatient (CURB-65 0-1):

  • Previously healthy: Amoxicillin 1g TID √ó 5d OR doxycycline 100mg BID √ó 5d
  • With comorbidities: Amoxicillin-clavulanate + macrolide OR levofloxacin 750mg daily

Inpatient Non-ICU:

  • Standard: Ceftriaxone 1-2g IV daily + azithromycin 500mg IV/PO daily
  • Alternative: Levofloxacin 750mg daily or moxifloxacin 400mg daily
  • Duration: Minimum 5 days if afebrile √ó 48-72hr and stable

ICU (Severe CAP):

  • Standard: Ceftriaxone 2g IV daily + azithromycin 500mg IV daily
  • MRSA Risk: Add vancomycin or linezolid
  • Pseudomonas Risk: Pip-tazo or cefepime + fluoroquinolone

Supportive: O2 to SpO2 >90%, IV fluids, VTE prophylaxis

Classic: Dyspnea, wheezing, chest tightness, cough, accessory muscle use, prolonged expiration

  • Moderate: Speaks phrases, HR 100-120, PEF 40-70%

Bronchodilators:

  • Albuterol: 2.5-5mg nebulized q20min √ó 3, then reassess. Consider continuous nebulization if severe

  • Terbutaline: 0.25mg subQ (use with caution - cardiac side effects)
  • Heliox: May reduce work of breathing (helium-oxygen mixture)

  • Normal PCO2 is concerning: Asthma patients should be hypocapnic (from hyperventilation). Normal/high CO2 = respiratory fatigue
  • Steroid timing: Takes 4-6 hours to work. Give early! Don't wait for severe deterioration

  • Need for continuous nebulization
  • Hemodynamic instability
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • PEF or FEV1 >70% predicted or personal best

  • On appropriate controller medication
  • Has action plan and follow-up arranged

  • Small PE: May be asymptomatic or minimal symptoms

  • V/Q Scan: If contrast contraindicated
  • Lower Extremity Doppler US: Look for DVT source

10. ACUTE KIDNEY INJURY (AKI)

Prerenal ‚Ä¢ Intrinsic ‚Ä¢ Postrenal
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Definition: Increase in Cr ‚â•0.3 mg/dL within 48hr OR increase to ‚â•1.5√ó baseline OR urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hr for 6hr

Classic: Oliguria, fluid overload, edema, uremic symptoms (N/V, confusion, asterixis, pericarditis)

Alternate Presentations:

  • Non-oliguric AKI: Cr rising but urine output preserved (often ATN from nephrotoxins)
  • Hyperkalemia: May be presenting sign, can be life-threatening
  • Metabolic Acidosis: High anion gap from uremia
  • Elderly: Minimal symptoms despite significant Cr elevation
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Labs: BMP (Cr, BUN, K+), baseline Cr for comparison, UA with microscopy, urine electrolytes (Na+, Cr, osmolality)

Imaging: Renal US (assess for obstruction, kidney size, hydronephrosis)

Consider: Renal biopsy if diagnosis unclear or concern for glomerulonephritis

Categorize AKI:

  • Prerenal (60-70%): Decreased renal perfusion - volume depletion, heart failure, cirrhosis, NSAIDs
  • Intrinsic (25-40%): Acute tubular necrosis (ischemia, nephrotoxins), acute interstitial nephritis, glomerulonephritis
  • Postrenal (5-10%): Obstruction - BPH, kidney stones, malignancy, neurogenic bladder

TREATMENT PROTOCOL

General Principles:

  • Treat underlying cause
  • Stop nephrotoxins (NSAIDs, ACE-I/ARB if AKI severe, aminoglycosides, contrast)
  • Adjust all medication doses for GFR
  • Avoid further insults (hypotension, contrast, nephrotoxic drugs)

Prerenal AKI:

  • Volume Depletion: IV fluids (LR or NS), correct hypotension
  • Cardiorenal: Diuresis if volume overloaded, optimize cardiac output
  • Hepatorenal: Albumin + midodrine + octreotide, or terlipressin

Intrinsic AKI:

  • ATN: Supportive care, avoid nephrotoxins, optimize hemodynamics. Usually resolves in 1-3 weeks
  • AIN (Acute Interstitial Nephritis): Stop offending drug, consider steroids if severe/not improving
  • Glomerulonephritis: Nephrology consult, may need steroids/immunosuppression ¬± plasmapheresis

Postrenal AKI:

  • Relieve Obstruction: Foley catheter (if bladder outlet obstruction), nephrostomy tubes, ureteral stents
  • Watch for post-obstructive diuresis - may need aggressive fluid replacement

Indications for Dialysis (AEIOU):

  • Acidosis - severe metabolic acidosis (pH <7.1) refractory to medical management
  • Electrolytes - hyperkalemia refractory to medical therapy
  • Ingestions - toxic alcohols, lithium, salicylates
  • Overload - volume overload refractory to diuretics, pulmonary edema
  • Uremia - uremic pericarditis, encephalopathy, bleeding

  • BUN:Cr ratio: >20:1 suggests prerenal (BUN reabsorbed more than Cr). <15:1 suggests intrinsic renal disease
  • Muddy brown casts: Pathognomonic for ATN on urine microscopy
  • Triple whammy: ACE-I/ARB + diuretic + NSAID = high risk for AKI. Avoid this combination!

  • Need for emergent dialysis
  • Hemodynamic instability

11. HYPERKALEMIA

K+ >5.5 mEq/L ‚Ä¢ Life-Threatening Arrhythmias
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: Often asymptomatic until severe. Weakness, paresthesias, palpitations

ECG Changes (Progressive):

  • Mild (5.5-6.5): Peaked T waves (tall, narrow, symmetric)
  • Moderate (6.5-8): PR prolongation, P wave flattening/loss, QRS widening
  • Severe (>8): Sine wave pattern, ventricular fibrillation, asystole

Alternate Presentations:

  • Pseudohyperkalemia: Hemolysis during blood draw, severe leukocytosis/thrombocytosis, fist clenching
  • Acute on Chronic: Patients on ACE-I/ARB with CKD who take NSAIDs or get dehydrated
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Labs: Repeat K+ to confirm (rule out pseudohyperkalemia), BMP, Mg, Ca, VBG, consider digoxin level if on digoxin

ECG: CRITICAL - assess for cardiotoxicity. ECG changes indicate true emergency

Identify Cause:

  • Increased Intake: K+ supplements, salt substitutes, IV K+
  • Transcellular Shift: Acidosis, insulin deficiency, Œ≤-blocker, digoxin toxicity, succinylcholine, tumor lysis
  • Decreased Excretion: AKI/CKD, hypoaldosteronism, ACE-I/ARB, K+-sparing diuretics, NSAIDs, heparin, TMP-SMX
TREATMENT PROTOCOL (Sequence Matters!)

Step 1: Cardiac Membrane Stabilization (if ECG changes or K >6.5):

  • Calcium Gluconate: 1-2 amps (10-20 mL of 10% solution) IV over 2-3 min
  • OR Calcium Chloride: 0.5-1g (5-10 mL of 10%) IV via central line (more potent, sclerosing)
  • Effect: Immediate (1-3 min), duration 30-60 min
  • Does NOT lower K+, only protects heart
  • Can repeat dose if persistent ECG changes

Step 2: Shift K+ Intracellularly (Temporary):

  • Insulin + Dextrose: Regular insulin 10 units IV + D50 50mL (25g dextrose) IV push
    • If glucose >250: give insulin alone, no dextrose
    • Effect: 15-30 min, duration 4-6 hours
    • Lowers K+ by 0.5-1.5 mEq/L
    • Check glucose in 1hr (risk hypoglycemia)
  • Albuterol: 10-20mg nebulized (or 0.5mg IV)
    • Effect: 30 min, duration 2-4 hours
    • Lowers K+ by 0.5-1 mEq/L
    • Additive with insulin
  • Sodium Bicarbonate: 50-100 mEq (1-2 amps) IV over 5 min (only if concurrent metabolic acidosis)
    • Controversial efficacy if pH normal

Step 3: Remove K+ from Body (Definitive):

  • Loop Diuretic: Furosemide 40-80mg IV (if adequate renal function)
  • Sodium Polystyrene Sulfonate (Kayexalate): 15-30g PO/PR
    • Effect: Hours, lowers K+ by 0.5-1 mEq/L
    • Avoid if ileus (risk colonic necrosis)
  • Patiromer or Sodium Zirconium: Newer K+ binders, better tolerated but slower onset
  • Hemodialysis: Most effective. Indications:
    • K+ >6.5 with ECG changes refractory to medical management
    • Severe AKI/ESRD
    • Ongoing tissue breakdown (rhabdo, tumor lysis)

Step 4: Stop Exacerbating Factors: Hold ACE-I/ARB, K+-sparing diuretics, NSAIDs, K+ supplements

üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • ECG trumps K+ level: If ECG shows changes, treat aggressively regardless of exact K+ value. ECG changes = cardiac emergency!
  • Calcium first! If ECG changes, give calcium before anything else. Protects heart immediately while you arrange other therapies
  • Insulin hypoglycemia: Very common! Check glucose 30min and 1hr after insulin. Have D50 ready. Consider dextrose infusion
  • Kayexalate controversy: Limited evidence for efficacy, risk of colonic necrosis esp with sorbitol. Consider newer agents
  • Repeat K+ frequently: Check K+ q2-4h during acute management. Temporizing measures wear off!
  • Chronic management: Low K+ diet, loop diuretics, K+ binders. Avoid "hyperkalemia cocktail" (ACE-I + spironolactone + NSAIDs)
‚ö†Ô∏è ESCALATE TO ICU IF:
  • K+ >6.5 with ECG changes
  • Hemodynamically significant arrhythmias
  • Need for emergent dialysis
  • Rapidly rising K+ despite treatment
  • Cardiac arrest from hyperkalemia
  • Requires continuous cardiac monitoring
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • K+ <5.5 mEq/L and downtrending
  • ECG normalized (no peaked T waves)
  • Cause identified and addressed
  • K+-raising medications stopped/adjusted
  • Patient educated on low K+ diet
  • Close follow-up with repeat K+ within 2-3 days
  • If CKD: nephrology follow-up arranged
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

Cardiac: Ventricular arrhythmias (VT, VF), heart block, asystole, sudden cardiac death

    • SIADH (most common), hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency, psychogenic polydipsia
  • Hypervolemic: Edema, elevated JVP, ascites
    • CHF, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome, advanced CKD

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL: Correct slowly! Maximum 8-10 mEq/L in first 24 hours. Faster correction ‚Üí osmotic demyelination syndrome

Acute Symptomatic (<48hr onset with seizures/coma):

  • 3% Hypertonic Saline: 100 mL IV bolus over 10 min, can repeat √ó 2 if needed
  • Goal: Raise Na by 4-6 mEq/L (enough to stop seizures), then slow correction
  • Check Na q2-4h

  • Hypervolemic: Fluid + sodium restriction, diuretics, treat underlying (CHF, cirrhosis)

  • Osmotic demyelination (ODS): From overly rapid correction. Presents 2-6 days later with dysarthria, dysphagia, quadriplegia, locked-in syndrome. PREVENT by slow correction!

  • Rapid changes requiring q2h monitoring
  • Respiratory compromise

13. DIABETIC KETOACIDOSIS (DKA)

Hyperglycemia ‚Ä¢ Acidosis ‚Ä¢ Ketones
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic Triad: (1) Hyperglycemia (>250), (2) Anion gap metabolic acidosis, (3) Ketones

Classic Symptoms: Polyuria, polydipsia, N/V, abdominal pain, Kussmaul respirations (deep, rapid), fruity breath odor, altered mental status

Precipitants (5 I's):

  • Infection: Most common (UTI, pneumonia, etc)
  • Insulin: Non-compliance or inadequate dosing
  • Ischemia: MI, stroke
  • Intoxication: Alcohol, drugs
  • Infant: Pregnancy
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

DKA Criteria:

  • Glucose >250 mg/dL (or known diabetic)
  • pH <7.3 (arterial) or HCO3 <18
  • Anion gap >10
  • Positive serum/urine ketones

Labs: VBG (pH, HCO3), BMP, anion gap, Œ≤-hydroxybutyrate (preferred over urine ketones), phosphate, Mg, CBC, blood cultures, UA/CXR if infection suspected

Calculate: Anion gap = Na - (Cl + HCO3). Normal 8-12. Also calculate osmolality if altered mental status

Severity:

  • Mild: pH 7.25-7.30, HCO3 15-18
  • Moderate: pH 7.0-7.24, HCO3 10-14
  • Severe: pH <7.0, HCO3 <10
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

1. Fluids (MOST IMPORTANT):

  • Initial: NS 1-1.5 L over first hour (15-20 mL/kg/hr)
  • Subsequent:
    • If Na normal/high: 0.45% NS at 250-500 mL/hr
    • If Na low: NS at 250-500 mL/hr
  • Once glucose <250: Switch to D5 0.45% NS to prevent hypoglycemia while continuing insulin
  • Total deficit usually 5-10 L - replace over 24-48 hours

2. Insulin:

  • Regular insulin IV infusion: 0.1 units/kg/hr (usually 5-10 units/hr)
  • Optional bolus: 0.1 units/kg IV if severe DKA
  • Goal: Decrease glucose by 50-75 mg/dL/hr
  • Do NOT stop insulin when glucose reaches 250! Switch to D5-containing fluids and continue insulin until anion gap closes
  • Once anion gap closed: Transition to subQ insulin, overlap by 1-2 hours before stopping gtt

3. Potassium Repletion (CRITICAL):

  • If K+ <3.3: Hold insulin, give K+ 20-30 mEq/hr until K >3.3
  • If K+ 3.3-5.2: Add 20-30 mEq K+ to each liter of fluids
  • If K+ >5.2: No K+ initially, check q2-4h (will drop with insulin)
  • Goal: K+ 4-5 mEq/L

4. Phosphate (if <1.0 mg/dL): K-phos 20-30 mEq/L in fluids

5. Bicarbonate (controversial, only if pH <6.9): 100 mEq in 400 mL H2O + 20 mEq KCl over 2 hours

6. Treat Precipitant: Antibiotics if infection, adjust insulin regimen, etc

üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • DKA resolution: Glucose <200 AND anion gap <12 AND HCO3 >18 AND pH >7.3. Don't stop insulin just because glucose normalized!
  • Two-bag system: Run two IV bags (one NS, one D5-NS) through Y-connector. Adjust ratio to maintain glucose 150-200 while continuing insulin
  • Euglycemic DKA: Can occur with SGLT2 inhibitors. Check ketones even if glucose <250!
  • K+ shifts: Total body K+ depleted despite normal/high initial K+. With insulin, K+ shifts into cells ‚Üí life-threatening hypokalemia. Monitor closely!
  • Cerebral edema: Rare but fatal. More common in kids. From too-rapid correction. Symptoms: Headache, AMS, bradycardia. Give mannitol/3% saline
  • Anion gap: Closes before ketones clear. Don't discharge until ketones clearing and patient eating
‚ö†Ô∏è ESCALATE TO ICU IF:
  • pH <7.0
  • HCO3 <10
  • Altered mental status
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Need for continuous insulin infusion
  • Severe electrolyte derangements
  • Cerebral edema concern
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • DKA resolved (AG <12, HCO3 >18, pH >7.3)
  • Tolerating PO intake
  • Transitioned to subQ insulin
  • Ketones clearing
  • Precipitant identified and treated
  • Patient educated on sick day management
  • Diabetes follow-up arranged
  • No ongoing complications
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

Acute: Cerebral edema (1%), hypokalemia, hypoglycemia, ARDS, thrombosis, mucormycosis

From Treatment: Hyperchloremic non-gap acidosis (from NS), volume overload

14. HYPEROSMOLAR HYPERGLYCEMIC STATE (HHS)

Extreme Hyperglycemia ‚Ä¢ Hyperosmolarity ‚Ä¢ Type 2 DM
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Key Differences from DKA: More extreme hyperglycemia (>600), higher osmolality (>320), NO significant ketoacidosis, more profound dehydration

Classic: Altered mental status, severe dehydration, hypotension. Often insidious onset over days-weeks

Typical Patient: Elderly Type 2 diabetic with infection or medication non-compliance

DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

HHS Criteria:

  • Glucose >600 mg/dL
  • Effective serum osmolality >320 mOsm/kg
  • pH >7.30
  • HCO3 >15
  • Minimal ketones (can have trace)

Calculate Osmolality: 2(Na) + glucose/18 + BUN/2.8. Normal 280-295

Labs: Same as DKA - VBG, BMP, identify precipitant

TREATMENT PROTOCOL

1. Aggressive Fluid Resuscitation (MORE than DKA):

  • Initial: NS 1-1.5 L first hour
  • Subsequent: 250-500 mL/hr based on volume status
  • Total deficit often 8-12 L (more than DKA)
  • Goal: Replace half of deficit in first 12-24 hours

2. Insulin (LOWER rates than DKA):

  • Wait 1-2 hours, give fluids first to avoid vascular collapse
  • Then start insulin 0.05-0.1 units/kg/hr (lower than DKA)
  • Goal: Decrease glucose by 50-75 mg/dL/hr
  • Once glucose <300: Add dextrose to fluids, continue insulin

3. Potassium: Same principles as DKA

4. Correct Slowly: Osmolality should decrease by <3 mOsm/kg/hr to avoid cerebral edema

  • Why no ketosis? Type 2 patients have enough insulin to prevent lipolysis but not enough to control glucose
  • Mortality higher: HHS mortality 10-20% (vs 1-5% for DKA) - sicker, older patients
  • Fluids first! Give 1-2 L NS before starting insulin to prevent cardiovascular collapse
  • Slower correction: Osmolality built up over longer time, correct more slowly to avoid cerebral edema

  • Altered mental status/coma
  • Osmolality >350
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Need for insulin infusion
  • Severe electrolyte abnormalities

  • Glucose <250 and stable
  • Mental status at baseline
  • Osmolality <310
  • Taking PO
  • On appropriate diabetes regimen
  • Precipitant treated

16. CIRRHOSIS WITH ASCITES

  • SBP: Fever, abdominal pain/tenderness, altered mental status without obvious peritonitis
  • Hepatic Hydrothorax: Right-sided pleural effusion (usually) without cardiopulmonary disease
  • Tense Ascites: Respiratory compromise, severe discomfort, umbilical/inguinal hernias
  • Hepatorenal Syndrome: Progressive AKI despite volume resuscitation
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Imaging: Ultrasound (detects >100mL ascites, evaluates liver texture, portal vein), CT abdomen if unclear etiology or concern for HCC

  • Total protein: <1g/dL = high SBP risk
  • Gram stain and culture: Inoculate blood culture bottles at bedside (improves yield)

  • ‚â•1.1 g/dL: Portal hypertension (97% accuracy) - cirrhosis, cardiac ascites, Budd-Chiari syndrome, sinusoidal obstruction syndrome
  • <1.1 g/dL: Non-portal causes - peritoneal carcinomatosis, TB peritonitis, pancreatic ascites, nephrotic syndrome, serositis
  • SAAG unaffected by diuretics or albumin infusions

Sodium Restriction: <2g/day (88 mEq/day). This is THE most important intervention! Effective in 10-20% as monotherapy

  • Initial regimen: Spironolactone 100mg PO daily + Furosemide 40mg PO daily (100:40 ratio)
  • Titration: Increase both medications together every 3-5 days maintaining the 100:40 ratio

    • Step 3: Spironolactone 300mg + Furosemide 120mg

  • Hold diuretics if: Na <120, K >6, Cr rise >2mg/dL above baseline, encephalopathy worsens, severe hypovolemia

  • Indications: Tense/refractory ascites, respiratory compromise, severe discomfort, new-onset ascites for diagnosis
  • Safe to remove 10-12 liters in single session
  • Albumin replacement: 6-8g IV per liter removed if >5 liters removed (prevents post-paracentesis circulatory dysfunction)
  • Can use 20% or 25% albumin (20% is more cost-effective)
  • Complications rare: bleeding <1%, infection <1%, bowel perforation <0.1%

Refractory Ascites:

  • Management options:
    • Serial large volume paracentesis q2-4 weeks + albumin
    • TIPS (transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt) - if appropriate candidate (MELD <18, no encephalopathy)
    • Liver transplant evaluation

  • SAAG is gospel: ‚â•1.1 = portal hypertension with 97% accuracy. Unaffected by diuretics, paracentesis, or albumin

18. HEPATIC ENCEPHALOPATHY

Altered Mental Status in Cirrhosis
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

West Haven Grading:

  • Grade 0 (Minimal): Normal mental status, subtle psychometric abnormalities
  • Grade 1: Trivial confusion, euphoria/anxiety, shortened attention span, impaired addition/subtraction
  • Grade 2: Lethargy, disorientation to time, obvious personality change, inappropriate behavior, asterixis
  • Grade 3: Somnolent but arousable, gross disorientation, bizarre behavior
  • Grade 4: Coma, unresponsive to painful stimuli

Physical Exam Findings:

  • Asterixis: "Flapping tremor" - extend arms with dorsiflexed wrists, observe for irregular flapping. Present in grades 2-3
  • Hyperreflexia: Increased deep tendon reflexes
  • Fetor hepaticus: Sweet, musty breath odor
  • Stigmata of cirrhosis: Jaundice, spider angiomas, palmar erythema, ascites

Alternate Presentations:

  • Acute Change: Sudden worsening in previously stable patient suggests precipitant
  • Post-TIPS: Can develop weeks after TIPS procedure
  • Subclinical: Patient/family report subtle changes not apparent to clinicians
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Diagnosis of Exclusion: Rule out other causes of altered mental status first!

Essential Studies:

  • Labs: CBC, CMP, ammonia level (correlates poorly but >200 suggests HE), venous pH/lactate
  • Infection workup: UA, CXR, diagnostic paracentesis if ascites present (rule out SBP!)
  • Toxicology screen: Especially in acute change
  • Head CT: If focal neurologic signs, trauma, first episode, or atypical presentation

Identify Precipitants (The "5 I's" + more):

  • Infection: SBP (most common), UTI, pneumonia, any infection
  • GI bleed: Protein load from blood
  • Constipation: Increased colonic transit time ‚Üí more ammonia absorption
  • Medications: Benzos, opioids, diuretic-induced hypokalemia/alkalosis
  • Dehydration/Azotemia: From overdiuresis
  • Electrolyte abnormalities: Hyponatremia, hypokalemia
  • TIPS: Increased porto-systemic shunting
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma: Tumor burden
  • Dietary indiscretion: Large protein meal
  • Non-compliance: Stopping lactulose
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

1. Identify and Treat Precipitant (MOST IMPORTANT!):

  • Tap ascites (rule out SBP)
  • Check for GI bleeding
  • Treat infections
  • Review medication list - stop sedatives/opioids
  • Correct electrolyte abnormalities

2. Lactulose (First-line Therapy):

  • Initial dose: 30mL (20g) PO q1-2 hours until first bowel movement
  • Maintenance: 30mL PO TID, titrate to 2-3 soft bowel movements per day
  • Goal: Acidify colon, decrease ammonia production/absorption
  • If cannot take PO: 300mL lactulose in 700mL water as retention enema, hold 30-60 min
  • Watch for: Dehydration, hypernatremia (diarrhea), cramping

3. Rifaximin (Add-on Therapy for Recurrent/Chronic HE):

  • Dose: 550mg PO BID
  • Mechanism: Non-absorbable antibiotic, reduces ammonia-producing gut bacteria
  • Evidence: Reduces HE recurrence by 58% when added to lactulose
  • Cost: Expensive (~$2000/month), often requires prior auth
  • Use for patients with ‚â•2 HE episodes requiring hospitalization

4. Zinc Supplementation:

  • Dose: Zinc sulfate 220mg PO daily
  • Many cirrhotics are zinc deficient
  • Zinc is cofactor for urea cycle enzymes

5. Supportive Care:

  • Airway protection if grade 3-4 (consider intubation)
  • Avoid sedatives (worsen encephalopathy)
  • If agitated: low-dose haloperidol 0.5-2mg (avoid benzos)
  • Nutrition: Don't restrict protein! Give 1.2-1.5 g/kg/day. Protein restriction is OUTDATED

What NOT to Do:

  • Do NOT restrict protein: Outdated practice, worsens malnutrition
  • Do NOT use neomycin: Nephrotoxic, ototoxic. Rifaximin is better
  • Ammonia level: Don't follow serial levels - correlates poorly with clinical status
üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • Always find the precipitant: HE rarely occurs without a trigger. Treat the cause, not just the symptom
  • Ammonia level controversy: Poor correlation with severity. Level >200 supports diagnosis but normal level doesn't exclude HE
  • Protein restriction is WRONG: Old dogma. Cirrhotics need ADEQUATE protein (1.2-1.5g/kg/day) to prevent sarcopenia
  • Vegetable protein better than animal: May be better tolerated but don't eliminate meat entirely
  • Lactulose goals: 2-3 soft BMs/day. Too much = dehydration. Too little = constipation worsens HE
  • Rifaximin not for acute: Add only for chronic/recurrent HE. Takes days to work. Not for initial episodes
  • Post-TIPS HE: Occurs in 30-50%. May need TIPS revision or consider transplant if refractory
  • Asterixis is NOT specific: Also seen in uremia, hypercapnia, other metabolic encephalopathies
‚ö†Ô∏è ESCALATE TO ICU IF:
  • Grade 3-4 encephalopathy (coma)
  • Inability to protect airway
  • Rapidly progressive deterioration
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Severe concurrent illness (sepsis, GI bleed)
  • Need for mechanical ventilation
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • Mental status at baseline (grade 0-1)
  • Precipitant identified and treated
  • Established bowel regimen (2-3 BM/day)
  • On appropriate lactulose dose
  • Rifaximin added if recurrent HE
  • Family educated on HE signs, lactulose use
  • Hepatology follow-up within 1 week
  • Transplant evaluation if not listed
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

Acute: Aspiration pneumonia, falls/trauma, pressure ulcers, malnutrition, inability to take medications

Chronic: Recurrent HE episodes (50% recur within 6 months), cognitive decline, poor quality of life, increased mortality

From Treatment: Dehydration and hypernatremia from excessive lactulose, diarrhea, abdominal cramping

19. ACUTE PANCREATITIS

CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: Epigastric pain radiating to back, nausea/vomiting, anorexia. Pain may be relieved by leaning forward

Physical Exam: Epigastric tenderness, decreased bowel sounds, tachycardia, fever, Cullen sign (periumbilical ecchymosis) or Grey Turner sign (flank ecchymosis) in hemorrhagic pancreatitis

Common Causes (GET SMASHED):

  • Gallstones (40%)
  • Ethanol (30%)
  • Trauma
  • Steroids
  • Mumps/other infections
  • Autoimmune (IgG4)
  • Scorpion/snake bites
  • Hypertriglyceridemia (>1000 mg/dL), Hypercalcemia
  • ERCP
  • Drugs (azathioprine, 6-MP, valproic acid, thiazides, furosemide, sulfonamides, tetracycline)
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Diagnosis Requires 2 of 3:

  • 1. Characteristic abdominal pain
  • 2. Serum lipase >3√ó upper limit normal (more specific than amylase)
  • 3. Imaging findings

Labs:

  • Lipase: More sensitive/specific than amylase. Stays elevated longer
  • CBC (leukocytosis common), CMP (assess calcium, glucose), lipid panel (TG), LFTs (assess biliary etiology)
  • If ALT >150: 95% PPV for gallstone pancreatitis

Imaging:

  • RUQ ultrasound: Look for gallstones, CBD dilation (do in ALL patients)
  • CT abdomen with IV contrast: If diagnosis unclear OR severe disease. Delay 48-72hr if possible (pancreatic necrosis takes time to develop)
  • MRCP: If concern for choledocholithiasis

Severity Scoring:

  • Ranson Criteria: On admission + at 48 hours. Score ‚â•3 = severe
  • BISAP Score: Simpler. 1 point each for: BUN >25, Impaired mental status, SIRS, Age >60, Pleural effusion. Score ‚â•3 = severe
  • APACHE-II: Complex but accurate
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Fluid Resuscitation (MOST IMPORTANT):

  • Aggressive IVF: Lactated Ringer's preferred over NS (reduces SIRS, mortality)
  • Rate: 250-500 mL/hr initially (15-20 mL/kg bolus, then 250-500 mL/hr)
  • Goal: UOP >0.5 mL/kg/hr, HR <120, MAP 65-85
  • Caution: Watch for volume overload in elderly/cardiac disease
  • Early aggressive hydration (within 24hr) reduces organ failure and mortality

Nutrition:

  • Early oral feeding: Start within 24 hours if tolerated (even in severe pancreatitis!)
  • Diet: Low-fat solid diet as tolerated. No need for clear liquids first
  • If cannot tolerate PO: Enteral nutrition via NG/NJ tube preferred over TPN
  • NPO only if: Intractable vomiting, ileus, or altered mental status

Pain Control:

  • IV opioids PRN (morphine, hydromorphone)
  • Myth: Opioids don't worsen pancreatitis via sphincter of Oddi spasm

ERCP (Urgent <24hr):

  • Indications: Cholangitis OR biliary obstruction with bilirubin rising
  • NOT indicated: Mild gallstone pancreatitis without obstruction (just do cholecystectomy later)

Antibiotics:

  • NOT routinely indicated for prophylaxis
  • Give only if: Infected necrosis (proven by aspiration or gas on CT), cholangitis, or extrapancreatic infection
  • If infected necrosis: Carbapenems or fluoroquinolone + metronidazole

Cholecystectomy (for Gallstone Pancreatitis):

  • Timing: During SAME admission once acute inflammation resolves (pancreatic enzymes normalizing)
  • Usually safe after 48-72 hours if improving
  • Recurrence rate 30-60% if not done
üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • LR > NS: Lactated Ringer's reduces SIRS and mortality compared to normal saline
  • Early feeding is safe: Outdated to keep NPO. Start low-fat diet within 24hr
  • No prophylactic antibiotics: Don't reduce infection or mortality. Only treat proven infection
  • CT timing: Necrosis takes 48-72hr to develop. Early CT often non-diagnostic
  • Lipase > Amylase: Lipase more sensitive/specific. Can skip amylase entirely

  • Sterile vs infected necrosis: Infected = fever, leukocytosis, gas on CT. Needs drainage. Sterile necrosis = conservative management

  • Severe pancreatitis (BISAP ‚â•3, Ranson ‚â•3)
  • Organ failure (respiratory, renal, cardiovascular)
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Persistent SIRS >48 hours
  • Pancreatic necrosis with infection
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • Pain controlled on PO medications
  • Tolerating regular diet
  • Lipase trending down
  • No organ dysfunction
  • Cholecystectomy done (if gallstone etiology)
  • Alcohol cessation counseling (if EtOH cause)
  • Follow-up arranged
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

Early: Pancreatic necrosis (10-20%), infected necrosis, ARDS, AKI, shock, hypocalcemia

20. CELLULITIS

Skin/Soft Tissue Infection
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: Acute onset of erythema, warmth, edema, and tenderness in affected area. Usually unilateral lower extremity. Pain typically present. Systemic symptoms: fever, chills, malaise in 40-60% of hospitalized patients

Physical Exam: Ill-defined borders spreading centrifugally (vs erysipelas which has raised sharp borders), no fluctuance (vs abscess), lymphangitic streaking in 15-20%, regional lymphadenopathy. Measure and mark borders with pen. Look for portal of entry (trauma, tinea pedis, fissures)

Common Organisms: Œ≤-hemolytic Streptococcus (Group A Strep, most common in non-purulent), Staphylococcus aureus (especially MRSA if purulent/abscess present)

Risk Factors: Obesity, diabetes mellitus, venous insufficiency, chronic lymphedema, tinea pedis (toe web intertrigo), immunosuppression, prior cellulitis (20-30% recurrence rate), skin barrier disruption

Alternate Presentations:

  • Erysipelas: Superficial cellulitis with sharply demarcated, raised, indurated borders. "Orange peel" appearance. Almost always Group A Strep. Face and legs most common
  • Periorbital Cellulitis: Eyelid edema, erythema WITHOUT vision changes or pain with eye movement (vs orbital cellulitis which needs emergent CT/ENT). Can progress to orbital cellulitis
  • Facial Cellulitis: Consider dental source (odontogenic), sinusitis. Higher risk of cavernous sinus thrombosis if near nasolabial triangle
  • Diabetic Patients: More likely polymicrobial, higher risk of progression to necrotizing infection. Check for gas on imaging
  • Immunocompromised: Consider atypical organisms (fungi, mycobacteria, Pseudomonas). May lack fever/leukocytosis despite severe infection
  • Post-Surgical: If within 48 hours, consider Group A Strep (rapidly progressive, very painful). If days later, S. aureus more likely
  • Water Exposure: Vibrio vulnificus (salt water), Aeromonas (fresh water) - both can cause necrotizing fasciitis. Ask about water exposure!
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Clinical Diagnosis: Based on appearance - culture/biopsy rarely needed for typical cellulitis. Positive predictive value of clinical diagnosis >70% by experienced clinicians

Labs (Obtain if systemic symptoms or severe):

  • CBC: Leukocytosis common (WBC >12k in 50%), left shift. Leukopenia concerning for overwhelming sepsis
  • Blood Cultures: Low yield (<5% positive) but obtain if: fever >38.5¬∞C, hypotension, immunocompromised, suspected bacteremia, failed outpatient therapy
  • ESR/CRP: Elevated but non-specific. Can be useful if diagnosis uncertain or to follow treatment response
  • BMP: If systemic toxicity (assess renal function before vancomycin/aminoglycosides)
  • Lactate: If concern for sepsis or necrotizing infection

Imaging (NOT routinely needed):

  • Ultrasound: Can differentiate cellulitis from abscess. "Cobblestoning" of subcutaneous tissue seen in cellulitis. Can identify fluid collections needing drainage
  • CT with IV contrast: If necrotizing fasciitis suspected (gas in tissues, fascial involvement), deep abscess, osteomyelitis concern
  • MRI: Gold standard for osteomyelitis, deep space infections, necrotizing fasciitis. Use if diagnosis unclear and high clinical suspicion
  • X-ray: Can identify foreign body, subcutaneous gas (necrotizing infection), osteomyelitis (after 2 weeks)

Culture/Biopsy (Consider if):

  • Purulent drainage present ‚Üí Send for culture & sensitivity (guides antibiotic de-escalation)
  • Failed initial therapy
  • Immunocompromised (atypical organisms)
  • Penetrating trauma or water exposure (atypical organisms)
  • Punch biopsy/aspiration: Low yield (20-40% positive), painful, generally not recommended for uncomplicated cellulitis

Mark Borders: Draw around erythema with marker, date/time. Essential for tracking progression/regression. Take photo for documentation

Differential Diagnosis to Consider:

  • Stasis Dermatitis: Bilateral, chronic, hyperpigmentation, varicose veins. Often misdiagnosed as cellulitis
  • DVT: Asymmetric edema but typically no erythema. Get duplex if uncertain
  • Contact Dermatitis: Pruritic, vesicular, bilateral distribution, exposure history
  • Necrotizing Fasciitis: Pain out of proportion, hemorrhagic bullae, crepitus, systemic toxicity, rapid progression
  • Gout/Pseudogout: Acute monoarticular arthritis, usually joint-based
  • Lipodermatosclerosis: Chronic, indurated, inverted champagne bottle appearance of lower leg
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Key Decision: Purulent vs Non-Purulent

  • Non-Purulent: No drainable abscess, no purulent discharge ‚Üí Target Streptococcus, NO MRSA coverage needed
  • Purulent: Abscess, furuncle, carbuncle, purulent drainage ‚Üí MUST cover MRSA

Non-Purulent Cellulitis (No Abscess/Furuncle) - Streptococcal Coverage:

  • Mild (Outpatient):
    • Cephalexin 500mg PO QID √ó 5-10 days (first-line)
    • Dicloxacillin 500mg PO QID √ó 5-10 days
    • Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875mg BID √ó 5-10 days
  • Moderate (Inpatient/Observation):
    • Cefazolin 1-2g IV q8h (excellent Strep/MSSA coverage)
    • Ceftriaxone 1-2g IV q24h (good for once-daily dosing)
  • PCN Allergy (Non-anaphylaxis):
    • Cephalosporins safe if non-IgE mediated reaction (~98% safe)
  • Severe PCN Allergy (Anaphylaxis):
    • Clindamycin 600mg IV q8h OR 300-450mg PO TID (covers Strep/MSSA, not MRSA reliably)
    • Doxycycline 100mg PO/IV BID
    • Levofloxacin 750mg PO/IV daily (avoid if other options available)

  • Immunocompromised: Broader coverage - consider Vancomycin + Cefepime or Vancomycin + Meropenem
  • Water Exposure:

    • PCN allergic: Doxycycline + Metronidazole OR Moxifloxacin

  • Elevation: Keep affected limb elevated above heart level. CRITICAL for reducing edema and speeding recovery
  • Analgesia: NSAIDs (ibuprofen 600mg q6h) or acetaminophen 1g q6h. NSAIDs have anti-inflammatory benefit
  • Treat Underlying Conditions:
    • Tinea pedis: Topical antifungal (terbinafine cream BID √ó 2 weeks)

  • Wound Care: Keep clean and dry. Gentle cleansing with soap and water. No need for topical antibiotics

  • Severe: 10-14 days if slow response, extensive disease, or immunocompromised

21. URINARY TRACT INFECTION / PYELONEPHRITIS

Cystitis ‚Ä¢ Pyelonephritis
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Cystitis (Lower UTI): Acute onset of dysuria, urinary frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain/pressure, hematuria (gross or microscopic). NO fever (Temp <38¬∞C) or flank pain. Symptoms typically develop over hours to 1-2 days

Pyelonephritis (Upper UTI): Fever >38.5¬∞C, rigors, unilateral flank/back pain, costovertebral angle (CVA) tenderness on exam, nausea/vomiting. Often accompanied by dysuria and frequency. Can progress to sepsis

Complicated UTI Definition: Structural/functional abnormality of GU tract, instrumentation, immunosuppression, pregnancy, males, indwelling catheter, recent antimicrobial use, diabetes, renal transplant, hospital-acquired. Higher risk of treatment failure and complications

Alternate Presentations:

  • Elderly Patients: May present with altered mental status, confusion, falls, functional decline, or generalized weakness WITHOUT classic UTI symptoms. Fever often absent or blunted. Dysuria may not be reported. High index of suspicion needed
  • Diabetic Patients: Higher risk of emphysematous pyelonephritis (gas-forming infection - E. coli, Klebsiella), papillary necrosis, perinephric abscess. Worse outcomes, longer treatment needed. May have poor glucose control as only manifestation
  • Pregnancy: 20-30% of untreated asymptomatic bacteriuria progresses to pyelonephritis. Pyelonephritis can trigger preterm labor, low birth weight. Must screen ALL pregnant women and treat asymptomatic bacteriuria. Higher risk of recurrence during pregnancy
  • Men (Any Age): Almost always complicated UTI. Assume prostatic involvement until proven otherwise. Need 7-14 days of treatment. Evaluate for structural abnormalities, BPH, prostatitis. First UTI in young man = red flag for anatomic defect
  • Catheter-Associated (CAUTI): New fever, flank pain, suprapubic pain, altered mental status in catheterized patient. Often polymicrobial. Pyuria alone is NOT diagnostic (50% of catheterized patients have asymptomatic pyuria). Need symptoms to treat
  • Perinephric/Renal Abscess: Persistent fever despite 48-72 hours appropriate antibiotics. Unilateral flank pain, palpable flank mass. Risk factors: diabetes, IV drug use, renal stones, obstruction. CT diagnostic. May need percutaneous or surgical drainage
  • Xanthogranulomatous Pyelonephritis: Rare chronic infection causing granulomatous destruction of kidney. Mimics renal mass on imaging. Presents with persistent flank pain, fever, weight loss. Kidney non-functional, requires nephrectomy
  • Emphysematous Pyelonephritis: Life-threatening gas-forming infection. 90% diabetics. Presents with severe sepsis, CVA tenderness. CT shows gas in renal parenchyma. High mortality (20-40%). Requires IV antibiotics + possible nephrectomy
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Urinalysis (Essential for ALL):

  • Pyuria: >10 WBC/hpf highly suggestive. >5 WBC/hpf = abnormal. Absence of pyuria makes UTI unlikely (95% NPV)
  • Bacteriuria: Any bacteria on unspun urine microscopy = ‚â•10^5 CFU/mL
  • Leukocyte Esterase: Sensitive (75-96%) but less specific. Produced by WBCs. Can be false positive with contamination
  • Nitrites: Highly specific (90-100%) but only 50% sensitive. Many uropathogens (Enterococcus, Staph saprophyticus, Pseudomonas) don't produce nitrites. Requires bacteria in bladder ‚â•4 hours
  • Hematuria: Present in 40-60% of UTIs but non-specific
  • WBC Casts: Pathognomonic for pyelonephritis (indicates renal parenchymal involvement). Differentiates from cystitis

Urine Culture & Sensitivity (When to Send):

  • ALWAYS send before antibiotics if: Pyelonephritis, complicated UTI, pregnant, recent antibiotic use, healthcare-associated, symptoms >7 days, recurrent UTI, immunocompromised
  • NOT needed for: Uncomplicated cystitis in young women (treat empirically)
  • Collection: Mid-stream clean catch (not catheter unless catheter present <2 hours). First morning void best
  • Interpretation:
    • ‚â•10^5 CFU/mL = diagnostic for cystitis
    • ‚â•10^4 CFU/mL = diagnostic for pyelonephritis
    • ‚â•10^2 CFU/mL = diagnostic if catheter specimen
    • Multiple organisms = contamination (repeat culture)
  • Common Organisms: E. coli (75-95%), Klebsiella pneumoniae (5-10%), Proteus mirabilis (5%), Enterococcus (5%), Staphylococcus saprophyticus (5-15% in young women)

Blood Cultures:

  • Obtain if: Pyelonephritis with sepsis/hemodynamic instability, immunocompromised, persistent fever despite 48-72hr of antibiotics, concern for bacteremia
  • Positive in 15-25% of hospitalized pyelonephritis patients
  • Usually same organism as urine culture

Additional Labs:

  • CBC: Leukocytosis common in pyelonephritis (WBC often 12-20k with left shift). Leukopenia suggests severe sepsis
  • BMP: Assess renal function (baseline Cr), check for AKI. Evaluate electrolytes
  • Pregnancy test: ALL women of childbearing age (ectopic can present as flank pain, UTI changes management in pregnancy)
  • Lactate: If sepsis suspected (goal <2, elevated suggests tissue hypoperfusion)

Imaging (NOT routine, but obtain if):

  • CT Abdomen/Pelvis WITH IV Contrast (Test of Choice):
    • No clinical improvement after 48-72 hours of appropriate antibiotics
    • Sepsis/septic shock
    • Immunocompromised (evaluate for abscess)
    • Concern for urinary obstruction (hydronephrosis, stones)
    • Recurrent pyelonephritis (>2 episodes)
    • Men with pyelonephritis (evaluate for anatomic abnormality)
    • Suspected emphysematous pyelonephritis (gas in tissues)
    • Suspected perinephric/renal abscess
  • Renal Ultrasound: First-line if pregnant or contraindication to IV contrast. Can detect hydronephrosis (obstruction) but less sensitive for abscess than CT. Good for assessing kidney size/echogenicity
  • KUB X-ray: Limited utility. Can identify radiopaque stones (calcium) but misses 10-20% of stones

Special Diagnostic Considerations:

  • Asymptomatic Bacteriuria (ASB): Positive culture (‚â•10^5 CFU/mL) WITHOUT symptoms. Very common in elderly, catheterized patients. DO NOT TREAT except in pregnancy or before urologic procedures. Treatment causes antibiotic resistance without benefit
  • Sterile Pyuria: WBCs in urine but negative culture. Differential: TB (send AFB culture √ó 3), fungal infection, interstitial nephritis, appendicitis, recent antibiotic use, urethritis (chlamydia, gonorrhea), kidney stones
  • Catheter-Associated UTI Diagnosis: Catheterized patient with fever (>38¬∞C) OR new onset suprapubic pain OR flank pain OR altered mental status WITHOUT other source PLUS positive culture ‚â•10^3 CFU/mL. Pyuria alone is NOT diagnostic

22. C. DIFFICILE INFECTION (CDI)

Clostridioides difficile ‚Ä¢ Antibiotic-Associated Diarrhea ‚Ä¢ Pseudomembranous Colitis
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: ‚â•3 unformed (loose or watery) stools in 24 hours in setting of recent antibiotic exposure (typically within past 3 months). Crampy abdominal pain, low-grade fever. Diarrhea is NOT bloody in most cases. Leukocytosis often pronounced (WBC 15-30k)

Physical Exam: Diffuse abdominal tenderness (worse in left lower quadrant), abdominal distension, hyperactive bowel sounds. If peritoneal signs present ‚Üí concerning for toxic megacolon or perforation

Risk Factors:

  • Antibiotic exposure: #1 risk factor. Highest risk: clindamycin, fluoroquinolones, cephalosporins, carbapenems. Even single dose can trigger. Usually within 3 months but can be up to 3 years
  • Healthcare exposure: Hospitalization, nursing home, recent admission
  • Advanced age: >65 years (decreased immunity, increased exposure)
  • Medications: PPIs (impair gastric acid barrier), H2 blockers, chemotherapy
  • Immunosuppression: IBD, transplant, malignancy, HIV
  • GI surgery/manipulation: Recent abdominal surgery, NG tube, PEG tube

Alternate Presentations:

  • Severe CDI: WBC ‚â•15k, Cr ‚â•1.5√ó baseline. Presents with high fever (>38.5¬∞C), severe abdominal pain, hypotension. May have minimal diarrhea (ileus develops)
  • Fulminant CDI (2-3% of cases): Hypotension requiring pressors, shock, ileus, megacolon, perforation, multi-organ failure. WBC >35k or <2k (leukemoid reaction or leukopenia both bad signs). High mortality (30-50%)
  • Toxic Megacolon: Abdominal distension, absent bowel sounds, peritoneal signs. Colon >6cm on imaging. Life-threatening - requires surgical evaluation
  • Elderly Patients: May present with only altered mental status, functional decline, or minimal diarrhea. Lower threshold for testing
  • IBD Patients: Difficult to distinguish from IBD flare. C. diff superinfection in 5-10% of IBD hospitalizations. Always test IBD patients with worsening symptoms
  • Community-Acquired CDI: No recent healthcare or antibiotic exposure (15-25% of cases). Often younger, healthier patients. Associated with PPI use, outpatient antibiotics
  • Asymptomatic Carriage: 3-15% of healthy adults, 20-40% of hospitalized patients colonized without symptoms. Do NOT test or treat asymptomatic patients (even if formed stool sent for testing comes back positive)
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

When to Test: ONLY test patients with ‚â•3 unformed stools in 24 hours. Do NOT test formed stools or asymptomatic patients (high false positive rate from colonization)

Diagnostic Testing (Multi-Step Algorithm Preferred):

  • NAAT (Nucleic Acid Amplification Test/PCR):
    • Detects toxin genes (tcdB). Most sensitive test (>95%)
    • Problem: Can't distinguish active infection from colonization
    • If positive ‚Üí need clinical correlation (symptoms + risk factors)
  • EIA for Toxins A/B:
    • Detects actual toxins in stool. High specificity (>95%) but poor sensitivity (60-80%)
    • If positive ‚Üí definite infection (can start treatment)
    • If negative ‚Üí doesn't rule out (may need NAAT or repeat testing)
  • GDH (Glutamate Dehydrogenase) Antigen:
    • Screening test. Sensitive but not specific (detects organism, not toxin)
    • If negative ‚Üí C. diff ruled out
    • If positive ‚Üí need confirmatory toxin testing
  • Recommended 2-Step Algorithm:
    • Step 1: GDH + Toxin EIA
    • If both positive ‚Üí Treat
    • If GDH positive but Toxin negative ‚Üí Send NAAT (if high suspicion, can empirically treat while awaiting)
    • If both negative ‚Üí C. diff excluded
  • Do NOT send repeat testing: If initially negative but high clinical suspicion persists, can repeat once after 24-48 hours. More than 2 tests in 7 days is excessive
  • Test of cure NOT recommended: Patients can shed toxin for weeks. Treat symptoms, not test results

Labs (Assess Severity):

  • CBC: Leukocytosis common (15-30k). WBC >15k = severe. WBC >35k or <2k = very high risk for complications/death
  • BMP: Cr >1.5√ó baseline = severe. Check for AKI, electrolyte abnormalities (hypokalemia from diarrhea)
  • Albumin: Hypoalbuminemia from protein-losing enteropathy
  • Lactate: Elevated if sepsis/shock

Imaging (If Severe or Complications Suspected):

  • CT Abdomen/Pelvis WITH IV Contrast:
    • Indications: Severe CDI, fulminant presentation, concern for megacolon/perforation, ileus, peritoneal signs
    • Findings: Colonic wall thickening (>4mm), pericolonic stranding, "accordion sign" (contrast trapped in thickened colonic folds), ascites, megacolon (>6cm), pneumatosis
    • Helps determine need for surgery
  • KUB X-ray: Can identify megacolon, free air (perforation), but CT preferred if available
  • Colonoscopy: Generally NOT needed. Consider if diagnosis uncertain or refractory to treatment. Pseudomembranes (yellow-white plaques) are pathognomonic but only present in 50% of cases. Risk of perforation - avoid if severe colitis

Severity Classification (Guides Treatment):

  • Non-Severe (Mild-Moderate): WBC <15k, Cr <1.5 mg/dL
  • Severe: WBC ‚â•15k OR Cr ‚â•1.5√ó baseline
  • Fulminant: Hypotension, shock, ileus, megacolon, perforation, ICU admission, WBC >35k or <2k

General Principles:

  • Stop offending antibiotics immediately if medically possible (single most important intervention)
  • Discontinue PPIs/H2 blockers if possible
  • Contact isolation: Private room, gloves and gown for all contact. Soap and water hand hygiene (alcohol doesn't kill spores)
  • Avoid anti-motility agents: Loperamide, opiates can precipitate toxic megacolon. Contraindicated
  • Avoid unnecessary antibiotics: But continue life-saving antibiotics if needed (C. diff treatment will work alongside)

Initial Episode - Non-Severe/Severe:

  • FIRST-LINE: Oral Vancomycin 125mg PO QID √ó 10 days
    • Preferred for both non-severe AND severe CDI (2021 IDSA guidelines changed recommendation)
    • Acts locally in gut (not absorbed systemically)
    • Higher doses (500mg QID) do NOT improve outcomes for non-fulminant disease
  • ALTERNATIVE: Fidaxomicin 200mg PO BID √ó 10 days

    • Lower recurrence rate than vancomycin (15% vs 25%)
    • Very expensive - reserve for: recurrent CDI, high risk for recurrence, immunocompromised
  • If Oral Vanc/Fidaxomicin Unavailable: Metronidazole 500mg PO TID √ó 10 days

    • Only use if vanc/fidaxomicin not available
    • Systemic side effects (neuropathy with prolonged use)

  • Some experts use 500mg QID for severe, but 125mg QID likely equivalent
  • Monitor closely for progression to fulminant

    • Perforation or peritonitis

    • Mental status decline
  • Mortality without surgery: 80% if meet criteria. With surgery: 40-50%

  • Renal Failure: Vancomycin dose unchanged (not absorbed). Metronidazole - monitor for neuropathy
  • Concomitant Antibiotics Needed: Continue C. diff treatment alongside. Stop offending antibiotics if possible, but don't withhold life-saving antibiotics
  • Prevention in High-Risk: Antibiotic stewardship (limit unnecessary antibiotics), avoid PPIs, probiotics NOT recommended for prevention

  • Expect improvement: Within 3-5 days (decreased diarrhea, resolution of fever). If no improvement by day 5-7 ‚Üí re-evaluate

23. DEEP VEIN THROMBOSIS (DVT)

Venous Thromboembolism ‚Ä¢ Lower Extremity ‚Ä¢ Pulmonary Embolism Risk
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: Unilateral leg swelling, pain, warmth, erythema. Calf tenderness, palpable cord. Swelling typically involves entire leg if proximal DVT (iliofemoral), calf only if distal DVT

Physical Exam: Measure calf/thigh circumference (>3cm difference = significant), assess for pitting edema, erythema, warmth. Homan's sign (calf pain with dorsiflexion) is unreliable - do NOT use for diagnosis

Anatomic Classification:

  • Proximal DVT: Popliteal vein or above (femoral, iliofemoral). High PE risk (50% if untreated). ALWAYS requires anticoagulation
  • Distal DVT: Calf veins only (peroneal, tibial, gastrocnemius). Lower PE risk (10%). May monitor without anticoagulation if low risk
  • Upper Extremity DVT: Axillary, subclavian, internal jugular. 10% of DVTs. Often catheter-related

Alternate Presentations:

  • Asymptomatic DVT: 50% of DVTs have minimal symptoms. May present as PE. Screen high-risk hospitalized patients
  • Phlegmasia Alba Dolens: "Milk leg" - pale, swollen leg from extensive iliofemoral DVT with spasm. Painful but pulses intact
  • Phlegmasia Cerulea Dolens: Blue, painful, massively swollen leg from complete venous occlusion. Arterial compromise, gangrene risk. Surgical emergency
  • May-Thurner Syndrome: Left common iliac vein compression by right common iliac artery. Young women with left leg DVT
  • Upper Extremity DVT: Arm swelling, pain. Paget-Schroetter syndrome (effort thrombosis) in athletes. Central line-associated in hospitalized
  • Superficial Thrombophlebitis: Palpable tender cord, erythema along superficial vein. Low PE risk but can extend to deep system. If >5cm or near saphenofemoral junction, consider anticoagulation

Clinical Decision Rule - Wells Score for DVT:

  • Active cancer (treatment within 6 months or palliative): +1
  • Paralysis, paresis, or recent immobilization of lower extremity: +1
  • Recently bedridden >3 days or major surgery within 4 weeks: +1
  • Localized tenderness along deep venous system: +1
  • Entire leg swollen: +1
  • Calf swelling >3cm compared to other leg (10cm below tibial tuberosity): +1
  • Pitting edema (greater in symptomatic leg): +1
  • Collateral superficial veins (non-varicose): +1
  • Alternative diagnosis as likely or more likely than DVT: -2
  • Score interpretation: ‚â§0 = Low probability (5% DVT), 1-2 = Moderate (17%), ‚â•3 = High (53%)

Diagnostic Algorithm:

  • Low Probability (Wells ‚â§0): D-dimer first. If negative ‚Üí DVT excluded (99% NPV). If positive ‚Üí Ultrasound
  • Moderate-High Probability (Wells ‚â•1): Proceed directly to ultrasound (don't wait for D-dimer)

D-Dimer:

  • High sensitivity (95-98%), low specificity (40-50%)
  • Excellent negative predictive value - if negative, DVT/PE ruled out in low-risk patients
  • Many false positives: infection, malignancy, pregnancy, post-op, elderly, hospitalization
  • Age-adjusted D-dimer: Age √ó 10 mcg/L if age >50 (improves specificity in elderly)
  • When NOT to use: High pretest probability, active cancer, recent surgery, pregnancy (always elevated)

Compression Ultrasonography (Test of Choice):

  • Technique: 2-point compression (common femoral vein, popliteal vein). Add full duplex if high suspicion
  • Positive = Non-compressibility of vein (vein doesn't collapse with probe pressure)
  • Sensitivity/Specificity: >95% for proximal DVT, 50-70% for distal (calf) DVT
  • If negative but high suspicion: Repeat ultrasound in 1 week (5-10% of distal DVTs extend proximally)
  • Limitations: Operator-dependent, difficult with edema/obesity, poor for iliac veins and calf veins

Additional Imaging (Special Situations):

  • CT/MR Venography: If ultrasound non-diagnostic and high suspicion. Better for pelvic/IVC thrombus
  • Contrast Venography: Gold standard but invasive, rarely used now. Reserved for cases where diagnosis remains uncertain

Labs (Assess for Complications and Etiology):

  • CBC: Baseline hemoglobin (before anticoagulation), platelet count (baseline for HIT monitoring)
  • PT/INR, aPTT: Baseline coagulation studies
  • BMP, Cr: Renal function (affects anticoagulant dosing)
  • Hypercoagulability Workup: Generally NOT needed for provoked DVT. Consider if: age <50, recurrent VTE, strong family history, unusual sites
    • Factor V Leiden, Prothrombin G20210A mutation, Protein C/S deficiency, Antithrombin deficiency
    • Antiphospholipid antibodies (lupus anticoagulant, anticardiolipin, anti-Œ≤2-glycoprotein)
    • Test AFTER acute phase and OFF anticoagulation (if possible) - results unreliable during acute DVT
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Immediate Anticoagulation (Start Immediately if High Suspicion - Don't Wait for Imaging):

Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs) - FIRST-LINE for Most Patients:

  • Rivaroxaban (Xarelto): 15mg PO BID √ó 21 days, then 20mg PO daily
    • Advantages: Single drug, no LMWH bridge, BID dosing only first 3 weeks
  • Apixaban (Eliquis): 10mg PO BID √ó 7 days, then 5mg PO BID
    • Advantages: Single drug, no LMWH bridge, lowest bleeding risk of DOACs
    • Preferred if high bleeding risk
  • Edoxaban (Savaysa): LMWH √ó 5-10 days FIRST, then Edoxaban 60mg PO daily
    • Requires bridge - less convenient
  • Dabigatran (Pradaxa): LMWH √ó 5-10 days FIRST, then Dabigatran 150mg PO BID
    • Requires bridge - less convenient
    • Advantage: Reversible with idarucizumab (Praxbind)

DOAC Contraindications (Use LMWH/Warfarin Instead):

  • Severe renal impairment (CrCl <30 mL/min for most DOACs, <25 for apixaban)
  • Mechanical heart valves
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome (high recurrence with DOACs - use warfarin)
  • Active malignancy (LMWH preferred - see below)
  • Extremes of body weight (<50kg or >120kg - limited data)

Low Molecular Weight Heparin (LMWH):

  • Enoxaparin (Lovenox): 1mg/kg SC q12h OR 1.5mg/kg SC q24h
    • Bridge to warfarin √ó 5-10 days (until INR 2-3 √ó 2 consecutive days)
    • Or continue as monotherapy (especially cancer patients)
  • Dalteparin: 200 units/kg SC daily (max 18,000 units) √ó 1 month, then 150 units/kg daily
  • Advantages: No monitoring needed, predictable dosing, safe in renal impairment (CrCl >30)
  • Dosing adjustments:
    • Obesity (>150kg): Consider anti-Xa levels
    • Renal impairment (CrCl 15-30): Reduce dose by 50% or switch to UFH

Unfractionated Heparin (UFH):

  • Use when: Severe renal failure (CrCl <15), high bleeding risk (short half-life, reversible), possible need for procedure, massive PE
  • Dosing: 80 units/kg IV bolus, then 18 units/kg/hr infusion
  • Goal aPTT: 1.5-2.5√ó control (60-80 seconds typically)
  • Monitor: aPTT q6h until therapeutic, then daily. Platelet count q2-3 days (HIT risk)

Warfarin (Coumadin):

  • Start 5mg PO daily (overlap with LMWH/UFH √ó 5-10 days)
  • Goal INR: 2-3 (2.5-3.5 for antiphospholipid syndrome)
  • Monitor: INR daily until stable, then weekly, then monthly
  • Advantages: Cheapest option, long track record, reversible with vitamin K/FFP/PCC
  • Disadvantages: Requires monitoring, drug-food interactions, narrow therapeutic window

Special Populations:

  • Cancer-Associated VTE:
    • LMWH preferred over warfarin/DOACs (lower recurrence rate)
    • Enoxaparin 1mg/kg SC BID √ó 3-6 months minimum, often lifelong
    • Alternative: Apixaban or rivaroxaban if LMWH not tolerated (recent trials show non-inferiority)
  • Pregnancy:
    • LMWH throughout pregnancy (enoxaparin 1mg/kg SC q12h)
    • DOACs and warfarin are teratogenic - CONTRAINDICATED
    • Switch to UFH at 36 weeks (shorter half-life for delivery)
    • Resume anticoagulation 6-12 hours postpartum, continue √ó 6 weeks minimum postpartum
  • Obesity: Weight-based LMWH dosing, DOACs have limited data >120kg
  • Elderly: Apixaban preferred (lowest bleeding risk)
  • Renal Failure:
    • CrCl >30: Any anticoagulant
    • CrCl 15-30: Apixaban (dose-adjust), warfarin, or reduced-dose LMWH
    • CrCl <15 or dialysis: UFH or warfarin

Duration of Anticoagulation:

  • Provoked DVT (surgery, immobilization, estrogen, pregnancy): 3 months
  • Unprovoked DVT: 3-6 months, then reassess. Consider indefinite if:
    • Low bleeding risk
    • Proximal DVT or PE
    • Male sex (higher recurrence)
    • Elevated D-dimer 1 month after stopping anticoagulation
  • Recurrent VTE: Lifelong anticoagulation
  • Active cancer: 3-6 months minimum, often indefinite while cancer active
  • Thrombophilia: Duration depends on specific deficiency and circumstances

IVC Filter:

  • Indications:
    • Absolute contraindication to anticoagulation (active bleeding)
    • Recurrent VTE despite therapeutic anticoagulation
    • Complication of anticoagulation (major bleeding)
  • Retrievable filters preferred: Remove once anticoagulation safe (ideally within 3 months)
  • NOT indicated for: Large DVT, free-floating thrombus, prophylaxis
  • Complications: IVC thrombosis, filter migration, perforation

Thrombolysis (Catheter-Directed or Systemic):

  • Consider for: Phlegmasia cerulea dolens (limb-threatening), massive iliofemoral DVT in young patients with low bleeding risk
  • Goal: Reduce post-thrombotic syndrome risk
  • Contraindications: Recent surgery, stroke, bleeding, pregnancy
  • Not routine: Bleeding risk usually outweighs benefit
üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • Homan's sign is useless: Neither sensitive (10-50%) nor specific. Don't use it. Make diagnosis with Wells score + imaging
  • D-dimer rules OUT, doesn't rule IN: Excellent negative predictive value (99%) in low-risk patients. But positive D-dimer doesn't confirm DVT - need imaging. Many false positives
  • Proximal vs distal DVT treatment differs: Proximal DVT (popliteal+) = ALWAYS anticoagulate (high PE risk). Distal (calf only) = can monitor with serial ultrasounds if low risk
  • DOACs are now first-line: Rivaroxaban and apixaban don't require LMWH bridge. Easier, equally effective, possibly safer than warfarin. Use unless contraindicated
  • Cancer = LMWH preferred: LMWH superior to warfarin for cancer-associated VTE (50% lower recurrence). DOACs now reasonable alternative (CARAVAGGIO, ADAM-VTE trials)
  • Don't wait for imaging if high suspicion: Start anticoagulation immediately while awaiting ultrasound. Risk of bleeding from 1 dose < risk of PE extension
  • Unprovoked DVT often needs indefinite anticoagulation: 30% 5-year recurrence risk off anticoagulation. Reassess at 3-6 months. If continuing, use lower dose apixaban/rivaroxaban (reduces bleeding)
  • IVC filter is NOT a substitute for anticoagulation: Filters DON'T treat DVT, just prevent PE. Still need anticoagulation once safe. Filters have complications (IVC thrombosis). Only use if bleeding contraindication
  • Superficial thrombophlebitis can extend to deep system: If >5cm or near saphenofemoral junction, needs anticoagulation (or at least prophylactic dose √ó 45 days). Don't ignore it
  • May-Thurner syndrome: Left leg DVT in young woman = think May-Thurner (left iliac vein compression by right iliac artery). May need endovascular stenting. Right leg DVT more common overall, but left leg in young = red flag
  • Post-thrombotic syndrome is common: 20-50% of DVT patients develop chronic leg swelling, pain, skin changes. Prevented by immediate anticoagulation and compression stockings (30-40 mmHg √ó 2 years)
  • Wells score -2 points for alternative diagnosis: Cellulitis, Baker's cyst, muscle strain can mimic DVT. If alternative equally likely, subtract 2 points from Wells score

  • Phlegmasia cerulea dolens (limb-threatening DVT with arterial compromise)
  • Massive PE with hemodynamic instability
  • Major bleeding complication from anticoagulation
  • Need for thrombolysis (catheter-directed or systemic)
  • Acute limb ischemia requiring urgent intervention
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • DVT confirmed on ultrasound and anticoagulation initiated
  • Hemodynamically stable
  • No signs of PE (no dyspnea, normal O2 sat, no tachycardia)
  • Pain controlled with oral analgesics
  • Patient able to take oral anticoagulants (or self-inject LMWH)
  • No active bleeding or high bleeding risk
  • Patient educated on: anticoagulation adherence, bleeding precautions, leg elevation, compression stockings, return precautions (dyspnea = PE)
  • Appropriate anticoagulant prescribed with clear instructions
  • Follow-up arranged: PCP within 1 week, hematology if unprovoked/recurrent
  • Compression stockings fitted (30-40 mmHg knee-high)
  • If distal DVT being monitored: repeat ultrasound scheduled in 1 week
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

Acute Complications: Pulmonary embolism (most feared - occurs in 50% of untreated proximal DVT), phlegmasia cerulea dolens (complete venous occlusion with arterial compromise, limb-threatening), venous gangrene, acute limb ischemia

Bleeding from Anticoagulation: Major bleeding (intracranial hemorrhage, GI bleed, retroperitoneal bleed, any bleeding requiring transfusion or hospitalization), minor bleeding (epistaxis, easy bruising, hematuria, menorrhagia), HIT (heparin-induced thrombocytopenia - paradoxical thrombosis, check platelets if on heparin >4 days)

Post-Thrombotic Syndrome (20-50% of DVT patients): Chronic leg swelling, pain, heaviness, skin hyperpigmentation, venous stasis dermatitis, venous ulcers (severe cases). Develops months to years after DVT. Prevention: immediate anticoagulation + compression stockings 30-40 mmHg √ó 2 years

Recurrent VTE: 5-10% per year off anticoagulation (unprovoked DVT), 30% cumulative 5-year risk. Higher risk if: unprovoked, proximal, male sex, obesity, residual thrombus. Provoked DVT: 1-3% per year recurrence

From IVC Filter: IVC thrombosis (2-10%), filter migration, IVC perforation, failure to retrieve (50% of retrievable filters never removed), recurrent DVT below filter

24. ACUTE ISCHEMIC STROKE

CVA ‚Ä¢ tPA ‚Ä¢ Mechanical Thrombectomy ‚Ä¢ Time Is Brain
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic: SUDDEN onset focal neurologic deficit. Maximal deficit at onset (not progressive). FAST mnemonic: Face droop (ask to smile), Arm weakness (raise both arms), Speech difficulty (slurred/aphasia), Time = brain (1.9 million neurons die per minute untreated). "Last known well" time is critical for tPA eligibility

Presentations by Vascular Territory:

  • Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA - 50% of strokes): Contralateral hemiparesis/hemianesthesia (face and arm > leg), aphasia (if dominant/left hemisphere), neglect (if non-dominant/right hemisphere), gaze preference toward lesion, homonymous hemianopia
  • Anterior Cerebral Artery (ACA): Contralateral leg weakness > arm, behavioral/personality changes, abulia, urinary incontinence, grasp reflex
  • Posterior Cerebral Artery (PCA): Contralateral homonymous hemianopia, alexia without agraphia, visual agnosia, memory impairment (if bilateral thalamic involvement)
  • Vertebrobasilar (Posterior Circulation): Dizziness, vertigo, diplopia, dysphagia, dysarthria, ataxia, crossed sensory/motor deficits (face one side, body opposite), bilateral symptoms, altered consciousness
  • Lacunar (Small Vessel - 25%): Pure motor hemiparesis, pure sensory stroke, ataxic hemiparesis, dysarthria-clumsy hand syndrome. NO cortical signs (aphasia, neglect, visual field cuts)

Alternate Presentations:

  • Wake-Up Stroke (25% of strokes): Patient wakes with deficits. Last known well = bedtime. Can still be tPA eligible if MRI shows DWI/FLAIR mismatch (DWI positive, FLAIR negative = likely <4.5 hours old)
  • Large Vessel Occlusion (LVO): Severe deficits (NIHSS >6), gaze deviation, dense hemiplegia, global aphasia or severe neglect. These need mechanical thrombectomy urgently - activate stroke team immediately
  • Posterior Circulation Stroke: Often misdiagnosed as "just dizziness" or vertigo. Red flags: sudden vertigo + HINTS exam abnormal (Head Impulse, Nystagmus, Test of Skew), inability to walk, severe headache
  • Basilar Artery Occlusion: Decreased consciousness, quadriplegia, locked-in syndrome, respiratory failure. Devastating if untreated. Needs emergent thrombectomy even beyond 24 hours
  • Stroke Mimics (20-30% of "stroke" activations): Hypoglycemia (<40 mg/dL - CHECK GLUCOSE FIRST), seizure with Todd's paralysis (post-ictal), complicated migraine with aura, conversion disorder, functional neurologic disorder, brain tumor, subdural hematoma
  • Hemorrhagic Stroke: Progressive symptoms (not maximal at onset), severe headache, nausea/vomiting, decreased consciousness. Must rule out with CT before tPA
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Immediate - STAT (Door-to-Needle Goal <60 Minutes):

  • Non-Contrast CT Head: FIRST TEST - rules out hemorrhage (absolute tPA contraindication). Only 60% sensitive for acute ischemia in first 6 hours. Look for: hyperdense MCA sign (visible clot), loss of gray-white differentiation, sulcal effacement, insular ribbon sign. If negative CT but high suspicion ‚Üí still likely ischemic stroke
  • NIHSS Score (National Institutes of Health Stroke Scale): 0=normal, 1-4=minor, 5-15=moderate, 16-20=moderate-severe, >20=severe. Score >6 suggests large vessel occlusion - get CTA immediately. Repeat NIHSS to track improvement/worsening
  • Fingerstick Glucose: MANDATORY FIRST - hypoglycemia (<60) mimics stroke perfectly and is immediately reversible. Never give tPA to hypoglycemia. If <60 ‚Üí give D50, reassess
  • CT Angiography (CTA) Head/Neck: If NIHSS >6 or clinical suspicion for LVO. Identifies: ICA occlusion, M1/M2 MCA occlusion, basilar occlusion. Guides thrombectomy decision. Don't delay tPA waiting for CTA - can do simultaneously

Labs (Send Immediately but Don't Delay tPA):

  • CBC: Platelets must be >100k for tPA. Severe anemia can cause neurologic deficits
  • BMP: Severe hyperglycemia (>400) or renal failure impacts outcomes
  • PT/INR, aPTT: INR must be <1.7 for tPA. Therapeutic heparin (aPTT >40) contraindication
  • Troponin: 10-15% have MI with stroke. Check for concurrent ACS
  • Hemoglobin A1c: Assess chronic glucose control
  • Lipid panel: For secondary prevention

Advanced Imaging:

  • MRI Brain with DWI: Gold standard for acute ischemia (>95% sensitive within hours). DWI bright = acute infarct. ADC dark = restricted diffusion (acute). Use for: unclear timing (wake-up stroke), TIA vs stroke, posterior circulation (CT misses 20% of posterior strokes), suspected mimics
  • MR/CT Perfusion: Identifies salvageable penumbra (ischemic but not dead). Mismatch between perfusion deficit and infarct core = tissue to save. Extends thrombectomy window to 24 hours if large penumbra present
  • Carotid Doppler or CTA Neck: Identify carotid stenosis. >70% symptomatic stenosis = CEA/stenting candidate (must be done within 2 weeks)
  • Echocardiogram: TTE for LV thrombus, EF, wall motion abnormalities. TEE if suspicion for: endocarditis, atrial thrombus, PFO with significant shunt, aortic arch atheroma

Stroke Etiology Workup (TOAST Classification):

  • Large Artery Atherosclerosis: Carotid stenosis >50%, intracranial stenosis
  • Cardioembolism: Atrial fibrillation (most common), mechanical valve, recent MI with LV thrombus, dilated cardiomyopathy (EF <30%), endocarditis, atrial myxoma
  • Small Vessel/Lacunar: Hypertension, diabetes. Small infarcts (<1.5cm) in basal ganglia, thalamus, pons
  • Other Determined Cause: Dissection (carotid/vertebral), vasculitis, hypercoagulable state, drug-related (cocaine, amphetamines)
  • Cryptogenic (30%): No clear cause. Consider: occult atrial fibrillation (30-day monitor), patent foramen ovale (PFO), atrial cardiopathy, cancer-associated

Additional Workup:

  • Telemetry √ó 48 hours minimum: Detect paroxysmal atrial fibrillation (20% of cryptogenic strokes)
  • Extended cardiac monitoring: If no cause found, 30-day event monitor or implantable loop recorder
  • Hypercoagulable workup: Only if young (<50), recurrent, family history, or unusual site. Test AFTER acute phase: Factor V Leiden, prothrombin mutation, protein C/S, antithrombin, lupus anticoagulant, anticardiolipin antibodies
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Time Is Brain - Every Second Counts:

  • Door-to-imaging goal: <25 minutes
  • Door-to-needle (tPA) goal: <60 minutes
  • Door-to-groin (thrombectomy) goal: <90 minutes

Intravenous tPA (Alteplase) - "Clot Buster":

  • Eligibility Window: <4.5 hours from symptom onset (or last known well time)
  • Dose: 0.9 mg/kg IV (maximum 90mg total)
    • Give 10% as bolus over 1 minute
    • Remaining 90% as infusion over 60 minutes
  • Benefit: NNT = 6 to prevent dependency at 3 months. Benefits persist even with symptomatic ICH risk
  • Absolute Contraindications:
    • Intracranial hemorrhage on CT (any amount)
    • Ischemic stroke within 3 months
    • Intracranial/spinal surgery within 3 months
    • Head trauma within 3 months
    • History of intracranial hemorrhage ever
    • GI or GU hemorrhage within 21 days
    • Arterial puncture at non-compressible site within 7 days
    • SBP >185 or DBP >110 despite aggressive treatment
    • Active internal bleeding or acute trauma/fracture
    • INR >1.7, aPTT >40 seconds, platelets <100,000
    • Glucose <50 mg/dL (treat and reassess)
  • Relative Contraindications: Seizure at stroke onset, glucose >400, large infarct on CT (>1/3 MCA territory), minor/rapidly improving symptoms (though recent data supports treating these), pregnancy (relative - can give if severe stroke)

Blood Pressure Management (CRITICAL - Most Common Error):

  • If Giving tPA - Must Lower BP:
    • Goal: <185/110 mmHg BEFORE starting tPA
    • Labetalol 10-20mg IV push q10-20min (up to 300mg total) OR
    • Nicardipine drip: Start 5mg/hr, titrate by 2.5mg/hr q5-15min to goal (max 15mg/hr)
    • AFTER tPA: Keep BP <180/105 √ó 24 hours (prevent hemorrhagic transformation)
  • If NOT Giving tPA - Permissive Hypertension:
    • ALLOW SBP up to 220 mmHg (brain needs perfusion pressure)
    • Only treat if: SBP >220 OR DBP >120 OR signs of end-organ damage (ACS, aortic dissection, acute pulmonary edema)
    • Goal: Lower by 15% over first 24 hours (NOT to normal)
    • Don't reflexively normalize BP - can extend infarct

Mechanical Thrombectomy - "Most Effective Stroke Treatment":

  • Indications: Large vessel occlusion (ICA, M1/M2 MCA, basilar artery) + salvageable tissue
  • Time Window:
    • Up to 6 hours: All LVO eligible
    • 6-16 hours: If good collaterals on CTA or small infarct core
    • 16-24 hours: If perfusion imaging shows large penumbra (mismatch)
    • Basilar occlusion: Consider even beyond 24 hours (devastating if untreated)
  • Benefit: NNT = 3 for functional independence (best intervention in all of medicine)
  • Transfer immediately if LVO identified at non-thrombectomy-capable center
  • Can give tPA AND thrombectomy - not mutually exclusive

Supportive Care:

  • NPO until swallow screen passed: 30-50% have dysphagia. Aspiration pneumonia is leading complication
  • Oxygen: Only if SpO2 <94% (hyperoxia may worsen outcomes)
  • Normothermia: Fever worsens outcomes. Treat aggressively (acetaminophen, cooling)
  • Euglycemia: Avoid hypoglycemia (<70) and severe hyperglycemia (>180). Both worsen outcomes
  • Head of bed: 30 degrees (improves venous drainage, reduces aspiration)
  • DVT prophylaxis: Pneumatic compression boots immediately. If no tPA given ‚Üí heparin 5000 units SQ TID. If tPA given ‚Üí wait 24 hours before starting heparin

Antiplatelet Therapy:

  • If tPA Given: Hold aspirin √ó 24 hours (hemorrhage risk), then ASA 162-325mg daily
  • If NO tPA Given: Aspirin 325mg immediately (or within 48 hours)
  • Minor Stroke (NIHSS ‚â§3) within 24 hours: Dual Antiplatelet Therapy (DAPT)
    • ASA 162-325mg load + Clopidogrel 600mg load on day 1
    • Then ASA 81mg + Clopidogrel 75mg daily √ó 21 days
    • Then ASA 81mg alone lifelong
    • Reduces 90-day stroke recurrence by 30% (CHANCE, POINT trials)

Anticoagulation (For Atrial Fibrillation):

  • Timing is critical - too early = hemorrhagic transformation:
    • Small stroke (NIHSS <8): Start at 3-4 days
    • Moderate stroke (NIHSS 8-15): Start at 6-8 days
    • Large stroke (NIHSS >15): Start at 12-14 days
  • DOACs preferred over warfarin: Apixaban 5mg BID, rivaroxaban 20mg daily, edoxaban 60mg daily, dabigatran 150mg BID
  • Reduce dose if: Age >80, weight <60kg, Cr >1.5 (apixaban 2.5mg BID)

Secondary Prevention:

  • High-Intensity Statin: Atorvastatin 80mg daily regardless of LDL (plaque stabilization, anti-inflammatory). Start immediately
  • Blood Pressure Control: Goal <140/90 (<130/80 if diabetic). Start after acute phase (don't lower acutely unless >220)
  • Carotid Stenosis >70% Symptomatic: CEA or carotid stenting within 2 weeks (stroke risk 20% at 2 weeks without intervention)
  • PFO Closure: Consider if cryptogenic stroke, age <60, large shunt, atrial septal aneurysm. Reduces recurrent stroke by 50%
  • Lifestyle: Smoking cessation (doubles stroke risk), exercise, Mediterranean diet, weight loss

CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Status Epilepticus Definition: Seizure lasting >5 minutes OR recurrent seizures without return to baseline consciousness between episodes. TRUE MEDICAL EMERGENCY - mortality 10-20% if untreated

Types of Seizures:

  • Generalized Tonic-Clonic (Grand Mal): Loss of consciousness, tonic phase (muscle rigidity 10-30 sec), clonic phase (rhythmic jerking 30-60 sec), postictal confusion/somnolence (minutes to hours). May have tongue biting (lateral tongue = specific), incontinence, foaming at mouth
  • Focal (Partial) Seizures: Originates one brain region. Can be motor (Jacksonian march up extremity), sensory, or with impaired awareness (staring, automatisms like lip-smacking). May secondarily generalize
  • Absence: Brief staring spells 5-10 seconds. Child stops mid-activity, stares, resumes. No postictal confusion. Pediatric primarily (petit mal)
  • Myoclonic: Brief muscle jerks. Can be benign (everyone has on falling asleep) or pathologic (juvenile myoclonic epilepsy)
  • Atonic (Drop Attacks): Sudden loss of muscle tone, falls. Lennox-Gastaut syndrome

Non-Convulsive Status Epilepticus (NCSE):

  • Altered mental status WITHOUT obvious motor activity
  • 10-20% of ICU patients with unexplained AMS have NCSE on EEG
  • Subtle findings: eye deviation, nystagmus, facial twitching, automatisms
  • Diagnosis requires EEG - have high suspicion in ICU patients with AMS

Seizure vs Syncope - Key Differences:

  • Seizure: Tonic-clonic >15 seconds, lateral tongue biting (specific), incontinence common, prolonged postictal confusion (30 min - hours)
  • Syncope: Brief myoclonic jerks <15 seconds, no tongue biting (or central only), incontinence rare, rapid recovery (<1 min), no postictal confusion

Etiology - "VITAMINS" Mnemonic:

  • Vascular: Ischemic stroke, hemorrhage, venous sinus thrombosis
  • Infection: Meningitis, encephalitis, brain abscess, neurocysticercosis
  • Trauma: Prior TBI, subdural hematoma, DAI
  • Autoimmune: SLE cerebritis, anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis, Hashimoto's encephalopathy
  • Metabolic: Hypoglycemia <40 (most common reversible cause), hyponatremia <120, hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia, uremia, hepatic encephalopathy
  • Iatrogenic/Intoxication: Tramadol, bupropion, fluoroquinolones, imipenem, isoniazid. Cocaine, amphetamines. WITHDRAWAL: alcohol, benzodiazepines, baclofen
  • Neoplasm: Primary brain tumor, metastases (lung, breast, melanoma, renal)
  • Structural: Remote stroke, prior TBI, congenital malformation, mesial temporal sclerosis

First Seizure Workup Approach:

  • Provoked Seizure: Clear reversible cause (metabolic, infection, drug). Treat cause, may not need chronic AED. Recurrence risk low if cause corrected
  • Unprovoked Seizure: No identifiable acute cause. 40-50% recurrence risk. Consider starting AED after first unprovoked seizure, especially if: abnormal MRI, abnormal EEG, nocturnal seizure, focal seizure
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Immediate Bedside:

  • Fingerstick Glucose: FIRST TEST - hypoglycemia (<40) causes seizures and is immediately reversible with D50. Never miss this
  • Accucheck if on floor: While waiting for labs

Labs (STAT):

  • BMP: Sodium (<120 causes seizures), calcium, magnesium, glucose, BUN/Cr (uremia)
  • CBC: Infection, anemia
  • LFTs: Hepatic encephalopathy
  • Toxicology Screen: Cocaine, amphetamines, PCP
  • Alcohol Level: If suspect withdrawal
  • Anti-Epileptic Drug (AED) Levels: If known epileptic (phenytoin, valproate, carbamazepine). Levetiracetam levels not clinically useful
  • Serum Prolactin: Peaks 10-20 min after seizure, normal by 60 min. >3√ó baseline supports seizure (but normal doesn't rule out). Not routinely needed
  • CK: Rhabdomyolysis common after prolonged seizure (CK >1000 in 50%)

Imaging:

  • Non-Contrast CT Head: FIRST imaging - rules out bleed, mass, stroke. Get immediately if: first seizure, trauma, focal deficits, immunocompromised, persistent AMS
  • MRI Brain: More sensitive for: hippocampal sclerosis, cortical dysplasia, small tumors, encephalitis, posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES). Get within 24-48hr for first seizure

Lumbar Puncture:

  • Indications: Fever, immunocompromised, concern for meningitis/encephalitis, first seizure with no clear cause
  • Get CT first if: Focal deficits, papilledema, immunocompromised (to rule out mass/herniation risk)
  • Send: Cell count, glucose, protein, Gram stain, culture, HSV PCR, consider autoimmune encephalitis panel

EEG (Electroencephalogram):

  • Routine EEG (30 min): All first-time seizure patients. Identifies: epileptiform discharges (spikes, sharp waves), focal slowing (structural lesion), generalized slowing (encephalopathy). Yield increases if done within 24-48 hours
  • Continuous EEG Monitoring:
    • Status epilepticus (confirms cessation after treatment)
    • Unexplained AMS in ICU (r/o non-convulsive status)
    • Burst suppression monitoring (if on pentobarbital/propofol for refractory status)
  • Limitations: Single EEG only captures epileptiform activity in 50% of epileptics. Repeat EEG or prolonged monitoring increases yield to 80-90%
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Active Seizure Management (Non-Status):

  • Position on SIDE (prevent aspiration)
  • Protect from injury (pad head, move objects away)
  • Do NOT restrain
  • Do NOT place anything in mouth (can break teeth, cause aspiration)
  • Supplemental O2 via NC or face mask
  • Establish IV access
  • Most seizures self-terminate in 1-3 minutes
  • If seizure >5 minutes ‚Üí STATUS EPILEPTICUS ‚Üí Start treatment algorithm

STATUS EPILEPTICUS TREATMENT ALGORITHM (Time-Sensitive!):

STAGE 1 (0-5 minutes): First-Line Benzodiazepines

  • Lorazepam (Ativan): 4mg IV over 2 minutes. Repeat once if continued seizure after 5 min (max 8mg)
    • Preferred over diazepam (longer duration of action)
    • Success rate: 60-80%
  • OR Midazolam (Versed): 10mg IM (if no IV access - absorbed well IM)
    • Alternative: 0.2mg/kg IV (typical 10-15mg)
    • Faster onset than lorazepam
    • IM route as effective as IV lorazepam in prehospital setting
  • OR Diazepam (Valium): 10mg IV or 20mg PR (rectal gel - useful prehospital/nursing home)
    • Rapid onset but shorter duration than lorazepam

STAGE 2 (5-20 minutes): Second-Line Anti-Epileptic Drugs (If Benzos Fail)

Choose ONE of the following:

  • Levetiracetam (Keppra): 60 mg/kg IV over 10 min (max 4500mg, typical dose 3000-4500mg)
    • Advantages: No drug interactions, safe profile, no cardiac monitoring needed, preferred by many
    • Disadvantages: May be slightly less effective than fosphenytoin (debated)
    • Side effects: Minimal. Rare psychiatric symptoms
  • Fosphenytoin: 20 PE/kg IV at max rate 150 PE/min (typical 1500-2000 PE, takes 10-15 min)
    • Advantages: Long track record, effective
    • Disadvantages: Cardiac monitoring required (bradycardia, hypotension, arrhythmias), purple glove syndrome, drug interactions (induces CYP450), can't use in heart block
    • Slower infusion if elderly or cardiac disease
    • Check ECG before and during infusion
  • Valproate (Depacon): 40 mg/kg IV over 10 min (max 3000mg)
    • Advantages: Well-tolerated, no cardiac effects
    • Disadvantages: Contraindicated in pregnancy (teratogenic), liver disease, pancreatitis history. Hyperammonemia, thrombocytopenia
  • Phenobarbital: 15 mg/kg IV at 50-100 mg/min
    • Advantages: Effective, cheap
    • Disadvantages: Sedation, respiratory depression (have airway equipment ready), long half-life

STAGE 3 (20-40 minutes): Refractory Status Epilepticus

  • If seizure continues despite 2 first-line agents ‚Üí REFRACTORY status
  • Intubate for airway protection (will need continuous sedation)
  • ICU admission
  • Continuous EEG monitoring
  • Choose continuous infusion:
    • Midazolam: 0.2 mg/kg bolus, then 0.05-2 mg/kg/hr infusion
      • First-line for refractory status (preferred by most)
      • Titrate to seizure cessation on EEG or burst suppression
    • Propofol: 1-2 mg/kg bolus, then 20-200 mcg/kg/min (2-10 mg/kg/hr)
      • Rapid on/off
      • Watch for propofol infusion syndrome (rhabdo, metabolic acidosis, cardiac arrest) if >48hr or high doses
      • Check CK, lactate, triglycerides daily
    • Pentobarbital: 5-15 mg/kg load over 1hr, then 0.5-10 mg/kg/hr
      • Most effective but most side effects
      • Severe hypotension (need pressors), ileus
      • Reserved for super-refractory status
  • Goal: Seizure suppression on EEG. Some aim for burst suppression (flat periods between bursts)
  • Duration: 24-48 hours, then attempt to wean. If seizures recur ‚Üí another 24 hours

Starting Maintenance Anti-Epileptic Drug (After First Unprovoked Seizure):

  • Levetiracetam (Keppra): 500mg BID, increase to 1000-1500mg BID
    • FIRST-LINE for most patients
    • No drug interactions, no monitoring needed
    • Side effects: Irritability, depression (10-15%)
  • Lamotrigine (Lamictal): Start 25mg daily, slow titration
    • Must titrate SLOWLY (increase by 25mg every 2 weeks)
    • Fast titration ‚Üí Stevens-Johnson syndrome risk
    • Good for generalized epilepsy, well-tolerated
    • Final dose: 200-400mg daily (divided BID)
  • Lacosamide (Vimpat): 50mg BID, increase to 200-400mg daily
    • Good for focal seizures
    • Side effects: Dizziness, ataxia, PR interval prolongation (check ECG)
  • Older Options (More Side Effects/Interactions):
    • Phenytoin: Gingival hyperplasia, hirsutism, CYP450 inducer, narrow therapeutic window
    • Carbamazepine: Hyponatremia (SIADH), aplastic anemia (rare), CYP450 inducer
    • Valproate: Weight gain, hair loss, tremor, hepatotoxicity, teratogenic

Special Considerations:

  • Provoked Seizures (Reversible Cause):
    • Alcohol withdrawal: Benzos (lorazepam, diazepam), thiamine
    • Hyponatremia: Correct slowly (risk of osmotic demyelination). If <120 with seizure, can give 100mL 3% saline bolus
    • Hypoglycemia: D50 50mL IV push
    • Hypocalcemia: Calcium gluconate 1-2g IV
    • Isoniazid overdose: Pyridoxine (vitamin B6) gram-for-gram match to INH dose
  • Pregnancy (Eclampsia): Magnesium sulfate 4-6g IV load, then 1-2g/hr. If seizures continue ‚Üí lorazepam

  • Status epilepticus = >5 minutes, not 30: Old definition was 30 min. Now ANY seizure >5 min = status. Start treatment aggressively - every minute increases risk of refractory status and permanent neuronal injury
  • Benzos first, AED second: Don't skip benzos and go straight to fosphenytoin. Benzos stop 60-80% of seizures. Give lorazepam 4mg IV immediately
  • Midazolam IM if no IV access: 10mg IM works as well as IV lorazepam. Don't delay trying to get IV - give IM midazolam immediately
  • Non-convulsive status is common in ICU: 10-20% of unexplained AMS in ICU is NCSE on EEG. Have low threshold for continuous EEG monitoring
  • Levetiracetam is now first-line AED: Safer, fewer interactions than phenytoin. Equally effective for status epilepticus (ESETT trial). Start with keppra for most patients
  • Provoked seizures may not need chronic AED: If clear reversible cause (alcohol withdrawal, severe hyponatremia <120, hypoglycemia <40), treat the cause. May not need lifelong AED
  • Check glucose ALWAYS: Hypoglycemia causes seizures and is immediately reversible with D50. Never miss this - it's the most treatable cause
  • Lateral tongue bite is specific for seizure: Central tongue bite can happen with any loss of consciousness (syncope, head trauma). LATERAL tongue bite is specific for tonic-clonic seizure
  • Propofol infusion syndrome is real: Rhabdomyolysis, metabolic acidosis, cardiac arrest. Risk with >48hr use or high doses (>80 mcg/kg/min). Check CK, lactate, triglycerides daily
  • Don't start multiple AEDs simultaneously: Use monotherapy first. Only add second AED if inadequate seizure control on max tolerated dose of first. Polypharmacy increases side effects and interactions
  • First unprovoked seizure has 40-50% recurrence risk: Consider starting AED, especially if: abnormal MRI, abnormal EEG, nocturnal seizure, patient has high-risk occupation (pilot, driver)
  • Todd's paralysis mimics stroke: Post-ictal focal weakness lasting 24-48 hours. MRI/CT normal. Resolves completely. Don't give tPA for this

COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

From Seizure Activity: Aspiration pneumonia (30-50% if prolonged seizure or decreased consciousness), rhabdomyolysis (CK >1000 in 50% of status epilepticus, can cause AKI), acute kidney injury (from rhabdomyolysis, hypotension), lactic acidosis (from muscle activity), hyperthermia (from muscle activity, increases neuronal injury), posterior shoulder dislocation (tonic phase - anterior more common in most trauma but posterior in seizures), vertebral compression fractures (from violent muscle contractions), oral trauma (tongue biting, broken teeth)

From Status Epilepticus: Permanent neuronal injury if >30 minutes (excitotoxicity from glutamate), cerebral edema, Todd's paralysis (post-ictal focal weakness lasting 24-48hr, mimics stroke), cognitive impairment, development of refractory epilepsy, death (10-20% mortality for status epilepticus)

From Treatment: Respiratory depression (benzodiazepines, barbiturates - have airway equipment ready), hypotension (propofol, phenytoin, barbiturates), propofol infusion syndrome (rhabdo, acidosis, cardiac arrest after >48hr high-dose propofol), cardiac arrhythmias (phenytoin - especially if infused too fast), purple glove syndrome (phenytoin extravasation causing limb ischemia), hepatotoxicity (valproate), thrombocytopenia (valproate), hyponatremia (carbamazepine causing SIADH), Steven-Johnson syndrome (lamotrigine if titrated too fast, carbamazepine)

SUDEP (Sudden Unexpected Death in Epilepsy): 1 in 1000 epilepsy patients per year. Higher risk if: poorly controlled seizures, generalized tonic-clonic, young age, non-adherence to AEDs, nocturnal seizures. Mechanism: likely cardiac arrhythmia or respiratory arrest during seizure

    • Acute blood loss: Obvious source (GI bleed, trauma, surgery, menorrhagia). Hgb may be normal initially
    • Hemolysis: Elevated LDH, low haptoglobin, elevated indirect bilirubin, +/- hemoglobinuria. Smear: schistocytes (TTP, HUS, DIC, mechanical valves), spherocytes (autoimmune, hereditary spherocytosis), sickle cells, bite cells (G6PD). Coombs test for autoimmune hemolytic anemia
  • Low reticulocyte count (<2%) = Inadequate Response:
    • Anemia of chronic disease/inflammation: Ferritin normal/high, low TIBC. Underlying chronic illness (CKD, cancer, infection, autoimmune)
    • CKD: Low EPO production. Cr elevated, usually Hgb 9-11. Start EPO if Hgb <10
    • Bone marrow failure: Pancytopenia (‚ÜìWBC, ‚Üìplatelets, ‚ÜìRBC). Aplastic anemia, myelodysplasia, leukemia, myelofibrosis. Needs bone marrow biopsy
    • Early iron/B12/folate deficiency: May be normocytic before becoming micro/macrocytic. Check iron studies, B12, folate

MACROCYTIC (MCV >100 fL):

  • B12 deficiency: B12 <200 pg/mL (or <300 if high clinical suspicion). Elevated methylmalonic acid and homocysteine (more sensitive). Hypersegmented neutrophils on smear. Causes: pernicious anemia (anti-IF antibodies), malabsorption (Crohn's, celiac, gastric bypass), strict vegan diet, metformin
  • Folate deficiency: Folate <2 ng/mL. Elevated homocysteine but normal methylmalonic acid. No neurologic symptoms (vs B12). Causes: poor diet (alcoholics, elderly), malabsorption, medications (methotrexate, phenytoin, TMP-SMX), pregnancy/lactation (increased demand)
  • Alcohol: Direct marrow suppression. Often with folate deficiency. MCV 100-110. Elevated GGT, AST>ALT
  • Hypothyroidism: Check TSH. MCV usually 100-110
  • Medications: Hydroxyurea, zidovudine (AZT), azathioprine, 5-FU, methotrexate
  • Myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS): Elderly, refractory anemia, dysplastic cells on smear, cytopenias. Bone marrow biopsy shows dysplasia. Risk of transformation to AML
  • Reticulocytosis: Young RBCs are larger. If brisk hemolysis/blood loss with appropriate reticulocyte response, MCV may be elevated

Additional Workup for Specific Situations:

  • GI workup if iron deficiency with no obvious source: EGD + colonoscopy to evaluate for occult GI bleeding (gastritis, ulcer, colon cancer, AVM). Celiac serologies. H. pylori testing
  • Hemolysis workup: Coombs (direct antiglobulin test) for autoimmune hemolytic anemia. G6PD level (if bite cells, acute hemolysis after oxidative stress). Osmotic fragility (hereditary spherocytosis). PNH flow cytometry if intravascular hemolysis
  • Bone marrow biopsy if: Pancytopenia, suspected malignancy (leukemia, lymphoma, myeloma), unexplained anemia, MDS suspected
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Transfusion Thresholds:

  • Stable, no CAD: Transfuse if Hgb <7 g/dL (restrictive strategy)
  • CAD or high cardiac risk: Transfuse if Hgb <8 g/dL
  • Acute MI/ACS: Transfuse if Hgb <10 g/dL (some say <8, controversial - individualize)
  • Symptomatic (dyspnea, angina, orthostasis): Transfuse regardless of Hgb if symptomatic
  • Chronic anemia: Often tolerate lower Hgb (compensatory mechanisms). Treat underlying cause, transfuse only if symptomatic or Hgb <7
  • Each unit pRBC raises Hgb by ~1 g/dL (or Hct by ~3%)

Specific Treatments by Etiology:

Iron Deficiency:

  • Oral iron (first-line): Ferrous sulfate 325mg PO TID (65mg elemental iron per tablet). Take on empty stomach with vitamin C (enhances absorption). Common SE: constipation, dark stools, nausea. Response in 2-4 weeks (‚Üëreticulocytes), full repletion 3-6 months. Continue 3 months after Hgb normalized to replete stores
  • IV iron (if oral intolerance, malabsorption, CKD, need rapid repletion): Iron sucrose 200mg IV √ó 5 doses or Ferric carboxymaltose 750mg IV √ó 2 doses or Iron dextran (test dose required). Risk: anaphylaxis (rare with newer formulations), hypophosphatemia
  • Treat underlying cause: GI bleeding (PPI for ulcer, colonoscopy for polyp/cancer removal), menorrhagia (NSAIDs, OCPs, IUD, ablation)

B12 Deficiency:

  • Cyanocobalamin 1000 mcg IM daily √ó 7 days, then weekly √ó 4 weeks, then monthly lifelong (if pernicious anemia or malabsorption)
  • Oral B12 1000-2000 mcg daily: Alternative if dietary deficiency or mild malabsorption (compliance issue - less reliable)
  • Neurologic symptoms may be irreversible if prolonged deficiency: Treat urgently
  • NEVER give folate without B12 in macrocytic anemia - can worsen B12 neuropathy!

Folate Deficiency:

  • Folic acid 1mg PO daily √ó 1-4 months until replete
  • Treat underlying cause: Alcohol cessation, improve diet
  • Pregnancy: All women of childbearing age should take 400-800 mcg daily (prevent neural tube defects)

Anemia of Chronic Disease:

  • Treat underlying condition (control inflammation, treat cancer, manage CKD)
  • If concurrent iron deficiency (ferritin <100 in setting of inflammation): IV iron (oral poorly absorbed)
  • EPO (erythropoietin): If CKD with Hgb <10, or cancer-related anemia. Epoetin alfa 50-100 units/kg SC 3√ó/week. Target Hgb 10-11 (don't overcorrect - ‚Üëthrombosis risk). Requires adequate iron stores

Hemolytic Anemia:

  • Autoimmune (warm AIHA): Prednisone 1mg/kg daily, taper over weeks-months. If refractory: Rituximab, IVIG, splenectomy. Folic acid 1mg daily (increased RBC turnover)
  • Cold agglutinin disease: Avoid cold exposure. Rituximab. Don't transfuse unless life-threatening (can worsen hemolysis)
  • G6PD deficiency: Avoid oxidative triggers (sulfa drugs, dapsone, antimalarials, fava beans). Supportive care, transfuse if severe
  • TTP: URGENT plasmapheresis (see TTP section)

Thalassemia:

  • Trait (minor): No treatment needed. Avoid unnecessary iron supplementation. Genetic counseling
  • Major: Chronic transfusions (maintain Hgb >9-10), iron chelation (deferoxamine, deferasirox) to prevent iron overload. Splenectomy if hypersplenism. Bone marrow transplant curative
üíé TEACHING PEARLS
  • MCV is your roadmap: Microcytic ‚Üí think iron/thalassemia/chronic disease. Normocytic ‚Üí check retic count (high = bleeding/hemolysis, low = bone marrow problem). Macrocytic ‚Üí B12/folate/alcohol/hypothyroid
  • Ferritin <30 = iron deficiency: Most specific test. But ferritin is acute phase reactant - may be falsely normal/elevated with inflammation. If ferritin 30-100 with chronic disease, still suspect iron deficiency
  • RDW helps distinguish: High RDW = iron deficiency (mixed cell sizes). Normal RDW = thalassemia, chronic disease (uniform small cells)
  • Thalassemia has HIGH RBC count: Despite low Hgb/MCV, RBC count often >5 million (vs iron deficiency with low RBC count). Mentzer index <13 suggests thalassemia
  • B12 deficiency causes neuro symptoms, folate doesn't: Paresthesias, ataxia, dementia = B12. Never give folate without checking B12 first in macrocytic anemia - can worsen neuropathy!
  • Acute blood loss: Hgb lags: Initial Hgb may be normal because both RBCs and plasma lost equally. Takes hours for equilibration. Treat based on clinical status (hypotension, tachycardia)
  • Hemolysis triad: Elevated LDH + low haptoglobin + elevated indirect bilirubin. If all three present, hemolysis confirmed. Then figure out cause (Coombs, smear)
  • Restrictive transfusion saves lives: Transfuse <7 in stable patients (or <8 if CAD). Overtransfusion increases mortality, volume overload, immunosuppression
  • Each unit pRBC raises Hgb ~1 g/dL: Don't transfuse more than needed. Reassess after each unit
  • Iron pills cause constipation/GI upset: Take with food if intolerant (absorption slightly decreased but better than not taking). Try every-other-day dosing (absorption actually better). Black stools are expected
‚ö†Ô∏è ICU TRANSFER CRITERIA
  • Hemodynamic instability (hypotension, tachycardia unresponsive to fluids/transfusion)
  • Active massive hemorrhage requiring massive transfusion protocol
  • Severe symptomatic anemia (Hgb <5-6) with angina, AMS, respiratory distress
  • TTP/HUS with neurologic changes or renal failure
  • Severe autoimmune hemolytic anemia with rapid decompensation
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • Hemodynamically stable
  • Hgb stable or improving
  • No active bleeding
  • Tolerating PO intake (including iron/B12 if applicable)
  • Underlying cause identified and treated (or plan in place)
  • Appropriate outpatient follow-up arranged (PCP, hematology, GI)
  • Patient educated on medications, dietary recommendations
  • Transfusion reactions ruled out if transfused
  • Safe for discharge from functional standpoint
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

Acute Complications: High-output heart failure (severe chronic anemia - Hgb <6-7 with inadequate compensation), angina/MI (demand ischemia from anemia in patient with CAD), syncope, falls (orthostasis, weakness), transfusion reactions (see transfusion section)

From Iron Deficiency: Restless leg syndrome (improves with iron repletion), cognitive impairment (especially children), increased risk infection (impaired immune function), pica complications (lead poisoning from paint chips, bowel obstruction)

From B12 Deficiency: Irreversible neurologic damage (subacute combined degeneration - ataxia, spasticity, paresthesias), dementia, depression, increased homocysteine ‚Üí thrombosis risk

From Treatment: Iron overload (chronic transfusions - hemochromatosis, cardiac/liver damage, requires chelation), transfusion reactions, infection from immunosuppression (rituximab, steroids for AIHA)

28. NEUTROPENIC FEVER

Oncologic Emergency ‚Ä¢ Empiric Antibiotics Within 1 Hour
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Definition: Single oral temperature ‚â•38.3¬∞C (101¬∞F) OR temperature ‚â•38¬∞C (100.4¬∞F) for ‚â•1 hour WITH absolute neutrophil count (ANC) <500 cells/¬µL OR ANC expected to decline to <500 within 48 hours

Calculation: ANC = WBC √ó (% neutrophils + % bands) / 100

Typical Presentation: Fever may be ONLY sign of infection (neutropenic patients lack inflammatory response - no pus, minimal symptoms). Subtle findings: mild hypotension, tachycardia, confusion. Absence of fever does NOT rule out infection

Common Sources (often no clear source found 50-60%):

  • GI tract (most common): Oral mucositis, esophagitis, typhlitis (cecal inflammation), C. diff, perirectal abscess
  • Lungs: Pneumonia (bacterial, fungal - Aspergillus, PCP)
  • Skin/soft tissue: Cellulitis, catheter site infection, perirectal abscess
  • Bloodstream: Catheter-related bloodstream infection (CRBSI - coag-neg Staph, S. aureus)
  • Urinary tract: Less common (neutrophils needed for pyuria - may have negative UA despite UTI)
  • Sinusitis: Especially if prolonged neutropenia (fungal - Mucor, Aspergillus)

Alternate/High-Risk Presentations:

  • Septic shock: Hypotension, tachycardia, AMS, lactate elevation. Requires urgent resuscitation + antibiotics
  • Typhlitis (neutropenic enterocolitis): RLQ pain, diarrhea, distension. CT shows bowel wall thickening cecum/ascending colon. High mortality if perforation. Needs surgical consult
  • Perirectal abscess: Severe perianal pain, tenderness. Often NO fluctuance (neutropenic - can't form pus). Needs exam under anesthesia + drainage + antibiotics
  • Invasive fungal infection (if prolonged neutropenia >7-10 days): Persistent fever despite antibiotics. Aspergillus (pulmonary nodules, halo sign on CT), Candida (bloodstream, hepatosplenic candidiasis), Mucormycosis (sinusitis, black eschar)
  • Atypical pneumonia: PCP (HIV/lymphoma), CMV, viral (RSV, influenza)
  • CNS infection: Headache, AMS, focal deficits. Listeria, Aspergillus, Toxoplasma, Cryptococcus
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Labs (STAT):

  • CBC with differential: Confirm ANC. Trend daily. Platelets (thrombocytopenia common)
  • Blood cultures √ó 2 sets: One from EACH lumen of central line (if present) + peripheral. Label which lumen. Positive in 20-30%
  • CMP: Renal function (for antibiotic dosing), electrolytes, LFTs
  • Lactate: If concern for sepsis
  • UA with culture: Even if UA negative (neutropenia ‚Üí may not have WBCs in urine despite UTI)
  • Stool culture, C. diff if diarrhea
  • Procalcitonin: May help distinguish bacterial vs non-bacterial (not routinely recommended)
  • Fungal markers if prolonged fever (>4-7 days) or high risk: Serum (1‚Üí3)-Œ≤-D-glucan (Candida, Aspergillus, PCP), Galactomannan (Aspergillus), Cryptococcal antigen

Imaging:

  • Chest X-ray: All patients. But CXR may be normal early (neutropenia ‚Üí no infiltrate). If negative but respiratory symptoms, get CT chest
  • CT Chest (if respiratory symptoms, hypoxia, or persistent fever): More sensitive for pneumonia, fungal nodules. Look for: infiltrates, nodules, halo sign (Aspergillus), cavitation
  • CT Abdomen/Pelvis (if abdominal pain or no clear source): Evaluate for typhlitis, abscess, colitis
  • CT Sinuses (if sinus symptoms, especially prolonged neutropenia): Rule out invasive fungal sinusitis

Other Studies:

  • Respiratory viral panel (if respiratory symptoms): Influenza, RSV, COVID
  • Lumbar puncture (if CNS symptoms): After platelets >50k. CSF for cell count, protein, glucose, Gram stain, culture, Crypto Ag, fungal culture
  • Skin biopsy (if skin lesions): Culture and histology. Rule out disseminated fungal (Fusarium, Mucor)

Risk Stratification (MASCC Score - guides inpatient vs outpatient, intensity):

  • MASCC Score ‚â•21: Low risk (mortality <5%). May consider outpatient management in select patients
  • MASCC Score <21: High risk (mortality >20%). Requires inpatient management + aggressive treatment
  • High-risk features: Prolonged neutropenia (>7 days expected), ANC <100, hemodynamic instability, pneumonia, mucositis grade 3-4, neurologic changes, abdominal pain, renal/liver dysfunction, age >60
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

EMPIRIC ANTIBIOTICS (START WITHIN 1 HOUR):

Initial Monotherapy (Preferred):

  • Cefepime 2g IV q8h (antipseudomonal cephalosporin, preferred)
  • OR Piperacillin-Tazobactam 4.5g IV q6h (broader spectrum)
  • OR Meropenem 1g IV q8h (if PCN allergy or resistant organisms suspected)
  • Monotherapy covers: Pseudomonas, E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterobacter, Proteus, Staph (not MRSA), Strep

ADD Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h if:

  • Central line/catheter infection suspected (erythema, tenderness at site)
  • Severe mucositis (oral, esophageal, GI - ‚ÜëStrep viridans, Enterococcus risk)
  • Hemodynamic instability/septic shock
  • Skin/soft tissue infection
  • Known MRSA colonization
  • Pneumonia (cover MRSA)

ADD Antifungal Coverage if:

  • Persistent fever >4-7 days despite broad-spectrum antibiotics (empiric antifungal)
  • Clinical/radiographic evidence of invasive fungal infection: Pulmonary nodules, halo sign, sinusitis
  • Positive fungal markers: Œ≤-D-glucan, galactomannan
  • Options: Micafungin 100mg IV daily (Candida), Voriconazole 6mg/kg q12h √ó 2 then 4mg/kg q12h (Aspergillus), Liposomal Amphotericin B 3-5mg/kg IV daily (broad spectrum, if refractory)

Special Situations:

  • Perirectal abscess/typhlitis: Pip-tazo + Vancomycin (or Meropenem + Vanc). Metronidazole 500mg IV q8h if extensive bowel involvement. Surgical consult
  • PCP suspected (HIV, lymphoma): TMP-SMX 5mg/kg IV q8h. Prednisone if severe (PaO2 <70 or A-a gradient >35)
  • Viral respiratory infection (influenza): Oseltamivir 75mg PO BID √ó 5d if within 48hr symptom onset (or longer if immunocompromised)
  • HSV/VZV (mucositis, vesicular rash): Acyclovir 5-10mg/kg IV q8h

Duration of Antibiotics:

  • If source identified: Treat for appropriate duration (7-14 days typical)
  • If NO source, clinically improving, afebrile >48hr: Continue until ANC >500 (at least 7 days total)
  • If persistent fever but stable: Continue antibiotics until ANC >500 or until fungal workup complete
  • Do NOT stop antibiotics just because fever persists - fever may persist due to tumor, drugs, blood products, non-bacterial causes

Supportive Care:

  • G-CSF (filgrastim 5 mcg/kg SC daily): Consider if high-risk (MASCC <21, pneumonia, septic shock, invasive fungal, prolonged neutropenia expected >7-10 days). Shortens duration neutropenia. NOT needed routinely low-risk patients
  • Fluid resuscitation: NS boluses if hypotensive. Early aggressive hydration improves outcomes
  • Avoid rectal temps/exams, suppositories, enemas: Risk of bacteremia from mucosal trauma
  • Transfusion support: pRBCs if Hgb <7-8, platelets if <10k (or <50k if bleeding/procedure)
  • Oral care: Chlorhexidine or salt/soda rinses QID. Pain control (opiates if severe mucositis)
  • Isolate patient: Private room, hand hygiene, avoid fresh flowers/plants (fungal spores), low-microbial diet controversial
üíé TEACHING PEARLS
  • Door-to-antibiotic <1 hour saves lives: Mortality increases 18% for each hour delay. Don't wait for cultures - empiric antibiotics immediately
  • Fever may be only sign: Neutropenic patients can't mount inflammatory response. No pus, minimal symptoms despite severe infection. Low threshold to treat
  • ANC <100 is very high risk: "Profound neutropenia." Mortality >20%. Need aggressive treatment, GCSF, close monitoring
  • Persistent fever >4-7 days ‚Üí add antifungal: Empiric coverage for invasive fungal (Aspergillus, Candida). Don't wait for positive cultures/markers
  • Don't forget perirectal exam: But be gentle! Perirectal abscess common, may not have fluctuance. Severe tenderness = likely abscess. Needs drainage
  • CXR can be falsely negative: Neutropenia ‚Üí no infiltrate despite pneumonia. If respiratory symptoms, get CT chest even if CXR normal
  • Mucositis = add vancomycin: Oral/GI mucositis ‚Üërisk Strep viridans bacteremia. Must add vancomycin to empiric regimen
  • Typhlitis is surgical emergency: RLQ pain + fever + neutropenia = typhlitis until proven otherwise. CT shows cecal wall thickening. High mortality if perforation. Surgical consult urgently
  • Don't remove central line unless CRBSI confirmed: Lines are precious in chemo patients. Can treat through line unless Staph aureus, Candida, or persistent bacteremia
  • GCSF shortens duration: Use if high-risk (MASCC <21). Shortens neutropenia by ~2-3 days, reduces infection complications. NOT needed routinely
‚ö†Ô∏è ICU TRANSFER CRITERIA
  • Septic shock requiring vasopressors
  • Respiratory failure requiring intubation
  • Multi-organ dysfunction (renal failure, hepatic failure)
  • Hemodynamic instability despite fluid resuscitation
  • Profound neutropenia (ANC <100) with sepsis
  • Typhlitis with concern for perforation
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • Afebrile >24-48 hours off antibiotics (or on PO antibiotics if continued)
  • Hemodynamically stable
  • ANC recovering (>200-500 and rising)
  • No evidence of active infection requiring IV therapy
  • Able to tolerate PO intake
  • Close outpatient follow-up arranged (oncology within 48-72hr)
  • Patient education on infection precautions, when to return
  • Low-risk by MASCC score if outpatient management planned
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

Infectious Complications: Septic shock (mortality 30-50%), invasive fungal infection (Aspergillus pneumonia, disseminated candidiasis - high mortality), typhlitis with perforation (requires surgery, high mortality), perirectal abscess with necrotizing fasciitis, catheter-related bloodstream infection (S. aureus, Candida - may require line removal), PCP pneumonia with respiratory failure

29. ALCOHOL WITHDRAWAL

CIWA-Ar Protocol ‚Ä¢ Delirium Tremens ‚Ä¢ Thiamine BEFORE Glucose
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Timeline of Symptoms:

  • Minor withdrawal (6-12 hours after last drink): Tremor, anxiety, agitation, diaphoresis, nausea/vomiting, headache, insomnia, mild tachycard ia, mild hypertension
  • Withdrawal seizures (12-48 hours): Generalized tonic-clonic, typically brief (<2 min), single or clustered (2-4 seizures), self-limited. If prolonged or focal ‚Üí think other cause (structural lesion, infection)
  • Alcoholic hallucinosis (12-24 hours): Visual, auditory, or tactile hallucinations WITH intact orientation (patient knows hallucinations aren't real). No autonomic instability. Benign, self-limited
  • Delirium Tremens/DTs (48-72 hours, can be up to 7-10 days): Life-threatening. Severe autonomic instability (tachycardia >120, hypertension, hyperthermia >38.5¬∞C, profound diaphoresis), delirium (disoriented, agitated, hallucinations), tremor. Mortality 5-15% if untreated, <1-5% with treatment

Risk Factors for Severe Withdrawal/DTs: History of DTs/severe withdrawal, concurrent illness (pneumonia, pancreatitis, trauma), advanced age, multiple prior detoxifications (kindling phenomenon), prolonged heavy drinking, abrupt cessation, metabolic abnormalities (‚ÜìK, ‚ÜìMg, ‚ÜìPO4)

Alternate/Complicated Presentations:

  • Occult withdrawal in hospitalized patients: Admitted for other reason (trauma, surgery, infection), develops withdrawal 24-72hr into admission. High suspicion if unexplained tachycardia, hypertension, agitation
  • Concurrent Wernicke encephalopathy: Acute thiamine deficiency. Triad: confusion, ataxia, ophthalmoplegia (nystagmus, gaze palsy). Often only 1-2 of triad present. Needs urgent IV thiamine BEFORE glucose
  • Co-ingestion: Benzodiazepines (may mask withdrawal initially then rebound), opioids (can have concurrent withdrawal), stimulants (confound tachycardia/hypertension assessment)
  • Hepatic encephalopathy mimicking DTs: Both have AMS, but HE has asterixis, elevated ammonia, slower onset. Can coexist
  • Seizure complications: Status epilepticus (rare but life-threatening), aspiration pneumonia, traumatic injury from fall
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Clinical Diagnosis: Based on history, timeline, CIWA-Ar score. No specific diagnostic test

CIWA-Ar Score (Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment - Alcohol, revised):

10-item scale, max score 67. Assess q1-2h initially:

  • Nausea/vomiting (0-7)
  • Tremor (0-7)
  • Paroxysmal sweats (0-7)
  • Anxiety (0-7)
  • Agitation (0-7)
  • Tactile disturbances (0-7)
  • Auditory disturbances (0-7)
  • Visual disturbances (0-7)
  • Headache (0-7)
  • Orientation/clouding of sensorium (0-4)

Interpretation: <8 = minimal withdrawal, 8-15 = moderate, >15 = severe, >20 = very severe (high DT risk)

Labs:

  • BMP: Hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, hypophosphatemia common. Anion gap metabolic acidosis (lactic acidosis, ketoacidosis). Renal function
  • CBC: Macrocytosis (MCV >100), thrombocytopenia. Leukocytosis if infection
  • LFTs: Elevated AST>ALT (2:1 ratio), GGT elevated. Assess for cirrhosis, hepatitis
  • Albumin, INR: Synthetic liver function (low albumin, elevated INR = advanced liver disease)
  • Magnesium, phosphate: Correct deficiencies (affect seizure threshold, cardiac arrhythmias)
  • Thiamine level: Usually not sent (takes days, unreliable). Just give empiric thiamine
  • Ethanol level: Helpful to know if still intoxicated vs pure withdrawal. But low/negative ethanol doesn't rule out withdrawal
  • Lipase, troponin if abdominal pain/chest pain: Pancreatitis and ACS common in alcoholics
  • Ammonia if concern for hepatic encephalopathy
  • Blood cultures if fever: Rule out infection (pneumonia, SBP, bacteremia)

Imaging:

  • CT Head (if first seizure, focal seizure, persistent AMS, focal neurologic deficit, head trauma): Rule out ICH, subdural, stroke, mass
  • CXR: Aspiration pneumonia common (especially post-seizure), eval for other pulmonary pathology
  • EEG (if prolonged seizure or concern for non-convulsive status): Withdrawal seizures are brief, self-limited. If prolonged ‚Üí other etiology
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Benzodiazepines (Cornerstone of Treatment):

CIWA-Ar Protocol (Symptom-Triggered):

  • Assess CIWA-Ar q1-2h (or q4h if stable/asleep)
  • If CIWA ‚â•8: Lorazepam 2-4mg IV/PO OR Diazepam 10-20mg IV/PO OR Chlordiazepoxide 50-100mg PO
  • Reassess 1 hour after dose, repeat if CIWA still ‚â•8
  • Goal: CIWA <8, patient calm but arousable
  • IV preferred if severe withdrawal, NPO, vomiting, or poor absorption

Fixed-Dose Protocol (if CIWA not feasible - ICU, intubated):

  • Lorazepam 2mg IV q6h scheduled + 2mg q1h PRN breakthrough
  • OR Diazepam 10mg IV q6h scheduled + 10mg q1h PRN
  • Taper over 3-5 days once stable

Choice of Benzodiazepine:

  • Lorazepam (Ativan): Intermediate-acting. No active metabolites. Safe in liver disease. Predictable IM absorption. PREFERRED if: liver disease, elderly, need IM route
  • Diazepam (Valium): Long-acting, active metabolites (smoother withdrawal, less breakthrough). Preferred by some for DTs. Accumulates in liver disease. PREFERRED if: normal liver function, severe withdrawal/DTs
  • Chlordiazepoxide (Librium): Long-acting, PO only. Good for mild-moderate withdrawal outpatient. NOT for severe/DTs

Delirium Tremens (DTs) - Aggressive Management:

  • ICU admission
  • High-dose benzodiazepines: Lorazepam 2-4mg IV q10-15min or Diazepam 10-20mg IV q10-15min until symptoms controlled. May need 100s of mg/day
  • Phenobarbital (if refractory to benzos): Phenobarbital 130-260mg IV q15-20min (max 10-15 mg/kg loading dose) then 30-60mg IV q6-8h maintenance. Synergistic with benzos, works on different GABA receptor
  • Propofol/dexmedetomidine infusion (if refractory, intubated): Propofol 20-80 mcg/kg/min, Dexmedetomidine 0.2-1.5 mcg/kg/hr. Adjuncts, not replacements for benzos
  • Avoid antipsychotics (haloperidol) as monotherapy: Lower seizure threshold, don't treat withdrawal. Can use low-dose as adjunct for hallucinations if needed (haloperidol 0.5-2mg)
  • Aggressive supportive care: IVF, cooling for hyperthermia, electrolyte repletion

Thiamine & Nutritional Repletion (CRITICAL):

  • Thiamine 100mg IV daily √ó 3-5 days, then 100mg PO daily
  • MUST give thiamine BEFORE glucose (glucose depletes thiamine, can precipitate Wernicke encephalopathy)
  • If concern for Wernicke: Thiamine 500mg IV TID √ó 3 days (higher dose)
  • Folic acid 1mg PO daily
  • Multivitamin daily
  • Magnesium repletion: Mag sulfate 2-4g IV (or Mag oxide 400-800mg PO TID). Recheck and replete until >2 mg/dL. Hypomagnesemia ‚Üìefficacy of benzos, ‚Üëseizure risk
  • Phosphate repletion: K-phos or NaPhos based on K level. Goal >2.5 mg/dL
  • Potassium repletion: 40-80 mEq IV (if <3.5). Recheck q4-6h until >3.5

Seizure Management:

  • Acute seizure: Lorazepam 2-4mg IV (treat seizure + withdrawal). Usually self-limited
  • NO prophylactic AEDs: Benzodiazepines prevent seizures. AEDs (phenytoin, levetiracetam) don't prevent withdrawal seizures
  • If status epilepticus or prolonged seizure: Standard status protocol (lorazepam, then phenytoin/fosphenytoin or levetiracetam loading)
  • Replete magnesium aggressively (low Mg lowers seizure threshold)

Supportive Care:

  • Hydration: NS 100-150 mL/hr (many patients volume depleted). Monitor for volume overload if cirrhosis/cardiomyopathy
  • Cooling for hyperthermia: Ice packs, cooling blanket. Acetaminophen (avoid NSAIDs if cirrhosis/varices)
  • Aspiration precautions: NPO if AMS, HOB elevated, monitor for pneumonia
  • Restraints only if necessary: Can worsen agitation, rhabdomyolysis. Verbal de-escalation, sitter, adequate sedation preferred
  • Treat concurrent conditions: Pancreatitis, pneumonia, GI bleed common
üíé TEACHING PEARLS
  • Thiamine BEFORE glucose: Glucose depletes thiamine stores, can precipitate Wernicke encephalopathy (irreversible). Always give thiamine first in alcoholic patients
  • Withdrawal seizures are 12-48hr, self-limited: If seizure <12hr (still intoxicated?) or >48hr (other cause?), broaden differential. No need for AED prophylaxis - benzos prevent withdrawal seizures
  • DTs peak 48-72hr: Life-threatening. Autonomic storm (tachy, HTN, fever, diaphoresis) + delirium. Needs ICU, massive doses of benzos. Mortality 5-15% if untreated
  • Magnesium is key: Hypomagnesemia lowers seizure threshold, reduces benzo efficacy. Aggressively replete Mg until >2 mg/dL
  • CIWA doesn't work if intubated/ICU: Need fixed-dose protocol. CIWA requires patient cooperation
  • Phenobarbital for refractory DTs: If benzos failing (which is rare), add phenobarbital. Different GABA receptor, synergistic. Works when benzos don't
  • Don't confuse with hepatic encephalopathy: Both have AMS in alcoholic. HE has asterixis, elevated ammonia, responds to lactulose/rifaximin. Can coexist. If unsure, treat both
  • Occult withdrawal in hospital: Patient admitted for trauma/surgery/infection, becomes unexplained tachy/HTN/agitated 24-72hr later. Think withdrawal, check alcohol history
  • Restraints worsen outcomes: Increase agitation, rhabdomyolysis, aspiration. Use adequate sedation instead. Chemical > physical restraint
  • Can't oversedatewith benzos in withdrawal: Respiratory drive preserved until massive doses. Goal is calm but arousable. Don't underdose - prevents DTs
‚ö†Ô∏è ICU TRANSFER CRITERIA
  • Delirium tremens (autonomic instability + delirium)
  • Refractory withdrawal requiring high-dose benzos or phenobarbital
  • Seizures (especially if recurrent or status epilepticus)
  • Severe agitation requiring intubation for airway protection
  • Hemodynamic instability, severe hypertension
  • Concurrent critical illness (sepsis, GI bleed, pancreatitis)
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • CIWA <8-10 for >24 hours
  • Stable vital signs off benzodiazepines
  • No seizure activity
  • Tolerating PO intake
  • Ambulating safely
  • Completed thiamine/vitamin repletion (or on PO regimen)
  • Substance use treatment/counseling initiated or arranged
  • Outpatient follow-up arranged (PCP, addiction medicine)
  • Safe discharge environment

Acute Complications: Delirium tremens (mortality 5-15% if untreated), seizures (aspiration, trauma, status epilepticus), Wernicke encephalopathy (irreversible if not treated - ataxia, confusion, ophthalmoplegia ‚Üí Korsakoff syndrome with permanent memory impairment), aspiration pneumonia, rhabdomyolysis (from agitation, seizures, restraints - can cause AKI)

30. DELIRIUM

Acute Confusional State ‚Ä¢ CAM Criteria ‚Ä¢ Treat Underlying Cause
CLINICAL PRESENTATION & DIAGNOSIS

CAM Criteria (Confusion Assessment Method) - ALL required:

  • (1) Acute onset + fluctuating course: Symptoms developed acutely (hours-days), wax/wane throughout day
  • (2) Inattention: Difficulty focusing, easily distracted, can't follow conversation
  • PLUS EITHER: (3) Disorganized thinking (rambling, illogical) OR (4) Altered level of consciousness (hyperalert, lethargic, stuporous)

Types: Hyperactive (25% - agitated, combative, hallucinations), Hypoactive (25% - lethargic, withdrawn, often missed), Mixed (50%)

Common Causes - "I WATCH DEATH": Infections, Withdrawal, Acute metabolic, Trauma/CNS, Hypoxia, Deficiencies (B12, thiamine), Endocrine, Acute vascular, Toxins/drugs, Heavy metals

High-Risk Medications (Avoid): Anticholinergics (diphenhydramine, TCAs), benzodiazepines (paradoxical), opioids, H2 blockers, corticosteroids, dopamine agonists

WORKUP & TREATMENT

Labs: CBC, CMP, UA/culture, blood cultures if fever, TSH, B12, drug screen, ammonia if liver disease, ABG if hypoxia

Imaging: CT head if focal signs, fall, anticoagulated, new-onset

LP if: Fever + AMS with no clear source (meningitis/encephalitis)

Non-Pharmacologic (FIRST-LINE):

  • Reorient frequently (clocks, calendars, familiar objects)
  • Normalize sleep-wake cycle (lights on during day, dark/quiet at night)
  • Early mobilization (PT/OT daily)
  • Sensory aids (glasses, hearing aids)
  • Avoid restraints (worsen delirium)
  • Family/sitter presence

Pharmacologic (ONLY if severe agitation, danger to self/others):

  • Haloperidol 0.5-2mg IV/PO q4-6h PRN (low-dose). Avoid if QTc >500, Parkinson's, Lewy body dementia
  • Quetiapine 25-50mg PO q12h (if hyperactive, sundowning). Better for elderly
  • Avoid benzodiazepines (except alcohol/benzo withdrawal)
üíé TEACHING PEARLS
  • Delirium vs Dementia: Delirium = acute onset (hours-days), fluctuating, inattention. Dementia = chronic (months-years), stable throughout day, memory loss predominates
  • Hypoactive delirium often missed: Quiet, withdrawn, "pleasantly confused." Worse outcomes than hyperactive. Screen all elderly admissions with CAM
  • Anticholinergics are enemy #1: Diphenhydramine, TCAs, scopolamine, first-gen antihistamines. Avoid in elderly
  • Restraints worsen delirium: Increase agitation, prolong delirium, cause injury. Use sitters, reorientation instead
  • Every delirious patient needs infection workup: UTI, pneumonia, bacteremia most common. Even if afebrile
‚ö†Ô∏è ICU TRANSFER CRITERIA
  • Severe agitation refractory to medications
  • Respiratory compromise requiring intubation for airway protection
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Concern for life-threatening cause (meningitis, severe sepsis)
‚úì DISCHARGE CRITERIA
  • Delirium resolved or significantly improved
  • Underlying cause identified and treated
  • Safe functional status (ambulation, ADLs)
  • Medication reconciliation (d/c deliriogenic meds)
  • Adequate support at discharge (may need rehab/SNF)
COMPLICATIONS

Acute: Falls, aspiration, pressure ulcers, prolonged hospitalization (‚ÜëLOS by 5-10 days), functional decline

Long-term: Persistent cognitive impairment (30-40%), increased mortality (11% at 1 month), institutionalization, progression to dementia

31. HYPERTHYROIDISM / THYROID STORM

Graves ‚Ä¢ Toxic Adenoma ‚Ä¢ Storm = Emergency

Presentation: Weight loss despite ‚Üëappetite, tremor, palpitations/AFib, heat intolerance, diarrhea, anxiety, proximal weakness. Graves: exophthalmos, pretibial myxedema, diffuse goiter

Storm (Life-Threatening): Fever >40¬∞C (104¬∞F), severe tachycardia (>140), AMS, agitation/psychosis, heart failure, vomiting/diarrhea. Burch-Wartofsky score ‚â•45 = storm. Mortality 10-20%

Storm Treatment (Order Matters!):

  1. Œ≤-blocker FIRST: Propranolol 60-80mg PO q4-6h (or 1mg IV q10min) OR Esmolol infusion. Blocks peripheral T4‚ÜíT3 conversion + controls symptoms
  2. Thionamide (1hr after Œ≤-blocker): PTU 600mg PO load then 200mg q6h OR Methimazole 20mg q6h. PTU preferred (blocks T4‚ÜíT3 conversion)
  3. Iodine (1hr AFTER thionamide): SSKI 5 drops PO q6h OR Lugol's solution 10 drops q8h. Blocks thyroid hormone release. Must give after thionamide (prevents iodine from being used for hormone synthesis)
  4. Steroid: Hydrocortisone 100mg IV q8h (blocks T4‚ÜíT3, treats possible adrenal insufficiency)
  5. Supportive: Cooling (acetaminophen, cooling blanket - NOT aspirin, displaces T4 from binding proteins), IVF, treat precipitant

  • PTU in pregnancy 1st trimester: Methimazole causes aplasia cutis. Switch to methimazole 2nd/3rd trimester

32. HYPOTHYROIDISM / MYXEDEMA COMA

Severe Hypothyroidism ‚Ä¢ Rare but Lethal

Chronic Hypothyroidism: Fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, hair loss, dry skin, bradycardia, delayed DTRs, periorbital edema. Labs: TSH elevated (>10), Free T4 low (<0.8)

Treatment Outpatient: Levothyroxine 1.6 mcg/kg/day PO (typical 75-125 mcg daily). Start 25-50 mcg if elderly/>65yo or CAD (risk MI from increased demand). Recheck TSH 6-8 weeks, titrate to normal TSH

Myxedema Coma (Emergency, Mortality 30-60%):

  • Presentation: Profound hypothermia (<95¬∞F), severe bradycardia (<50), hypoventilation (‚ÜìCO2 drive, respiratory failure), hyponatremia, hypoglycemia, AMS‚Üícoma, non-pitting edema, pericardial/pleural effusions
  • Precipitants: Infection, MI, stroke, hypothermia, sedatives/narcotics, surgery
  • Diagnosis: Clinical + TSH elevated (>20), Free T4 very low. Don't wait for labs - treat empirically if high suspicion

  • PLUS T3 (liothyronine) 10 mcg IV q8h: Faster onset (hours vs days). Use both T4+T3

üíé PEARLS
  • Steroid BEFORE thyroid hormone in crisis: Prevents adrenal crisis from increased cortisol metabolism

33. ADRENAL INSUFFICIENCY / CRISIS

Primary (Addison's) ‚Ä¢ Secondary ‚Ä¢ Crisis = Emergency

Chronic AI Presentation: Fatigue, weight loss, hypotension, hyponatremia, hypoglycemia. PRIMARY (Addison's) ALSO has: hyperkalemia, hyperpigmentation (‚ÜëACTH stimulates melanocytes), salt craving. SECONDARY: no hyperK/hyperpigmentation

Adrenal Crisis (Emergency): Shock (refractory hypotension), abdominal pain/vomiting (mimics acute abdomen), fever, severe dehydration, AMS/confusion. Precipitants: Infection, surgery, trauma, stopping steroids abruptly

Diagnosis:

  • Random cortisol: <3 mcg/dL = AI, >18 = excludes, 3-18 = indeterminate (do stim test)
  • Cosyntropin stimulation test: Give cosyntropin 250 mcg IV, check cortisol at 30 and 60 min. Normal: cortisol >18-20. Abnormal: <18 = AI
  • DON'T WAIT for test in crisis - treat empirically! Can do test later
  • Distinguish primary vs secondary: ACTH level. Primary: ACTH >100 (pituitary trying to stimulate failed adrenals). Secondary: ACTH <10 (pituitary failure)

Crisis Treatment (DON'T DELAY):

  • Hydrocortisone 100mg IV bolus immediately, then 50-100mg IV q6-8h (or 200mg/24hr continuous infusion)
  • Aggressive IVF: NS 1-2L rapidly, then 200-300 mL/hr. Patients profoundly volume depleted
  • Dextrose if hypoglycemic: D50 1-2 amps IV
  • Treat precipitant: Antibiotics if infection, stress-dose steroids perioperatively
  • NO fludrocortisone initially: High-dose hydrocortisone has mineralocorticoid activity. Add fludrocortisone once stable on oral therapy

Chronic Replacement (After Crisis):

  • Hydrocortisone 15-25mg/day divided BID-TID (give 2/3 AM, 1/3 afternoon - mimic diurnal rhythm)
  • Fludrocortisone 0.1mg PO daily if PRIMARY (mineralocorticoid replacement). Not needed if secondary
  • Stress dosing: Double-triple dose for illness, surgery. Fever/infection: double dose. Major surgery: hydrocortisone 100mg IV q8h √ó 24-48hr
üíé PEARLS
  • Don't wait to treat crisis: If suspected, give hydrocortisone 100mg IV immediately. Can do cosyntropin test after (use dexamethasone 4mg if need test, doesn't interfere)
  • Hyperkalemia + hyponatremia = PRIMARY: Aldosterone deficiency. Secondary AI has normal K (aldosterone intact)
  • Hyperpigmentation = PRIMARY: ‚ÜëACTH stimulates melanocytes. Look: knuckles, palmar creases, scars, mucous membranes
  • Most common cause chronic AI: Iatrogenic (stopping steroids abruptly after chronic use). Need slow taper

34. HYPERCALCEMIA

"Stones, Bones, Groans, Psychiatric Overtones"

Symptoms: Mild (Ca 10.5-12): Often asymptomatic. Moderate-Severe (Ca >12): Polyuria/polydipsia (nephrogenic DI), constipation, nausea/vomiting, weakness, confusion, lethargy, shortened QT interval. Very severe (>14): AMS, coma, arrhythmias

Causes (90% are these 2):

  • Primary hyperparathyroidism: Outpatient, mild-moderate (Ca 10.5-11.5). PTH elevated or "inappropriately normal" with high Ca. Adenoma usually. Treatment: Parathyroidectomy if symptomatic
  • Malignancy: Inpatient, moderate-severe (Ca >12). PTH suppressed (<20). Mechanisms: PTHrP (squamous cell, renal, breast), osteolytic mets (multiple myeloma, breast), calcitriol production (lymphoma). Treatment: Treat cancer, aggressive hydration, bisphosphonates
  • Other: Granulomatous disease (sarcoid, TB - ‚Üëcalcitriol), meds (thiazides, lithium, vitamin D intoxication), immobilization

Treatment (Severe Hypercalcemia Ca >12-14):

  1. IV Fluids: NS 200-300 mL/hr (goal UOP 100-150 mL/hr). Rehydrate (hypercalcemia causes dehydration from nephrogenic DI). Works in hours, lowers Ca 1-3 mg/dL. FIRST-LINE
  2. Calcitonin 4 units/kg subQ or IM q12h: Rapid onset (4-6hr), modest effect (‚ÜìCa 1-2 mg/dL). Tachyphylaxis in 48-72hr. Use as bridge while waiting for bisphosphonate
  3. Bisphosphonates (MOST effective): Pamidronate 60-90mg IV over 2-4hr OR Zoledronic acid 4mg IV over 15min. Onset 2-4 days, peak 5-7 days, lasts weeks. Lowers Ca 2-3 mg/dL. Dose-adjust if CrCl <30
  4. Dialysis: If refractory, severe renal failure, or Ca >18. Rapidly effective
  5. Avoid thiazides, lithium, vitamin D/calcium supplements

Special Cases:

  • Granulomatous (sarcoid): Prednisone 20-40mg daily (‚Üìcalcitriol production)
  • Lymphoma: Treat malignancy, steroids help
  • Vitamin D intoxication: Stop vitamin D, steroids, fluids, bisphosphonates
üíé PEARLS
  • PTH tells you the cause: PTH elevated/normal = hyperparathyroidism. PTH suppressed = malignancy or other
  • Outpatient vs inpatient: Outpatient mild hypercalcemia = hyperparathyroidism. Inpatient severe = malignancy
  • Calcitonin is bridge: Works fast (hours) but tachyphylaxis (stops working after 2-3 days). Use while waiting for bisphosphonate to kick in
  • Bisphosphonates most effective: But slow (2-4 days). Lasts weeks. Don't give if AKI (wait for rehydration)
  • Furosemide does NOT help: Old teaching. Causes dehydration, worsens. Only if volume overload

35. HYPOMAGNESEMIA

Mg <1.5 ‚Ä¢ Often With HypoK/HypoCa

Causes: GI losses (diarrhea, malabsorption), renal losses (diuretics - loops/thiazides, PPIs, aminoglycosides, cisplatin, amphotericin), alcohol, DKA, refeeding syndrome, chronic PPI use

Symptoms: Often asymptomatic until Mg <1. Tremor, muscle fasciculations, weakness, tetany (Trousseau/Chvostek signs), seizures, arrhythmias (torsades de pointes, afib, PVCs), personality changes. Refractory hypokalemia/hypocalcemia (Mg needed for K/Ca homeostasis)

ECG: Prolonged QT, widened QRS, peaked T waves, torsades de pointes

Treatment:

  • Asymptomatic/Mild (Mg >1): Magnesium oxide 400-800mg PO BID-TID (poorly absorbed, causes diarrhea). OR Mag glycinate/citrate better tolerated
  • Moderate (Mg <1) or Symptomatic: Magnesium sulfate 2g IV over 15-30min, repeat q6h until Mg >1.5. Then transition to PO
  • Severe/Life-Threatening (Torsades, seizure, tetany): Magnesium sulfate 2-4g IV over 10-15min, then continuous infusion 1-2g/hr. Monitor DTRs (loss = hypermagnesemia). Cardiac monitor
  • Replete K and Ca simultaneously: Won't normalize until Mg corrected. Check all three, replete together
  • Monitor serum Mg q6-12h during repletion. Goal >1.5-2 mg/dL
üíé PEARLS
  • Refractory hypokalemia = check Mg: Can't fix K until Mg replete. Mg needed for K channel function
  • Hypocalcemia with Mg deficiency: Mg needed for PTH secretion/action. Replete Mg first, Ca will follow
  • Torsades de pointes: 2g Mg IV over 1-2min (even if Mg normal). First-line treatment
  • Monitor DTRs: Loss of reflexes = hypermagnesemia (>4-5). Stop infusion if DTRs lost
  • PPI-induced hypomagnesemia: Chronic PPI use (months-years) ‚Üí severe hypomagnesemia. Stop PPI, switch to H2 blocker

36. RHABDOMYOLYSIS

CK >1000 ‚Ä¢ Myoglobinuria ‚Ä¢ AKI Risk

Causes - "CRAMPS": Crush injury/trauma, Recreational drugs (cocaine, PCP, MDMA, amphetamines), Alcohol/Anesthetics (malignant hyperthermia, NMS), Meds (statins, fibrates, colchicine), Prolonged immobilization/seizures, Strenuous exercise, Heat stroke, Infections (influenza, HIV)

Classic Triad (only 10% have all 3): Myalgias, weakness, dark urine (tea/cola-colored from myoglobinuria)

Presentation: Muscle pain/tenderness/swelling, weakness, dark urine. May have compartment syndrome (tense compartments, pain out of proportion, 5 P's). Severe: AKI, hyperkalemia, arrhythmias

Labs:

  • CK (creatine kinase): >1000 (often >5000, can be >100,000). Peak 24-36hr, normalize in 3-5 days. Trend daily
  • Urine: Dipstick positive for blood (myoglobin cross-reacts) but NO RBCs on microscopy. Urine myoglobin (rarely sent, not needed)
  • BMP: AKI (Cr rising), hyperkalemia (released from muscle - LIFE-THREATENING), hypocalcemia early (Ca deposits in necrotic muscle), hypercalcemia late (resorption), hyperphosphatemia (released from muscle), elevated anion gap (lactate)
  • ABG: Metabolic acidosis
  • Check: LDH, AST/ALT (elevated from muscle, not liver), uric acid (elevated)

Treatment (Aggressive IVF is Key):

  • IV Fluids: NS 200-300 mL/hr (or 1-1.5 L/hr if severe). Goal: UOP >200-300 mL/hr (flush myoglobin before it precipitates in tubules). Continue until CK downtrending <5000 AND Cr improving
  • Monitor: Strict I/Os, UOP hourly, daily CK/BMP/Ca/Phos, cardiac telemetry (hyperK risk)
  • Alkalinize urine (controversial, not routinely recommended): Some add NaHCO3 to IVF (3 amps in 1L D5W at 200 mL/hr) to maintain urine pH >6.5 (prevents myoglobin precipitation). But no proven benefit, risks volume overload/alkalosis
  • Avoid: NSAIDs, nephrotoxins
  • Hyperkalemia: Treat aggressively (insulin/dextrose, calcium, kayexalate, dialysis if severe)
  • Hypocalcemia (early): DO NOT treat unless symptomatic (seizures, tetany, long QT). Repleting Ca can worsen later hypercalcemia
  • Dialysis if: Severe AKI (oliguric, Cr >5-6), refractory hyperkalemia, severe acidosis, volume overload
  • Compartment syndrome: Ortho/surgery consult URGENTLY. Fasciotomy if pressures >30 mmHg
üíé PEARLS
  • IVF, IVF, IVF: Aggressive volume expansion is treatment. Prevents AKI by flushing myoglobin. Goal UOP >200 mL/hr
  • Hyperkalemia kills: Released from necrotic muscle. Check K q6-12h initially. Cardiac monitor. Treat aggressively
  • Don't treat early hypocalcemia: Ca deposits in muscle. Later, gets resorbed ‚Üí hypercalcemia. Treating early worsens late
  • Dipstick + but no RBCs = myoglobinuria: Myoglobin cross-reacts with hemoglobin on dipstick. Microscopy negative for RBCs
  • Statin-induced rhabdo: Risk factors: Asian, elderly, CKD, drug interactions (gemfibrozil, azoles, macrolides). Check CK before starting if high risk
  • Compartment syndrome: Tense compartment, pain out of proportion, pain with passive stretch. Pressures >30 mmHg. SURGICAL EMERGENCY - fasciotomy

37. TRANSFUSION REACTIONS

Acute Hemolytic ‚Ä¢ TRALI ‚Ä¢ TACO ‚Ä¢ Febrile

Acute Hemolytic (ABO Incompatibility) - WORST:

  • Timing: Within minutes of transfusion
  • Symptoms: Fever, chills, back/chest/flank pain, dyspnea, hypotension, hemoglobinuria (red/dark urine), DIC, AKI. IF INTUBATED: unexplained fever, hypotension, hemoglobinuria, oozing from surgical sites
  • Cause: Clerical error (wrong blood to wrong patient). ABO antibodies lyse donor RBCs
  • Treatment: STOP transfusion immediately! Maintain UOP >100 mL/hr (IVF, diuretics, alkalinize urine). Pressors if shock. Check: Coombs (DAT), LDH, haptoglobin, bilirubin, coags (DIC screen). Send blood bag + patient blood back to blood bank

Febrile Non-Hemolytic (FNHTR) - Most Common:

  • Timing: During or within 4hr of transfusion
  • Symptoms: Fever (>1¬∞C rise or >38¬∞C), chills, rigors. No hemolysis
  • Cause: Recipient antibodies against donor WBCs/platelets, or cytokines released from donor WBCs
  • Treatment: STOP transfusion. Check hemolysis labs (rule out hemolytic). If negative, give acetaminophen, can cautiously resume transfusion slowly. Prevent: Leukoreduced blood for future transfusions, pre-med with acetaminophen

TRALI (Transfusion-Related Acute Lung Injury):

  • Timing: Within 6hr of transfusion
  • Symptoms: Acute hypoxemia, dyspnea, bilateral infiltrates on CXR (looks like ARDS), fever, hypotension. Normal CVP/PCWP (vs TACO)
  • Cause: Donor antibodies against recipient WBCs ‚Üí capillary leak, non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema
  • Treatment: Supportive - O2, may need intubation/mechanical ventilation. Diuretics NOT helpful (not volume overload). Usually resolves 48-96hr

TACO (Transfusion-Associated Circulatory Overload):

  • Timing: During or within 6hr
  • Symptoms: Dyspnea, tachycardia, hypertension, JVD, pulmonary edema, bilateral crackles. Elevated BNP/NT-proBNP
  • Cause: Volume overload (especially in CHF, renal failure, elderly)
  • Treatment: Slow/stop transfusion. Diuretics (furosemide 20-40mg IV). O2, upright position. Prevent: Slow transfusion rate (<1 unit per 2-4hr), furosemide between units if high risk

Allergic:

  • Mild (urticaria): Stop transfusion, antihistamine (diphenhydramine 25-50mg). Can resume if improves. Pre-med with antihistamine for future
  • Severe (anaphylaxis): Angioedema, bronchospasm, hypotension. STOP transfusion. Epinephrine 0.3-0.5mg IM, antihistamines, steroids, IVF, pressors. Washed RBCs for future (removes plasma proteins)

Delayed Hemolytic (Days to Weeks):

  • Timing: 3-10 days post-transfusion
  • Symptoms: Unexplained fever, jaundice, falling Hgb, dark urine. Less severe than acute hemolytic
  • Cause: Anamnestic antibody response (patient previously sensitized, antibody level low ‚Üí transfusion boosts antibody production)
  • Labs: Positive DAT, elevated bilirubin, LDH, low haptoglobin
üíé PEARLS
  • STOP transfusion first: For ANY reaction (except very mild urticaria). Check vitals, send hemolysis labs, notify blood bank
  • Acute hemolytic is clerical error: Wrong blood to wrong patient. Double-check patient ID before transfusion. Devastating complication
  • TRALI vs TACO: Both have pulmonary edema within 6hr. TRALI = hypotension, normal CVP. TACO = hypertension, JVD, elevated BNP. TRALI = supportive, TACO = diuretics
  • Febrile reaction most common: Benign. Rule out hemolysis (check labs), then acetaminophen, resume transfusion. Prevent with leukoreduced blood
  • Pre-med selectively: Acetaminophen if prior febrile reactions. Diphenhydramine if prior allergic. NOT routinely for everyone

38. PLEURAL EFFUSION

Transudate vs Exudate ‚Ä¢ Light's Criteria

Presentation: Dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain, cough. Exam: Decreased breath sounds, dullness to percussion, decreased tactile fremitus

CXR: Blunted costophrenic angle (requires ~200 mL). Lateral decubitus view (layering confirms free-flowing fluid). If loculated, doesn't layer

Ultrasound: More sensitive than CXR (detects 50 mL). Guides thoracentesis

Light's Criteria (Exudate if ‚â•1 of 3):

  • Pleural fluid protein / Serum protein >0.5
  • Pleural fluid LDH / Serum LDH >0.6
  • Pleural fluid LDH >2/3 upper limit of normal serum LDH

Sensitivity 98%, Specificity 80%. Misclassifies ~20% transudates as exudates (especially if on diuretics). If clinically transudate but meets Light's, calculate serum-albumin gradient: Serum albumin - Pleural albumin >1.2 = transudate

Transudate (‚ÜìCapillary Hydrostatic Pressure or ‚ÜìOncotic Pressure):

  • CHF (most common): Bilateral, symmetric. Treat CHF (diuretics). Thoracentesis only if asymmetric, fever, or not responding to diuresis
  • Cirrhosis/Hepatic hydrothorax: Usually right-sided. Treat ascites (diuretics, TIPS if refractory)
  • Nephrotic syndrome: Hypoalbuminemia (<2.5), proteinuria >3.5 g/day
  • PE: Can be transudate OR exudate. If high suspicion, get CTPA

Exudate (Inflammation, Infection, Malignancy):

  • Pneumonia/Parapneumonic: Most common exudate. Thoracentesis if fluid >10mm on lateral decubitus or loculated. Simple parapneumonic: pH >7.2, glucose >60, LDH <1000, negative Gram/culture ‚Üí antibiotics alone. Complicated: pH <7.2, glucose <60, LDH >1000, or positive Gram/culture ‚Üí chest tube drainage
  • Empyema: Pus in pleural space. Frank pus, positive Gram stain/culture, pH <7.2, glucose <40. Needs chest tube + antibiotics +/- VATS/surgery
  • Malignancy: Lung cancer, breast, lymphoma, ovarian. Often bloody, exudative. Cytology positive in 60%. Thoracentesis for diagnosis + symptom relief. Pleurodesis if recurrent
  • TB: Lymphocytic predominance (>50%), elevated ADA (>40 U/L), positive AFB culture (50-80% if also send pleural biopsy). Pleural biopsy for culture + histology (granulomas)
  • PE: 1/3 transudates, 2/3 exudates. Often bloody. If suspected, get CTPA
  • Pancreatitis: Left-sided, elevated pleural fluid amylase (>serum)

Send Pleural Fluid for:

  • Always: pH, protein, LDH, cell count/diff, Gram stain, culture (aerobic/anaerobic)
  • If concern for malignancy: Cytology (3 samples ‚Üëyield)
  • If suspect TB: AFB smear/culture, ADA, pleural biopsy
  • If bloody: Hematocrit (Hct >50% of serum Hct = hemothorax)
  • If concern for pancreatitis, esophageal rupture: Amylase
  • If milky appearance: Triglycerides (>110 = chylothorax)

Treatment:

  • Treat underlying cause
  • Therapeutic thoracentesis: If symptomatic (dyspnea). Remove 1-1.5L max at a time (risk re-expansion pulmonary edema if >1.5L). Repeatthorax as needed
  • Chest tube: If empyema, complicated parapneumonic, hemothorax
  • Pleurodesis: If recurrent malignant effusion (talc, doxycycline, bleomycin). Prevents reaccumulation

  • Light's Criteria rules in exudate: Sens 98%, but 20% false positives (transudate called exudate). Use serum-albumin gradient if doesn't fit clinically
  • CHF effusions are bilateral/symmetric: If unilateral or asymmetric, tap it (could be empyema, malignancy, PE)

39. PNEUMOTHORAX

Primary ‚Ä¢ Secondary ‚Ä¢ Iatrogenic ‚Ä¢ TENSION = Emergency

Types:

  • Primary Spontaneous (PSP): Tall, thin, young men (15-35yo). Smokers. Rupture apical bleb/bullae. No underlying lung disease
  • Secondary Spontaneous (SSP): Underlying lung disease - COPD (most common), CF, PCP pneumonia, necrotizing pneumonia, TB, Marfan's
  • Iatrogenic: Central line placement (subclavian>IJ), thoracentesis, transbronchial biopsy, mechanical ventilation (barotrauma), CPR
  • Traumatic: Blunt/penetrating chest trauma, rib fractures

Presentation: Acute-onset pleuritic chest pain, dyspnea. Exam: Decreased breath sounds, hyperresonance to percussion, decreased tactile fremitus on affected side. Tracheal deviation toward affected side (simple PTX) or AWAY (tension PTX)

TENSION PNEUMOTHORAX - EMERGENCY:

  • Pathophys: One-way valve ‚Üí air enters pleural space but can't escape ‚Üí ‚Üëpressure collapses lung, shifts mediastinum, compresses contralateral lung + great vessels ‚Üí ‚Üìvenous return ‚Üí shock
  • Presentation: Severe respiratory distress, hypotension, tachycardia, JVD, tracheal deviation AWAY from affected side, absent breath sounds, hyperresonance, cyanosis
  • CLINICAL DIAGNOSIS - DON'T WAIT FOR CXR!
  • Treatment: Immediate needle decompression: 14-16 gauge angiocath, 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line (or 4th-5th ICS anterior axillary line). Insert needle over rib (avoid neurovascular bundle under rib). Rush of air = confirms diagnosis. THEN get CXR and place chest tube

CXR Findings: Visceral pleural line (edge of collapsed lung), absent lung markings peripherally. Upright film more sensitive. Deep sulcus sign on supine (air collects anteriorly). Quantify size: Measure distance from chest wall to lung edge at hilum level. Small <2cm, Large >2cm

Treatment Algorithm (Non-Tension):

  • Small (<2cm or <20%), stable, minimal symptoms:
    • Observation + supplemental O2 (hastens resorption - 4√ó faster with O2)
    • Repeat CXR in 6hr. If stable/improving, discharge with outpatient follow-up CXR 1-2 weeks
    • PSP: Resorbs ~1-2% per day (10-14 days). SSP: Higher risk progression, lower threshold to place tube
    • Instruct: Return if increased dyspnea, chest pain. No flying until resolved (pressure changes expand PTX)
  • Large (>2cm or >20%) OR symptomatic:
    • Aspiration (PSP, first episode, age <50, stable): 16-18 gauge IV catheter, 2nd ICS midclavicular line or 4th-5th ICS midaxillary line. Aspirate with 60 mL syringe + 3-way stopcock. Max 2.5L (if not successful, place chest tube). If successful (PTX <2cm, symptoms improved), observe 4hr, repeat CXR. Discharge if stable. Success ~60-80% PSP
    • Chest tube (SSP, large PSP failing aspiration, recurrent, bilateral, hemodynamic instability): 16-28Fr pigtail (small) or 28-32Fr tube (large). 4th-5th ICS, anterior axillary line, directed posteroapically. Connect to water seal +/- suction (-20 cm H2O). Keep tube until no air leak √ó 24hr + lung re-expanded on CXR
  • Recurrent PTX (20-50% recur within 2 years): Video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery (VATS) with bleb resection + pleurodesis. Reduces recurrence to <5%. Consider after 1st SSP or 2nd PSP

Special Situations:

  • Mechanical ventilation: If PTX develops, place chest tube immediately (positive pressure worsens). Don't wait/observe
  • Bilateral PTX: Higher risk respiratory compromise. Usually place bilateral tubes
  • Persistent air leak (tube in, still bubbling >7 days): Bronchopleural fistula. Consult thoracic surgery
üíé TEACHING PEARLS
  • Tension PTX is clinical diagnosis: Don't wait for CXR! Respiratory distress + hypotension + absent breath sounds + hyperresonance = needle decompression NOW
  • Tracheal deviation: Simple PTX ‚Üí toward affected side (volume loss). Tension PTX ‚Üí away from affected side (mass effect pushing mediastinum)
  • PSP = tall thin young men: "Walking around with time bombs." Apical blebs. Smoking ‚Üërisk 20√ó. Often rupture at rest. Recurrence 20-50%
  • SSP more dangerous than PSP: Underlying lung disease ‚Üí less reserve. Lower threshold to place chest tube. Higher morbidity/mortality
  • O2 speeds resolution 4√ó: Creates nitrogen gradient ‚Üí resorption. Give O2 even if saturating well
  • No flying until resolved: Pressure changes at altitude expand PTX. Wait 1-2 weeks post-resolution before flying
  • Aspiration vs tube: PSP, young, stable ‚Üí try aspiration first (60-80% success, less invasive). SSP ‚Üí chest tube (higher failure rate, don't waste time)
  • Recurrent PTX needs surgery: VATS pleurodesis after 1st SSP or 2nd PSP. Reduces recurrence <5%

40. SYNCOPE

Vasovagal ‚Ä¢ Cardiac ‚Ä¢ Orthostatic ‚Ä¢ Neurologic
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Definition: Transient loss of consciousness (LOC) with loss of postural tone, followed by spontaneous complete recovery. Caused by global cerebral hypoperfusion. Duration typically <20 seconds

Classic: Sudden LOC, fall, rapid spontaneous recovery without intervention. Patient recalls events before and after (vs seizure with postictal confusion)

Key Historical Questions:

  • Prodrome: Nausea, diaphoresis, tunnel vision, lightheadedness = vasovagal. No warning = cardiac
  • Position: Standing ‚Üí orthostatic or vasovagal. Supine ‚Üí cardiac (very concerning!)
  • Activity: Exertional syncope = cardiac until proven otherwise (HOCM, AS, arrhythmia, PE)
  • Triggers: Pain, emotion, standing, prolonged standing, hot environment = vasovagal. Post-micturition/defecation = situational
  • Recovery: Immediate = syncope. Confusion/lethargy = seizure or stroke
  • Witnesses: Tonic-clonic movements? (can occur in syncope but brief <15sec, vs prolonged in seizure). Tongue biting/incontinence more typical of seizure

Alternate Presentations by Etiology:

  • Vasovagal (Neurocardiogenic): Most common (40-50%). Prodrome of nausea, diaphoresis, visual changes. Triggered by emotional stress, pain, prolonged standing, hot environment. Young patients. Benign
  • Orthostatic Hypotension: Syncope upon standing from sitting/supine. Elderly, autonomic dysfunction, medications (Œ±-blockers, diuretics, vasodilators), volume depletion. BP drop ‚â•20/10 mmHg within 3 minutes of standing
  • Cardiac Arrhythmia: No warning, sudden collapse. Palpitations before/after. High-risk: age >60, exertional, supine, no prodrome, heart disease. Bradyarrhythmias (sick sinus, AV block) or tachyarrhythmias (VT, SVT)
  • Structural Cardiac: Aortic stenosis (exertional syncope, systolic murmur), HOCM (young athletes, exertional, family history sudden death), cardiac tamponade, myxoma, PE (dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain)
  • Neurologic (RARE cause): Vertebrobasilar TIA (diplopia, ataxia, vertigo), subclavian steal, seizure (postictal confusion differentiates from syncope)
  • Situational: Specific triggers - micturition (post-void), defecation, cough, swallow. Vasovagal-mediated
  • Carotid Sinus Hypersensitivity: Elderly men, triggered by tight collar, head turning, shaving. Bradycardia/hypotension with carotid massage
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

ALL Patients Must Have:

  • Detailed History: Events before/during/after, position, activity, prodrome, recovery time, witnesses, prior episodes, medications, cardiac history
  • Physical Exam: Complete cardiac exam (murmurs, JVD, edema), neurologic exam (focal deficits?), orthostatic vitals (supine ‚Üí standing at 1 and 3 min)
  • ECG (MANDATORY for ALL): Look for: prolonged QT (>480ms = high risk for Torsades), Brugada pattern (ST elevation V1-V3), pre-excitation (WPW = short PR, delta wave), epsilon wave (ARVC), pathologic Q waves (old MI), heart block, SVT/VT

Risk Stratification - San Francisco Syncope Rule (HIGH RISK if ANY):

  • History of CHF
  • ECG abnormal (any non-sinus rhythm or new changes)
  • Anemia (Hct <30%)
  • Respiratory - Shortness of breath
  • Triage systolic BP <90 mmHg
  • If ANY present ‚Üí 7-10% risk of serious outcome at 30 days ‚Üí ADMIT

Labs (Based on Clinical Suspicion):

  • Basic: CBC (anemia?), BMP (electrolytes - hypokalemia can cause QT prolongation), glucose (hypoglycemia?)
  • If cardiac suspected: Troponin (ACS?), BNP (heart failure?), D-dimer if PE suspected
  • If volume depletion suspected: BUN/Cr ratio, orthostatic vitals
  • Pregnancy test: All women of childbearing age (ectopic pregnancy can present as syncope)

Advanced Testing (If Indicated):

  • Echocardiography: If structural heart disease suspected (murmur, abnormal ECG, exertional syncope, known heart disease). Assess for AS, HOCM, RV strain (PE), tamponade, LV function
  • Telemetry/Holter Monitor: If arrhythmia suspected. Admit for telemetry if high-risk features. Outpatient Holter (24-48hr) or event monitor (30 days) for recurrent syncope
  • Exercise Stress Test: If exertional syncope (evaluate for ischemia, arrhythmias). Contraindicated if severe AS suspected
  • Tilt Table Test: If recurrent syncope, no cardiac cause found. Reproduces vasovagal response. Positive = BP drop >25mmHg or HR <60 with symptoms
  • Electrophysiology Study (EP Study): For unexplained syncope with structural heart disease or high suspicion for arrhythmia. Can induce/document VT, assess sinus/AV node function
  • CT Head: Only if focal neurologic deficits, head trauma, persistent altered mental status. Syncope alone does NOT require head CT
  • Carotid Ultrasound: Generally low yield. Consider if carotid bruit or vertebrobasilar symptoms
  • CT PE Protocol: If dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain, hypoxia, tachycardia, RV strain on ECG

Orthostatic Vitals Technique:

  • Supine √ó 5 minutes ‚Üí measure BP/HR
  • Stand immediately ‚Üí measure at 1 minute and 3 minutes
  • Positive: SBP drop ‚â•20 mmHg OR DBP drop ‚â•10 mmHg OR HR increase ‚â•30 bpm (or HR >120)
  • Symptoms (lightheadedness, presyncope) with standing = clinical orthostasis even if BP criteria not met
TREATMENT PROTOCOL

General Principles:

  • Treatment depends on underlying cause
  • Admit if high-risk features (see San Francisco Syncope Rule, cardiac cause suspected)
  • Discharge if low-risk and clear benign etiology (young, vasovagal, normal ECG, normal exam)

Vasovagal Syncope (Reflex/Neurocardiogenic):

  • Reassurance: Benign condition, excellent prognosis. Explain pathophysiology
  • Trigger Avoidance: Avoid prolonged standing, hot environments, dehydration, emotional stress. Sit/lie down at first prodromal symptom
  • Hydration: Increase fluid intake to 2-3 L/day. Liberal salt intake (6-10g/day) if no contraindications
  • Physical Counterpressure Maneuvers: At first sign of prodrome: leg crossing + tensing, squatting, arm tensing. Can abort episode
  • Tilt Training: Gradual exposure to prolonged standing (start 5 min, increase to 30+ min)
  • Pharmacologic (Refractory cases only):
    • Midodrine 5-10mg TID (Œ±-agonist, increases BP) - first-line medication
    • Fludrocortisone 0.1-0.2mg daily (volume expansion)
    • Œ≤-blockers (metoprolol) - controversial, limited evidence
  • Pacemaker: Very rarely indicated. Only if severe cardioinhibitory response (asystole >3 sec) on tilt table and failed medical therapy

Orthostatic Hypotension:

  • Identify and Address Causes:
    • Volume depletion ‚Üí IV fluids, oral hydration
    • Medications ‚Üí stop/reduce Œ±-blockers, diuretics, vasodilators, antihypertensives, antidepressants
    • Autonomic dysfunction ‚Üí treat underlying (diabetes, Parkinson's, amyloidosis)
  • Non-Pharmacologic:
    • Rise slowly from supine/sitting (sit at edge of bed √ó 1 min before standing)
    • Increase fluid intake (2-3 L/day) and salt (6-10g/day if no CHF)
    • Compression stockings (waist-high, 30-40 mmHg) - wear before rising
    • Elevate head of bed 10-20 degrees at night (reduces nocturnal diuresis)
    • Avoid large meals (can cause postprandial hypotension)
    • Physical counterpressure maneuvers
  • Pharmacologic:
    • Midodrine 2.5-10mg TID (last dose before 6 PM to avoid supine hypertension)
    • Fludrocortisone 0.1-0.3mg daily (watch for hypokalemia, edema, supine hypertension)
    • Droxidopa 100-600mg TID (norepinephrine precursor, for neurogenic OH)
    • Pyridostigmine 60mg TID (if autonomic failure)

Cardiac Syncope - ADMIT for Telemetry:

  • Arrhythmia Management:
    • Bradycardia (sick sinus, AV block) ‚Üí Cardiology consult, consider pacemaker
    • Tachyarrhythmia (VT, SVT) ‚Üí Antiarrhythmics, ablation, ICD evaluation
    • Long QT syndrome ‚Üí Remove offending drugs, electrolyte repletion (Mg, K), Œ≤-blockers, ICD if high risk
    • Brugada, ARVC ‚Üí EP study, ICD placement
  • Structural Disease:
    • Aortic stenosis ‚Üí Cardiology/CT surgery referral for valve replacement if severe + symptomatic
    • HOCM ‚Üí Œ≤-blockers, avoid volume depletion/vasodilators. ICD if high risk. Septal reduction if refractory
    • Pulmonary embolism ‚Üí Anticoagulation, thrombolytics if massive PE
    • Cardiac tamponade ‚Üí Pericardiocentesis
  • Ischemia: If syncope due to ACS ‚Üí treat per ACS protocol (aspirin, heparin, cath lab)

Situational Syncope:

  • Micturition syncope ‚Üí Sit to void, avoid straining, stay hydrated
  • Defecation syncope ‚Üí Avoid straining (stool softeners), adequate hydration
  • Cough/swallow syncope ‚Üí Treat underlying cause (GERD, cough), maneuvers to reduce triggering

Neurologic Syncope (Rare):

  • Vertebrobasilar TIA ‚Üí Neurology consult, antiplatelet therapy, imaging (MRA/CTA neck)
  • Subclavian steal ‚Üí Vascular surgery referral
  • Seizure (if postictal confusion suggests) ‚Üí Neurology consult, antiepileptics, EEG
üîë TEACHING PEARLS
  • ECG is MANDATORY for ALL syncope patients: Single most important test. Can diagnose Brugada, long QT, WPW, ARVC, heart block, old MI. Never discharge syncope patient without ECG
  • Syncope while supine = cardiac until proven otherwise: Vasovagal almost never occurs supine. If patient syncopizes lying down, think arrhythmia and admit for telemetry
  • Exertional syncope = high-risk: Think HOCM (young athletes), AS (elderly), VT, coronary ischemia. These patients need echo, stress test, and cardiology evaluation
  • Red flags for cardiac syncope: Age >60, exertional, supine position, no prodrome, known heart disease, abnormal ECG, family history sudden cardiac death. Admit these patients
  • Vasovagal has a prodrome, cardiac doesn't: If patient describes nausea, diaphoresis, tunnel vision before passing out = vasovagal (low risk). No warning, sudden collapse = cardiac (high risk)
  • Don't confuse syncope with seizure: Syncope = brief LOC (<20 sec), rapid complete recovery, may have brief myoclonic jerks (<15 sec). Seizure = prolonged LOC, tonic-clonic movements, tongue biting, incontinence, postictal confusion/lethargy. But can be tough to distinguish!
  • Head CT rarely useful: Only if focal neurologic findings, head trauma, or persistent altered mental status. Syncope is due to global hypoperfusion, not focal brain lesion. Don't reflexively order head CT
  • San Francisco Syncope Rule is your friend: If none of the 5 criteria (CHF, abnormal ECG, Hct <30%, SOB, SBP <90), patient is low-risk (<1% serious outcome) and can likely be discharged with outpatient follow-up if vasovagal etiology clear
  • Orthostatic vitals technique matters: Must be supine √ó 5 min first, then check at 1 and 3 minutes standing. Immediate standing without adequate supine time can give false positives
  • Young healthy patient with classic vasovagal story needs minimal workup: Prodrome, triggered by standing/heat/emotion, quick recovery, normal ECG = can discharge with reassurance and anticipatory guidance. Don't over-test these patients
  • Medications are a common culprit: Œ±-blockers (BPH meds), diuretics, antihypertensives, TCAs, antipsychotics, PDE5 inhibitors. Always review medication list carefully
  • Carotid massage to diagnose carotid sinus hypersensitivity: Only perform if safe (no carotid bruits, no recent stroke/TIA). Massage one side √ó 5 seconds. Positive = >3 sec asystole or >50 mmHg BP drop. Never massage both sides simultaneously!

  • Syncope with ongoing chest pain suggesting acute MI
  • Hemodynamically unstable (persistent hypotension, shock)
  • Malignant arrhythmia (sustained VT/VF requiring cardioversion)
  • High-grade AV block (Mobitz II, complete heart block) requiring pacing
  • Massive pulmonary embolism with hypotension
  • Cardiac tamponade
  • Aortic dissection
  • Ruptured ectopic pregnancy with hemorrhagic shock

  • NO high-risk features (San Francisco Syncope Rule negative)
  • Normal or unchanged ECG
  • Normal vital signs and orthostatics
  • No concerning cardiac exam findings
  • Labs normal (if obtained)
  • No recurrent episodes during observation
  • Able to ambulate safely without recurrence
  • Patient counseled on trigger avoidance (if vasovagal)
  • Close follow-up arranged (PCP within 1 week, Cardiology if needed)
  • Clear return precautions: recurrent syncope, chest pain, palpitations, dyspnea
  • Driving restrictions discussed (varies by state, typically 3-6 months abstinence after cardiac syncope)
COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR

From Syncope Episode: Trauma from fall (head injury, fractures, lacerations), motor vehicle accidents (if driving), sudden cardiac death (if malignant arrhythmia), prolonged cerebral hypoperfusion causing ischemia (rare)

From Underlying Cardiac Cause: Sudden cardiac death (VT/VF, complete heart block), acute MI/ACS, cardiac arrest, stroke from arrhythmia with thrombus formation, progression of structural heart disease (AS, HOCM)

From Recurrent Episodes: Injury from repeated falls, impaired quality of life, anxiety/depression, loss of driving privileges, loss of employment (especially if operate machinery), social isolation

From Treatment: Supine hypertension (midodrine, fludrocortisone - can cause nocturnal HTN), hypokalemia (fludrocortisone), edema (fludrocortisone), bradycardia (Œ≤-blockers), pacemaker complications (infection, lead malfunction), ICD shocks (appropriate or inappropriate)

41. PERICARDITIS / PERICARDIAL EFFUSION / CARDIAC TAMPONADE

Acute Pericarditis ‚Ä¢ Effusion ‚Ä¢ Tamponade
CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Classic Triad of Acute Pericarditis: Chest pain, pericardial friction rub, diffuse ST elevations on ECG

Chest Pain: Sharp, pleuritic, worse lying flat, better sitting up leaning forward. Radiates to trapezius (highly specific). Not exertional

DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

Labs: ESR/CRP (elevated), troponin (if myopericarditis), BMP, blood cultures if febrile

ECG: Stage 1: Diffuse ST elevation + PR depression. Stage 2: Normalization. Stage 3: T wave inversion. Stage 4: Resolution

Echo: Assess effusion size, tamponade signs (RA/RV collapse, IVC plethora, respiratory variation >25%/40%)

Cardiac MRI: If diagnosis unclear or assessing for myopericarditis

TREATMENT PROTOCOL

Acute Pericarditis:

  • NSAIDs: Ibuprofen 600-800mg PO TID √ó 1-2 weeks, taper over 2-4 weeks OR Aspirin 750-1000mg TID
  • Colchicine: 0.6mg BID √ó 3 months (reduces recurrence 50%). ALWAYS add to NSAIDs
  • PPI: GI protection
  • Avoid Steroids: Increase recurrence risk unless autoimmune/uremic/contraindication to NSAIDs
  • Activity Restriction: No strenuous exercise until symptoms resolve and CRP normalizes (3 months for athletes)

  • STAT cardiology/cardiac surgery
