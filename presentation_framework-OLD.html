Acute Chest Pain

TIERED DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
TIER 1: Acuity/Severity Assessment
  • Hemodynamically unstable? ‚Üí STEMI, Massive PE, Tamponade, Aortic Dissection
  • ST elevation present? ‚Üí STEMI - activate cath lab
  • Severe sudden onset with radiation? ‚Üí Aortic dissection
  • Hypotension + muffled heart sounds + JVD? ‚Üí Cardiac tamponade
TIER 2: Timing Pattern
  • Sudden onset (seconds-minutes): PE, Pneumothorax, Aortic dissection, MI
  • Acute onset (minutes-hours): ACS, Pericarditis, Pneumonia
  • Progressive (hours-days): Pneumonia, Pericarditis, Esophageal disease
  • Intermittent/recurrent: Stable angina, GERD, Musculoskeletal
TIER 3: Quality & Radiation
  • Pressure/squeezing radiating to jaw/arm ‚Üí ACS
  • Sharp, pleuritic, positional ‚Üí Pericarditis, PE, Pneumonia, Pneumothorax
  • Tearing, radiating to back ‚Üí Aortic dissection
  • Burning, worse after meals ‚Üí GERD, esophagitis
  • Reproducible with palpation ‚Üí Musculoskeletal, costochondritis
TIER 4: Pathophysiologic Category
Cardiovascular
  • #1 ACS/STEMI/NSTEMI - Troponin elevation, ECG changes
  • #2 Acute Heart Failure - Exertional dyspnea, crackles, edema
  • #17 Pulmonary Embolism - Unilateral leg swelling, hypoxia, pleuritic
  • #7 Pericarditis/Tamponade - Positional, friction rub, electrical alternans
  • Aortic Dissection - Sudden tearing, pulse/BP differential, widened mediastinum
Pulmonary
  • #14 Pneumonia - Fever, cough, infiltrate on CXR
  • #19 Pneumothorax - Sudden onset, decreased breath sounds, hyperresonance
  • #18 Pleural Effusion - Dullness to percussion, decreased breath sounds
GI/Other
  • GERD/Esophagitis - Burning, worse supine, response to antacids
  • Esophageal spasm - Intermittent, may have dysphagia
  • Musculoskeletal - Reproducible, worse with movement
‚ö†Ô∏è MUST NOT MISS
  • #1 STEMI - Activate cath lab within 90 minutes
  • #17 Massive PE - Hypotension, consider thrombolytics
  • Aortic Dissection - Hold anticoagulation, emergent CT, cardiothoracic surgery
  • #7 Cardiac Tamponade - Emergent pericardiocentesis
  • #19 Tension Pneumothorax - Needle decompression
üî¨ INITIAL WORKUP
  • ECG within 10 minutes - look for STEMI, NSTEMI, PE (S1Q3T3)
  • Troponin at 0, 3, 6 hours if initially negative
  • Chest X-ray - PTX, infiltrate, widened mediastinum, cardiomegaly
  • D-dimer if intermediate PE probability
  • CT chest with contrast (PE protocol) if high suspicion PE
  • CT angiography if dissection suspected
  • BNP if heart failure suspected
  • Bedside echo if tamponade suspected

Acute Dyspnea

TIERED DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
TIER 1: Acuity/Severity Assessment
  • Hemodynamically unstable? ‚Üí Massive PE, Tension PTX, Tamponade, Cardiogenic shock
  • Severe hypoxia (O2 sat <90% on room air)? ‚Üí High-acuity cause
  • Stridor/impending airway obstruction? ‚Üí Anaphylaxis, epiglottitis
  • Silent chest (minimal air movement)? ‚Üí Status asthmaticus
TIER 2: Timing Pattern
  • Sudden onset (seconds-minutes): PE, Pneumothorax, Aspiration, Flash pulmonary edema
  • Acute onset (hours): Pneumonia, CHF decompensation, Asthma/COPD exacerbation
  • Subacute (days-weeks): Pleural effusion, Anemia, Chronic CHF worsening
  • Chronic progressive: ILD, COPD, Chronic heart failure
TIER 3: Exam Pattern & Localization
  • Unilateral decreased breath sounds ‚Üí PE, Pneumothorax, Pneumonia, Pleural effusion
  • Bilateral crackles + JVD + edema ‚Üí Acute heart failure
  • Wheezing (diffuse) ‚Üí Asthma, COPD, CHF (cardiac asthma), Anaphylaxis
  • Wheezing (focal) ‚Üí Aspiration, mass/obstruction
  • Clear lungs + hypoxia ‚Üí PE, Anemia, Metabolic acidosis, Pulmonary vascular disease
TIER 4: Pathophysiologic Category
Cardiovascular
  • #2 Acute Heart Failure - Crackles, JVD, edema, elevated BNP
  • #1 ACS - Chest pain, troponin elevation, ECG changes
  • #17 Pulmonary Embolism - Leg swelling, pleuritic pain, hypoxia with clear lungs
  • #7 Cardiac Tamponade - Hypotension, JVD, muffled heart sounds
  • #10 Cardiogenic Shock - Hypotension, cold extremities, elevated lactate
Pulmonary - Airway
  • #16 Asthma Exacerbation - Wheezing, prolonged expiration, accessory muscles
  • #15 COPD Exacerbation - Wheezing, increased sputum, history of smoking
  • #22 Hypercapnic Respiratory Failure - Altered mental status, CO2 retention
  • Upper airway obstruction - Stridor, angioedema
Pulmonary - Alveolar
  • #14 Community-Acquired Pneumonia - Fever, cough, infiltrate on CXR
  • #23 Influenza - Fever, myalgias, diffuse infiltrates
  • #24 COVID-19 - Fever, bilateral infiltrates, lymphopenia
  • ARDS - Severe hypoxia, bilateral infiltrates, recent critical illness
Pulmonary - Pleural/Mechanical
  • #19 Pneumothorax - Sudden onset, unilateral decreased breath sounds
  • #18 Pleural Effusion - Dullness, decreased breath sounds, blunted costophrenic angle
  • #21 Massive Hemoptysis - Airway compromise, bleeding
Infectious
  • #14 CAP - Fever, productive cough, leukocytosis
  • #23 Influenza - Seasonal, myalgias, rapid antigen
  • #24 COVID-19 - Bilateral ground glass, elevated D-dimer
  • #25 Sepsis - SIRS criteria, source of infection
Metabolic
  • #47 DKA - Kussmaul breathing, anion gap acidosis, glucose >250
  • Severe Anemia - Hgb <7, fatigue, tachycardia
  • Metabolic Acidosis - Compensatory tachypnea, low HCO3
  • #49 Thyroid Storm - Tachycardia, hyperthermia, tremor
Inflammatory/Allergic
  • #84 Anaphylaxis - Urticaria, angioedema, wheezing, hypotension
  • #20 ILD Exacerbation - Chronic cough, restrictive pattern, honeycombing
  • Hypersensitivity pneumonitis - Exposure history, restrictive
‚ö†Ô∏è MUST NOT MISS
  • #17 Massive PE - Hypotension, consider thrombolytics
  • #19 Tension Pneumothorax - Needle decompression immediately
  • #1 ACS - Troponin, ECG, consider cath
  • #2 Flash Pulmonary Edema - IV diuretics, BiPAP
  • #16 Status Asthmaticus - ICU, consider intubation
  • #84 Anaphylaxis - IM epinephrine immediately
üî¨ INITIAL WORKUP
  • Chest X-ray - First test for almost all dyspnea
  • Pulse oximetry and ABG/VBG if hypoxic
  • ECG - Rule out ACS, look for PE pattern (S1Q3T3), atrial fibrillation
  • BNP/NT-proBNP - Elevated in CHF (>100 pg/mL rules in)
  • Troponin - Rule out ACS
  • CBC - Anemia, leukocytosis
  • D-dimer if intermediate PE probability
  • CT chest with PE protocol if high suspicion
  • Consider bedside ultrasound (lung, cardiac)

Altered Mental Status / Confusion

TIERED DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
TIER 1: Acuity/Severity Assessment
  • Coma/unresponsive (GCS <8)? ‚Üí Consider intubation, emergent CT, check glucose
  • Focal neurologic deficits? ‚Üí Stroke, ICH, mass lesion - STAT head CT
  • Seizure activity? ‚Üí Status epilepticus - benzos, check glucose
  • Severe hypoglycemia (<40)? ‚Üí D50 immediately
TIER 2: Timing Pattern
  • Hyperacute (seconds-minutes): Stroke, Seizure, Hypoglycemia, Intoxication
  • Acute (hours): Meningitis, Encephalitis, DKA, Severe sepsis, Wernicke
  • Subacute (days): UTI (elderly), Medication effect, Hepatic encephalopathy
  • Chronic/progressive: Dementia, NPH, Subdural hematoma
TIER 3: Associated Features
  • Fever + headache + neck stiffness ‚Üí Meningitis
  • Fever + focal deficits ‚Üí Encephalitis, Brain abscess
  • Asterixis + jaundice ‚Üí Hepatic encephalopathy
  • Recent trauma ‚Üí Subdural hematoma, Concussion
  • Kussmaul breathing + fruity odor ‚Üí DKA
  • Ophthalmoplegia + ataxia + confusion ‚Üí Wernicke encephalopathy
TIER 4: Pathophysiologic Category
Vascular/Neurologic
  • #67 Ischemic Stroke - Focal deficits, acute onset, NIHSS
  • #68 Hemorrhagic Stroke - Headache, HTN, focal deficits
  • #73 Increased ICP - Headache, vomiting, Cushing triad
  • #69 Subarachnoid Hemorrhage - Worst headache of life, nuchal rigidity
  • #70 TIA - Transient deficits that resolved
Infectious
  • #29 Bacterial Meningitis - Fever, headache, neck stiffness, Kernig/Brudzinski
  • #66 Encephalitis - Fever, focal deficits, seizures, CSF lymphocytosis
  • #25 Sepsis - SIRS, hypotension, source of infection
  • #27 UTI (elderly) - Dysuria may be absent, leukocytosis
  • #32 CNS TB/Opportunistic Infection - Immunocompromised, subacute
Metabolic
  • #47 DKA - Glucose >250, anion gap, Kussmaul breathing
  • #48 HHS - Glucose >600, profound dehydration, osmolality >320
  • #37 Severe Hyponatremia - Na <120, seizures possible
  • #36 Severe Hyperkalemia - Arrhythmia risk
  • Hypoglycemia - Glucose <50, diaphoresis, responds to D50
  • #40 Hypercalcemia - Calcium >14, "stones, bones, moans, groans"
  • Uremia - BUN >100, asterixis, pericardial rub
Toxic/Medication
  • #81 Drug Overdose - Toxidrome pattern, history
  • #82 Opioid Overdose - Miosis, respiratory depression
  • #83 Alcohol Withdrawal - Tremor, hallucinations, seizures
  • Wernicke Encephalopathy - Ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, alcoholism
  • Anticholinergic - Hot, dry, red, blind, mad
  • Serotonin syndrome - Hyperthermia, clonus, recent SSRI
Organ Failure
  • #56 Hepatic Encephalopathy - Asterixis, jaundice, ammonia >100
  • #35 Uremic Encephalopathy - Severe AKI/ESRD, asterixis
  • #22 Hypercapnic Failure - CO2 retention, somnolence, asterixis
  • Hypoxic Encephalopathy - Severe hypoxia, recent code
‚ö†Ô∏è MUST NOT MISS
  • #67 Ischemic Stroke - tPA window 4.5 hours, thrombectomy 24 hours
  • #68 Hemorrhagic Stroke - Hold anticoagulation, neurosurgery consult
  • #29 Bacterial Meningitis - Antibiotics within 1 hour
  • #65 Status Epilepticus - Benzos immediately, don't wait for labs
  • Hypoglycemia - Check glucose first, give D50 if <50
  • #47 DKA - Fluids, insulin, monitor K closely
  • #25 Sepsis - Antibiotics within 1 hour, fluids
üî¨ INITIAL WORKUP
  • Fingerstick glucose - FIRST TEST, treat if <50
  • Head CT non-contrast - Rule out bleed, mass, herniation
  • CBC, BMP (Na, glucose, Ca, renal function)
  • Ammonia if liver disease
  • Troponin, ECG - Cardiac cause
  • Urinalysis and culture - UTI common in elderly
  • Chest X-ray - Pneumonia
  • Blood cultures if febrile
  • Lumbar puncture if meningitis/encephalitis suspected (after CT)
  • Toxicology screen
  • Thiamine 500mg IV empirically (before dextrose if alcoholic)

Fever (Hospital Admission)

TIERED DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
TIER 1: Acuity/Severity Assessment
  • Septic shock (hypotension + organ dysfunction)? ‚Üí Fluids, antibiotics within 1 hour
  • Neutropenic (ANC <500)? ‚Üí High risk, broad antibiotics immediately
  • Meningeal signs? ‚Üí Bacterial meningitis until proven otherwise
  • Rapidly spreading erythema/crepitus? ‚Üí Necrotizing fasciitis - surgical emergency
TIER 2: Timing Pattern
  • Acute (<24 hours): Bacterial infection, meningitis, sepsis
  • Subacute (2-7 days): Pneumonia, UTI, cellulitis, viral illness
  • Chronic (>1 week): TB, endocarditis, malignancy, autoimmune
  • Post-operative: Surgical site infection, anastomotic leak, line infection
TIER 3: Localizing Features
  • Cough + infiltrate ‚Üí Pneumonia
  • Dysuria + flank pain ‚Üí Pyelonephritis
  • Skin erythema/swelling ‚Üí Cellulitis, abscess, necrotizing fasciitis
  • Headache + neck stiffness ‚Üí Meningitis
  • Diarrhea ‚Üí Infectious diarrhea, C. diff
  • No localizing symptoms ‚Üí Bacteremia, endocarditis, occult abscess
TIER 4: Pathophysiologic Category
Pulmonary Infectious
  • #14 Community-Acquired Pneumonia - Cough, infiltrate, leukocytosis
  • #23 Influenza - Seasonal, myalgias, rapid antigen positive
  • #24 COVID-19 - Bilateral infiltrates, lymphopenia, D-dimer elevation
  • #32 Tuberculosis - Chronic cough, night sweats, upper lobe cavitary lesion
Genitourinary Infectious
  • #27 UTI/Pyelonephritis - Dysuria, flank pain, pyuria, positive culture
  • Prostatitis - Male, perineal pain, tender prostate
  • PID - Female, pelvic pain, cervical motion tenderness
Skin/Soft Tissue Infectious
  • #26 Cellulitis - Erythema, warmth, tenderness, well-demarcated
  • #31 SSTI/Abscess - Fluctuance, purulent drainage
  • Necrotizing Fasciitis - Rapid spread, crepitus, severe pain, systemic toxicity
CNS Infectious
  • #29 Bacterial Meningitis - Headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, CSF pleocytosis
  • #66 Encephalitis - Altered mental status, focal deficits, seizures
  • Brain Abscess - Focal deficits, ring-enhancing lesion
GI/Intra-abdominal Infectious
  • #28 C. difficile - Diarrhea, leukocytosis, recent antibiotics
  • #55 SBP - Ascites + peritonitis, PMN >250
  • #58 Cholecystitis - RUQ pain, Murphy sign, gallstones
  • Diverticulitis - LLQ pain, CT with stranding
Cardiovascular Infectious
  • #8 Infective Endocarditis - New murmur, embolic phenomena, positive blood cultures
  • Line-associated bacteremia - Central line present, no other source
Systemic/Other
  • #25 Sepsis/Septic Shock - SIRS + source + organ dysfunction
  • #30 Neutropenic Fever - ANC <500, any fever >38.3¬∞C
  • #34 Opportunistic Infections - HIV/AIDS, immunosuppressed
‚ö†Ô∏è MUST NOT MISS
  • #25 Septic Shock - Antibiotics within 1 hour, 30mL/kg crystalloid
  • #29 Bacterial Meningitis - Antibiotics within 1 hour, LP if no contraindication
  • #30 Neutropenic Fever - Broad antibiotics immediately, high mortality
  • Necrotizing Fasciitis - Emergent surgical debridement
  • #8 Endocarditis - Blood cultures x3, echo, prolonged antibiotics
  • #32 TB (active) - Respiratory isolation, public health notification
üî¨ INITIAL WORKUP
  • Blood cultures x2 (before antibiotics if stable)
  • CBC with differential - leukocytosis, neutropenia, bandemia
  • Lactate - elevated in sepsis
  • Procalcitonin - bacterial vs viral (>0.5 suggests bacterial)
  • Urinalysis and urine culture
  • Chest X-ray
  • Directed imaging based on localizing symptoms
  • Consider LP if meningeal signs (after CT if focal deficits)
  • Empiric antibiotics if septic (within 1 hour)

Hypotension / Shock

TIERED DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
TIER 1: Acuity/Severity Assessment
  • SBP <90 mmHg or MAP <65? ‚Üí Shock, requires immediate intervention
  • Altered mental status? ‚Üí End-organ hypoperfusion
  • Lactate >4? ‚Üí Severe shock, high mortality
  • Unstable arrhythmia? ‚Üí Cardiogenic or obstructive shock
TIER 2: Hemodynamic Pattern
  • Warm extremities + bounding pulses ‚Üí Distributive (septic, anaphylactic)
  • Cold extremities + weak pulses ‚Üí Cardiogenic or hypovolemic
  • JVD elevated ‚Üí Cardiogenic or obstructive (PE, tamponade)
  • JVD low/flat ‚Üí Hypovolemic or distributive
  • Response to fluid bolus? ‚Üí Hypovolemic if improves
TIER 3: Associated Features
  • Chest pain + ST elevation ‚Üí Cardiogenic shock from STEMI
  • Fever + SIRS ‚Üí Septic shock
  • GI bleeding + melena/hematemesis ‚Üí Hemorrhagic shock
  • Urticaria + stridor + wheezing ‚Üí Anaphylactic shock
  • Muffled heart sounds + JVD ‚Üí Obstructive (tamponade)
  • Unilateral leg swelling + hypoxia ‚Üí Obstructive (massive PE)
TIER 4: Pathophysiologic Category
Distributive Shock
  • #25 Septic Shock - Warm, bounding, SIRS, lactate >2, source of infection
  • #84 Anaphylactic Shock - Urticaria, angioedema, recent exposure, IM epi
  • #51 Adrenal Crisis - Hyperpigmentation, hyperK, recent steroid cessation
  • #49 Thyroid Storm - Hyperthermia, tachycardia, tremor
Cardiogenic Shock
  • #10 Cardiogenic Shock - Cold, JVD elevated, crackles, low CO
  • #1 STEMI with shock - Chest pain, ST elevation, troponin elevation
  • #9 Myocarditis - Viral prodrome, troponin elevation, diffuse ST changes
  • #3 Atrial Fibrillation with RVR - Irregularly irregular, rate >150
  • #12 Ventricular Tachycardia - Wide complex tachycardia, unstable
Obstructive Shock
  • #7 Cardiac Tamponade - Muffled sounds, JVD, pulsus paradoxus, Beck triad
  • #17 Massive PE - Leg swelling, hypoxia, RV strain on echo
  • #19 Tension Pneumothorax - Decreased breath sounds, tracheal deviation
Hypovolemic Shock
  • #53 Upper GI Bleed - Hematemesis, melena, BUN/Cr ratio >30
  • Lower GI Bleed - Bright red blood per rectum, hematochezia
  • Hemorrhage (trauma) - External bleeding, hemoperitoneum on FAST
  • Dehydration - Poor PO intake, vomiting, diarrhea, dry mucous membranes
‚ö†Ô∏è MUST NOT MISS
  • #25 Septic Shock - Antibiotics + 30mL/kg crystalloid within 1 hour
  • #1 STEMI with cardiogenic shock - Emergent cath lab, consider mechanical support
  • #7 Cardiac Tamponade - Emergent pericardiocentesis
  • #17 Massive PE - Consider thrombolytics if hemodynamically unstable
  • #84 Anaphylaxis - IM epinephrine 0.3mg immediately
  • #53 Massive GI Bleed - 2 large-bore IVs, transfuse, emergent endoscopy
üî¨ INITIAL WORKUP
  • STAT ECG - STEMI, arrhythmia, PE pattern
  • Troponin - Cardiogenic cause
  • Lactate - Severity of shock (>4 = severe)
  • CBC - Anemia, leukocytosis
  • Type and screen/crossmatch if bleeding
  • Chest X-ray - Pneumothorax, infiltrate, cardiomegaly
  • Bedside ultrasound (POCUS/RUSH exam) - Cardiac function, IVC, lungs, FAST
  • Blood cultures if sepsis suspected
  • CT imaging based on suspected source
  • Resuscitation: 2 large-bore IVs, fluids, pressors as needed

Acute Abdominal Pain

TIERED DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
TIER 1: Acuity/Severity Assessment
  • Hemodynamically unstable? ‚Üí Ruptured AAA, ectopic pregnancy, perforated viscus
  • Peritoneal signs (guarding, rigidity, rebound)? ‚Üí Surgical abdomen
  • Free air on imaging? ‚Üí Perforated viscus - surgical emergency
  • Pulsatile abdominal mass? ‚Üí AAA - immediate vascular surgery consult
TIER 2: Timing Pattern
  • Sudden onset (seconds-minutes): Perforation, Ruptured AAA, Ectopic rupture, Volvulus
  • Acute onset (hours): Appendicitis, Cholecystitis, Pancreatitis, Bowel obstruction
  • Subacute (days): Diverticulitis, Inflammatory bowel disease
  • Chronic/recurrent: PUD, GERD, Chronic pancreatitis, IBS
TIER 3: Location & Character
  • RUQ + Murphy sign ‚Üí Cholecystitis, hepatitis, cholangitis
  • Epigastric + elevated lipase ‚Üí Pancreatitis
  • RLQ + McBurney point tenderness ‚Üí Appendicitis
  • LLQ + fever ‚Üí Diverticulitis
  • Diffuse + peritoneal signs ‚Üí Perforation, peritonitis, ischemia
  • Colicky + distension ‚Üí Bowel obstruction
  • Pain out of proportion to exam ‚Üí Mesenteric ischemia
TIER 4: Pathophysiologic Category
Vascular
  • #60 Ruptured AAA - Pulsatile mass, flank ecchymosis, hypotension
  • #61 Mesenteric Ischemia - Pain out of proportion, lactate >2, older adult with AFib
  • Splenic infarct - LUQ pain, sickle cell or AFib
Inflammatory/Infectious
  • #57 Acute Pancreatitis - Epigastric pain, lipase >3x ULN, nausea/vomiting
  • #58 Acute Cholecystitis - RUQ pain, Murphy sign, gallstones on US
  • Cholangitis - Charcot triad (RUQ pain, fever, jaundice)
  • Appendicitis - RLQ pain, McBurney point, leukocytosis
  • Diverticulitis - LLQ pain, fever, CT with fat stranding
  • #55 SBP - Ascites + fever + peritonitis, PMN >250
  • Inflammatory bowel disease - Chronic diarrhea, blood in stool
Mechanical/Obstruction
  • #59 Bowel Obstruction - Colicky pain, distension, air-fluid levels on XR
  • Volvulus - Sudden onset, "bird beak" on imaging
  • #62 Perforated Viscus - Free air under diaphragm, peritonitis
  • Intussusception - Colicky pain, "target sign" on imaging (pediatrics)
GI/Hepatic
  • #53 Upper GI Bleed - Hematemesis, melena, epigastric pain
  • #54 Cirrhosis with Ascites - Distension, shifting dullness, stigmata
  • PUD - Epigastric pain, worse with eating or fasting
  • Gastroenteritis - Diarrhea, nausea, vomiting
Genitourinary
  • Nephrolithiasis - Flank pain, hematuria, colicky
  • #27 Pyelonephritis - Flank pain, fever, CVA tenderness
  • Ectopic pregnancy - RLQ/LLQ pain, vaginal bleeding, positive Œ≤-hCG
  • Ovarian torsion - Sudden onset, unilateral, nausea
Other
  • #1 Inferior MI - Epigastric pain, ST elevation in inferior leads
  • DKA - Diffuse pain, nausea, Kussmaul breathing
  • Herpes zoster - Dermatomal pain, vesicular rash
‚ö†Ô∏è MUST NOT MISS
  • #60 Ruptured AAA - Immediate vascular surgery, no CT if unstable
  • #61 Mesenteric Ischemia - CTA abdomen, vascular surgery consult
  • #62 Perforated Viscus - NPO, antibiotics, emergent surgery
  • Ectopic pregnancy - Œ≤-hCG, ultrasound, Ob/Gyn consult
  • #58 Cholecystitis with cholangitis - Antibiotics, ERCP within 24 hours
  • #1 Inferior MI - ECG, troponin, may mimic GI pathology
üî¨ INITIAL WORKUP
  • CBC - Leukocytosis, anemia
  • BMP - Electrolytes, renal function
  • Lipase - Pancreatitis (>3x ULN diagnostic)
  • LFTs - Hepatobiliary pathology
  • Lactate - Ischemia, sepsis
  • Urinalysis, Œ≤-hCG in females of childbearing age
  • Upright CXR or left lateral decubitus - Free air
  • CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast - Gold standard for most causes
  • RUQ ultrasound if biliary pathology suspected
  • ECG - Rule out inferior MI

Hyponatremia (Na <135)

TIERED DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
TIER 1: Acuity/Severity Assessment
  • Symptomatic (seizures, altered mental status)? ‚Üí Treat urgently with 3% saline
  • Severe (Na <120)? ‚Üí High risk of seizures, cerebral edema
  • Acute (<48 hours)? ‚Üí Higher risk of symptoms, can correct faster
  • Chronic (>48 hours)? ‚Üí Risk of osmotic demyelination if corrected too fast
TIER 2: Volume Status Assessment
  • Hypovolemic: Dry mucous membranes, orthostatic vitals, low JVP, poor skin turgor
  • Euvolemic: Normal exam, no edema, normal JVP
  • Hypervolemic: Edema, JVD, ascites, crackles
TIER 3: Laboratory Pattern
  • Serum osmolality <280 ‚Üí True hyponatremia (hypotonic)
  • Serum osmolality 280-295 ‚Üí Isotonic (pseudohyponatremia)
  • Serum osmolality >295 ‚Üí Hypertonic (hyperglycemia, mannitol)
  • Urine sodium <20 ‚Üí Renal conservation (hypovolemia, CHF, cirrhosis)
  • Urine sodium >40 ‚Üí Renal losses (diuretics, SIADH, adrenal insufficiency)
  • Urine osmolality <100 ‚Üí Dilute urine (psychogenic polydipsia, beer potomania)
  • Urine osmolality >100 ‚Üí Concentrated urine (SIADH, hypovolemia)
TIER 4: Pathophysiologic Category
Hypovolemic Hyponatremia
  • GI losses - Vomiting, diarrhea, NG suction (Urine Na <20)
  • Renal losses - Diuretics, salt-wasting nephropathy (Urine Na >40)
  • Third spacing - Pancreatitis, burns, peritonitis (Urine Na <20)
  • #51 Adrenal Insufficiency - Hyperkalemia, hypotension, hyperpigmentation (Urine Na >40)
  • Cerebral salt wasting - Post-neurosurgery (Urine Na >40)
Euvolemic Hyponatremia
  • #38 SIADH - Urine Na >40, Urine osm >100, euvolemic on exam
  • #50 Hypothyroidism - Bradycardia, delayed reflexes, low TSH
  • Psychogenic polydipsia - Psychiatric history, very dilute urine (osm <100)
  • Beer potomania - Alcoholism, poor protein intake
  • Exercise-associated hyponatremia - Marathon, excessive free water
Hypervolemic Hyponatremia
  • #2 Heart Failure - Edema, JVD, crackles, elevated BNP
  • #54 Cirrhosis - Ascites, spider angiomas, jaundice
  • Nephrotic syndrome - Severe proteinuria >3g/day, edema
  • Advanced CKD - GFR <30, uremia
Pseudohyponatremia
  • Hyperglycemia - Correct Na: add 1.6 for every 100 mg/dL glucose >100
  • Hyperlipidemia - Triglycerides >1500, milky serum
  • Hyperproteinemia - Multiple myeloma, paraproteinemia
‚ö†Ô∏è MUST NOT MISS
  • #37 Symptomatic Severe Hyponatremia - 3% saline, goal increase 4-6 mEq/L in first 4-6 hours
  • Osmotic demyelination risk - Don't correct >10-12 mEq/L in 24 hours if chronic
  • #51 Adrenal Crisis - Hypotension, hyperkalemia, needs hydrocortisone + fluids
  • Cerebral salt wasting - Neurosurgical patient, needs salt replacement not restriction
üî¨ INITIAL WORKUP
  • Serum osmolality - Differentiate true vs pseudo-hyponatremia
  • Urine sodium and osmolality - Key to diagnosis
  • BMP - Assess potassium, glucose, renal function
  • Assess volume status - Physical exam critical
  • TSH, AM cortisol - Rule out hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency
  • Lipid panel if lipemic serum - Pseudohyponatremia
  • Medication review - Diuretics, SSRIs, carbamazepine, NSAIDs
  • CXR if SIADH suspected - Malignancy, pneumonia

Hyperkalemia (K >5.5)

TIERED DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
TIER 1: Acuity/Severity Assessment
  • ECG changes (peaked T, wide QRS, sine wave)? ‚Üí EMERGENT - give calcium immediately
  • Severe (K >6.5)? ‚Üí High risk of arrhythmia
  • Symptomatic (weakness, palpitations)? ‚Üí More urgent
  • Rate of rise? Acute hyperkalemia more dangerous than chronic
TIER 2: ECG Pattern
  • Peaked T waves ‚Üí Early sign (K 5.5-6.5)
  • Widened QRS, PR prolongation ‚Üí Moderate (K 6.5-7.5)
  • Loss of P wave, sine wave ‚Üí Severe (K >7.5), pre-arrest
  • Normal ECG? Reassuring but doesn't rule out need for treatment
TIER 3: Associated Features
  • AKI/oliguria ‚Üí Impaired excretion
  • Medications: ACEI, ARB, spironolactone, NSAIDs, heparin
  • Recent chemotherapy ‚Üí Tumor lysis syndrome
  • Muscle pain, dark urine ‚Üí Rhabdomyolysis
  • Hemolyzed specimen? ‚Üí Recheck, may be falsely elevated
TIER 4: Pathophysiologic Category
Increased Intake (Rare)
  • IV potassium - Iatrogenic, infusion too rapid
  • Massive blood transfusion - Old blood high in K
  • High-K foods with impaired excretion - Bananas, oranges, salt substitutes
Transcellular Shift
  • #47 DKA - Insulin deficiency, acidosis (total body K actually low)
  • Metabolic acidosis - Non-AG acidosis (RTA)
  • #85 Rhabdomyolysis - Muscle breakdown, elevated CK, myoglobinuria
  • #86 Tumor Lysis Syndrome - Recent chemotherapy, elevated LDH/uric acid/phos
  • Massive hemolysis - Elevated LDH, low haptoglobin
  • Digitalis toxicity - Inhibits Na-K ATPase
  • Succinylcholine - In burns, neuromuscular disease
Decreased Excretion - Renal
  • #35 Acute Kidney Injury - Elevated Cr, oliguria
  • #46 CKD/ESRD - GFR <30, chronic elevation
  • Type IV RTA - Low aldosterone, acidosis, diabetic nephropathy
Decreased Excretion - Medications
  • RAAS inhibitors - ACEI, ARB, spironolactone, eplerenone
  • NSAIDs - Inhibit renin release
  • Heparin - Inhibits aldosterone synthesis
  • Trimethoprim, pentamidine - Block K secretion in collecting duct
Decreased Excretion - Hormonal
  • #51 Adrenal Insufficiency - Hyponatremia, hypotension, hyperpigmentation
  • Hypoaldosteronism - Type IV RTA pattern
Pseudohyperkalemia
  • Hemolyzed specimen - Most common cause, recheck
  • Severe leukocytosis/thrombocytosis - K release from cells in tube
  • Prolonged tourniquet time - Fist clenching during draw
‚ö†Ô∏è MUST NOT MISS
  • #36 ECG changes - Calcium gluconate/chloride STAT for cardiac membrane stabilization
  • K >6.5 - Aggressive treatment even without ECG changes
  • #85 Rhabdomyolysis - Aggressive fluids, monitor for compartment syndrome
  • #86 Tumor Lysis Syndrome - Rasburicase, aggressive hydration, monitor closely
  • #51 Adrenal Crisis - Hydrocortisone + fluids, may have hyponatremia too
üî¨ INITIAL WORKUP
  • STAT ECG - Look for peaked T, widened QRS
  • Repeat potassium if hemolyzed specimen
  • BMP - Assess renal function, other electrolytes
  • Venous blood gas - Assess for acidosis
  • CK, urinalysis - Rhabdomyolysis (myoglobin)
  • LDH, uric acid, phosphate - Tumor lysis if recent chemotherapy
  • Medication review - ACEI, ARB, spironolactone, NSAIDs
  • Consider cortisol, aldosterone if unexplained

GI Bleeding

TIERED DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
TIER 1: Acuity/Severity Assessment
  • Hemodynamically unstable (SBP <90, HR >120)? ‚Üí Massive bleed, transfuse immediately
  • Active hematemesis or large volume hematochezia? ‚Üí Ongoing bleed
  • Hgb drop >2 g/dL? ‚Üí Significant blood loss
  • GBS (Glasgow-Blatchford) ‚â•12? ‚Üí High risk, needs urgent endoscopy
TIER 2: Localization - Upper vs Lower
  • Hematemesis (bright red blood/coffee grounds)? ‚Üí Upper GI source
  • Melena (black tarry stool)? ‚Üí Upper GI source (usually)
  • Hematochezia (bright red blood per rectum)? ‚Üí Lower GI OR massive upper GI
  • BUN/Cr ratio >30? ‚Üí Suggests upper GI bleed
  • NG lavage positive? ‚Üí Confirms upper GI source
TIER 3: Associated Features
  • Known cirrhosis/varices? ‚Üí Variceal hemorrhage
  • NSAID/aspirin use? ‚Üí PUD
  • Anticoagulation? ‚Üí Over-anticoagulation, need reversal
  • Recent vomiting then hematemesis? ‚Üí Mallory-Weiss tear
  • Age >60 + painless bleeding? ‚Üí Angiodysplasia, diverticulosis
  • Abdominal pain + bloody diarrhea? ‚Üí Mesenteric ischemia, IBD
TIER 4: Pathophysiologic Category
Upper GI - Variceal
  • #63 Variceal Hemorrhage - Known cirrhosis, massive hematemesis
  • Portal gastropathy - Cirrhosis, oozing rather than brisk bleeding
Upper GI - Non-Variceal
  • #53 Peptic Ulcer Disease - Epigastric pain, NSAID use, H. pylori
  • Mallory-Weiss Tear - Retching/vomiting preceding hematemesis
  • Esophagitis/GERD - Chronic heartburn, erosions
  • Gastritis - NSAID use, alcohol, stress
  • Dieulafoy Lesion - Massive bleeding, no preceding symptoms
  • Malignancy - Weight loss, dysphagia, anemia
Lower GI
  • Diverticulosis - Age >60, painless hematochezia, most common lower GI cause
  • Angiodysplasia - Elderly, chronic kidney disease
  • Hemorrhoids - Bright red blood on toilet paper, painless
  • IBD (Crohn's/UC) - Bloody diarrhea, abdominal pain, chronic
  • #61 Mesenteric Ischemia - Severe pain, bloody diarrhea, AFib/atherosclerosis
  • Ischemic Colitis - Elderly, sudden onset, LLQ pain
  • Colorectal cancer - Change in bowel habits, weight loss
  • Infectious colitis - Diarrhea, fever, recent travel
Massive Upper GI (presenting as hematochezia)
  • #53 Massive PUD - Can cause bright red blood per rectum if brisk
  • #63 Massive Variceal Bleed - Hemodynamically unstable
‚ö†Ô∏è MUST NOT MISS
  • #63 Variceal Hemorrhage - Octreotide, antibiotics, urgent endoscopy, consider TIPS
  • #53 Massive Upper GI Bleed - 2 large-bore IVs, transfuse, urgent endoscopy
  • #61 Mesenteric Ischemia - CTA, vascular surgery consult
  • Over-anticoagulation - #87 Anticoagulation reversal (PCC, FFP, vitamin K)
  • Aortoenteric Fistula - Prior AAA repair + "herald bleed" then massive bleeding
üî¨ INITIAL WORKUP
  • CBC - Baseline hemoglobin, monitor q4-6h
  • Type and screen (crossmatch 4 units if severe)
  • PT/INR, PTT - Coagulopathy
  • BMP - BUN/Cr ratio >30 suggests UGIB, renal function
  • Lactate - Ischemia
  • NG lavage if upper source suspected (controversial)
  • Glasgow-Blatchford Score - Risk stratification
  • Rockall Score - Post-endoscopy mortality risk
  • NPO, 2 large-bore IVs
  • GI consult for endoscopy (EGD for upper, colonoscopy for lower)
  • Consider CTA if brisk bleeding and endoscopy non-diagnostic

Acute Kidney Injury (Elevated Creatinine)

TIERED DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
TIER 1: Acuity/Severity Assessment
  • Severe (Cr >3x baseline or Cr >4 with acute rise)? ‚Üí High risk for dialysis
  • Oliguria/anuria (<400 mL/24hr)? ‚Üí More severe, worse prognosis
  • Hyperkalemia with ECG changes? ‚Üí Emergent dialysis indication
  • Volume overload with pulmonary edema? ‚Üí Emergent dialysis indication
TIER 2: Pre-renal vs Intrinsic vs Post-renal
  • Pre-renal: FeNa <1%, FeUrea <35%, BUN/Cr >20, responds to fluids
  • Intrinsic: FeNa >2%, casts on UA, doesn't respond to fluids
  • Post-renal: Hydronephrosis on ultrasound, bladder distension, urinary retention
TIER 3: Associated Features & Context
  • Hypotension, decreased PO intake ‚Üí Pre-renal
  • Recent contrast, NSAIDs, aminoglycosides ‚Üí ATN
  • Rash, eosinophiluria ‚Üí AIN
  • Hematuria, RBC casts, proteinuria ‚Üí Glomerulonephritis
  • Myoglobinuria, elevated CK ‚Üí Rhabdomyolysis
  • Recent chemotherapy ‚Üí Tumor lysis syndrome
  • Prostate enlargement, unable to void ‚Üí Post-renal obstruction
TIER 4: Pathophysiologic Category
Pre-Renal (60% of AKI)
  • True volume depletion - Vomiting, diarrhea, poor PO, hemorrhage
  • Decreased effective circulating volume - #2 CHF, #54 Cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome
  • Hypotension - #25 Septic shock, #10 Cardiogenic shock
  • Renal vasoconstriction - NSAIDs, ACEI/ARB, hepatorenal syndrome
  • Abdominal compartment syndrome - Increased intra-abdominal pressure
Intrinsic - ATN (Acute Tubular Necrosis)
  • Ischemic ATN - Prolonged hypotension, surgery, sepsis
  • Nephrotoxic ATN - Contrast, aminoglycosides, vancomycin, amphotericin
  • Pigment nephropathy - #85 Rhabdomyolysis (myoglobin), hemolysis
  • Crystal nephropathy - Uric acid (#86 TLS), oxalate, acyclovir
Intrinsic - Glomerulonephritis
  • Post-infectious GN - Recent strep infection, hematuria, proteinuria
  • Rapidly progressive GN - Crescentic, ANCA-associated, anti-GBM
  • Lupus nephritis - SLE, proteinuria, active urine sediment
  • IgA nephropathy - Hematuria, URI prodrome
Intrinsic - Acute Interstitial Nephritis
  • Drug-induced AIN - NSAIDs, PPIs, antibiotics, rash, eosinophiluria
  • Infection-related AIN - Pyelonephritis, viral
Intrinsic - Vascular
  • Renal artery stenosis/thrombosis - Started ACEI, sudden HTN
  • Renal vein thrombosis - Nephrotic syndrome, hypercoagulable
  • Atheroembolic disease - Recent vascular procedure
  • Thrombotic microangiopathy - #79 TTP/HUS, malignant HTN
Post-Renal (5-10% of AKI)
  • Bladder outlet obstruction - BPH, urinary retention, neurogenic bladder
  • Ureteral obstruction - Bilateral stones, malignancy, retroperitoneal fibrosis
  • Intratubular obstruction - Crystals, myeloma cast nephropathy
‚ö†Ô∏è MUST NOT MISS
  • #85 Rhabdomyolysis - Aggressive fluids (200-300 mL/hr), avoid loop diuretics initially
  • #86 Tumor Lysis Syndrome - Rasburicase, aggressive hydration, close monitoring
  • Post-renal obstruction - Bladder scan, renal US, Foley/nephrostomy if bilateral
üî¨ INITIAL WORKUP
  • BMP - Compare to baseline creatinine, assess for hyperkalemia
  • Urinalysis with microscopy - RBC casts (GN), WBC casts (AIN/pyelo), muddy brown (ATN)
  • Urine sodium, FeNa, FeUrea - Differentiate pre-renal from intrinsic
  • CBC - Anemia, schistocytes (TMA)
  • CK if rhabdomyolysis suspected
  • LDH, uric acid, phosphate if tumor lysis suspected
  • Renal ultrasound - Assess for hydronephrosis (post-renal)
  • Review medications - NSAIDs, ACEI/ARB, aminoglycosides, contrast
  • Bladder scan - Urinary retention
  • Consider: Complement, ANCA, anti-GBM, ANA if GN suspected

Acute Focal Neurologic Deficit

TIERED DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
TIER 1: Acuity/Severity Assessment
  • Time last known normal <4.5 hours? ‚Üí tPA candidate for ischemic stroke
  • Time last known normal <24 hours with large vessel occlusion? ‚Üí Thrombectomy candidate
  • Severe symptoms (NIHSS >10, aphasia, hemianopia)? ‚Üí Large vessel occlusion likely
  • Decreased level of consciousness? ‚Üí Hemorrhage, large infarct, posterior fossa
TIER 2: Timing Pattern
  • Sudden onset (seconds-minutes): Ischemic stroke, Hemorrhagic stroke, SAH
  • Acute onset (minutes-hours): Stroke, Seizure with Todd paralysis, Complicated migraine
  • Subacute (hours-days): Brain abscess, Tumor, Subdural hematoma
  • Fluctuating symptoms that resolved: TIA, Seizure
TIER 3: Pattern of Deficit
  • Unilateral weakness + aphasia ‚Üí Middle cerebral artery (MCA) stroke
  • Unilateral weakness + sensory loss ‚Üí Cortical or subcortical stroke
  • Isolated aphasia ‚Üí Left MCA (dominant hemisphere)
  • Crossed findings (face one side, body other) ‚Üí Brainstem stroke
  • Ataxia + vertigo + diplopia ‚Üí Posterior circulation stroke
  • Ascending paralysis ‚Üí Guillain-Barr√© syndrome
  • Bilateral weakness + sensory level ‚Üí Spinal cord lesion
TIER 4: Pathophysiologic Category
Vascular - Ischemic
  • #67 Ischemic Stroke - Sudden onset, vascular territory, CT initially negative
  • #70 TIA - Transient symptoms <24 hours (usually <1 hour), fully resolved
  • Lacunar infarct - Pure motor/sensory syndrome, small deep infarcts
Vascular - Hemorrhagic
  • #68 Intracranial Hemorrhage - Headache, HTN, blood on CT
  • #69 Subarachnoid Hemorrhage - Worst headache, nuchal rigidity, sentinel bleed
  • Subdural Hematoma - Trauma history, crescent-shaped blood on CT
  • Epidural Hematoma - Trauma, lucid interval, lens-shaped blood on CT
Infectious
  • #29 Meningitis - Fever, neck stiffness, headache, photophobia
  • #66 Encephalitis - Fever, altered mental status, seizures, focal deficits
  • Brain Abscess - Fever, focal deficits, ring-enhancing lesion
Inflammatory/Autoimmune
  • Multiple Sclerosis - Relapsing-remitting, young female, MRI plaques
  • #71 Guillain-Barr√© - Ascending paralysis, areflexia, recent infection
  • Transverse Myelitis - Acute paraplegia, sensory level, bowel/bladder dysfunction
Structural
  • Brain Tumor - Subacute, progressive, headache worse in AM
  • #73 Increased ICP - Headache, vomiting, papilledema, Cushing triad
  • #74 Spinal Cord Compression - Back pain, weakness, sensory level, sphincter dysfunction
Metabolic/Toxic
  • Hypoglycemia - Focal deficits possible, resolves with glucose
  • Wernicke Encephalopathy - Ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, confusion
  • Posterior Reversible Encephalopathy Syndrome (PRES) - HTN, seizures, vision changes
Seizure-Related
  • #65 Status Epilepticus - Ongoing seizure activity
  • Todd Paralysis - Post-ictal weakness, resolves over hours
  • Non-convulsive status - Subtle or no motor activity, EEG diagnostic
Neuromuscular
  • #72 Myasthenia Gravis Crisis - Respiratory failure, ptosis, diplopia
  • Botulism - Descending paralysis, pupillary involvement
  • Lambert-Eaton - Proximal weakness, improves with use, malignancy association
‚ö†Ô∏è MUST NOT MISS
  • #67 Ischemic Stroke - tPA within 4.5 hours if candidate, thrombectomy up to 24 hours
  • #68 Hemorrhagic Stroke - Hold anticoagulation, neurosurgery consult, BP control
  • #69 SAH - CT then LP if negative, neurosurgery consult, nimodipine
  • #74 Spinal Cord Compression - MRI spine emergently, neurosurgery/radiation oncology
  • #71 Guillain-Barr√© - ICU monitoring for respiratory failure, IVIG or plasmapheresis
  • #72 Myasthenia Crisis - ICU, respiratory support, IVIG or plasmapheresis
üî¨ INITIAL WORKUP
  • STAT head CT non-contrast - Rule out hemorrhage (required before tPA)
  • Fingerstick glucose - Hypoglycemia can mimic stroke
  • NIHSS score - Quantify severity, track progression
  • Time last known normal - Critical for tPA/thrombectomy decision
  • CT angiography (CTA) head/neck - Large vessel occlusion
  • MRI brain with DWI - Most sensitive for ischemic stroke (if time permits)
  • ECG - Atrial fibrillation (cardioembolic source)
  • CBC, PT/INR, PTT - Coagulopathy, thrombocytopenia
  • BMP, troponin
  • Lipid panel, HbA1c - Risk factors
  • LP if SAH suspected and CT negative
  • MRI spine if cord compression suspected

Seizure / Status Epilepticus

TIERED DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
TIER 1: Acuity/Severity Assessment
  • Ongoing seizure >5 minutes? ‚Üí #65 Status epilepticus - give benzos immediately
  • Recurrent seizures without recovery? ‚Üí Status epilepticus
  • Post-ictal with persistent altered mental status >30 min? ‚Üí Non-convulsive SE, EEG
  • First seizure ever? ‚Üí Higher acuity, needs full workup
TIER 2: Seizure Type & Context
  • Focal onset (one body part) ‚Üí Focal seizure, structural lesion more likely
  • Generalized onset (both sides) ‚Üí Generalized seizure or rapid spread
  • Known epilepsy with medication compliance? ‚Üí Breakthrough vs non-compliance
  • Recent medication changes? ‚Üí Lowered seizure threshold
  • Provoked (fever, drugs, alcohol withdrawal, hypoglycemia)? ‚Üí Treat underlying cause
TIER 3: Associated Features
  • Fever + headache + neck stiffness ‚Üí Meningitis/encephalitis
  • Focal deficits persisting after seizure ‚Üí Stroke, mass lesion, or Todd paralysis
  • Recent head trauma ‚Üí Post-traumatic seizure, hemorrhage
  • Severe hyponatremia (Na <120) ‚Üí Metabolic seizure
  • Recent alcohol cessation ‚Üí Withdrawal seizure
  • Tongue biting, incontinence ‚Üí Witnessed generalized tonic-clonic
TIER 4: Pathophysiologic Category
Structural
  • #67 Ischemic Stroke - New focal seizure in elderly
  • #68 Intracranial Hemorrhage - Headache, HTN, blood on CT
  • Brain Tumor - Subacute onset, progressive
  • Traumatic Brain Injury - Recent head trauma
  • Cerebral Venous Sinus Thrombosis - Headache, focal deficit, hypercoagulable
  • Vascular Malformation - AVM, cavernoma
Infectious
  • #29 Bacterial Meningitis - Fever, neck stiffness, headache
  • #66 Encephalitis - Fever, altered mental status, CSF lymphocytosis
  • Brain Abscess - Fever, focal deficits, ring-enhancing lesion
  • Neurocysticercosis - Travel to endemic area, multiple cysts
Metabolic
  • #37 Severe Hyponatremia - Na <120, rapid correction dangerous
  • Hypoglycemia - Check glucose first, give D50 if <50
  • #36 Severe Hyperkalemia - Rarely causes seizures but check
  • #40 Hypercalcemia - Calcium >14
  • Hypomagnesemia - Check Mg, repletion may prevent recurrence
  • Uremia - #35 Severe AKI/ESRD, BUN >100
  • Hepatic Encephalopathy - #56 Ammonia >100, asterixis
Toxic
  • #83 Alcohol Withdrawal - 24-48 hours after last drink, tremor, hallucinations
  • Benzodiazepine Withdrawal - Rapid taper or cessation
  • #81 Drug Intoxication - Cocaine, amphetamines, tramadol, bupropion
  • Serotonin Syndrome - SSRIs, hyperthermia, clonus
  • Drug Withdrawal - Barbiturates, baclofen
Inflammatory/Autoimmune
  • Autoimmune Encephalitis - Anti-NMDA, subacute, psychiatric symptoms
  • CNS Vasculitis - Subacute, multiple infarcts
  • Posterior Reversible Encephalopathy Syndrome (PRES) - Hypertension, visual changes
Idiopathic/Primary Epilepsy
  • Known epilepsy with medication non-compliance
  • Known epilepsy with breakthrough seizure
  • New-onset idiopathic (diagnosis of exclusion after workup negative)
‚ö†Ô∏è MUST NOT MISS
  • #65 Status Epilepticus - Benzos immediately (lorazepam 4mg or diazepam 10mg), then load AED
  • #67 Acute Stroke - CT, consider thrombolytics if seizure at onset
  • #29 Meningitis - LP after CT, antibiotics within 1 hour
  • #66 Encephalitis - Acyclovir empirically if suspected
  • Hypoglycemia - Check glucose, give D50 if <50
  • #37 Severe Hyponatremia - 3% saline cautiously (avoid osmotic demyelination)
  • #83 Alcohol Withdrawal - Benzos, thiamine, monitor for DTs
üî¨ INITIAL WORKUP
  • Fingerstick glucose - FIRST TEST, hypoglycemia easily treatable
  • BMP - Sodium (hyponatremia most common metabolic cause), glucose, Ca, Mg
  • CBC - Infection
  • Toxicology screen - Drug intoxication
  • Anticonvulsant levels if on medications - Assess compliance, subtherapeutic levels
  • Head CT non-contrast - Bleed, mass, stroke
  • LP if concern for meningitis/encephalitis (after CT if focal deficits)
  • MRI brain if new-onset or concern for structural lesion (more sensitive than CT)
  • EEG - Especially if prolonged post-ictal state (non-convulsive SE)
  • Pregnancy test in females of childbearing age
  • Consider: Ammonia (hepatic encephalopathy), alcohol level, COVID, HIV

Acute Anemia (Hgb Drop)

TIERED DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
TIER 1: Acuity/Severity Assessment
  • Hemodynamically unstable (hypotension, tachycardia)? ‚Üí Active bleeding, transfuse
  • Severe (Hgb <7)? ‚Üí Consider transfusion even if stable
  • Symptomatic (chest pain, dyspnea, syncope)? ‚Üí Lower threshold to transfuse
  • Acute drop (>2 g/dL in 24 hours)? ‚Üí Active process, find source
TIER 2: Bleeding vs Hemolysis vs Decreased Production
  • Evidence of bleeding? ‚Üí GI, retroperitoneal, post-op, traumatic
  • Elevated reticulocyte count? ‚Üí Hemolysis or acute bleed with marrow response
  • Low reticulocyte count? ‚Üí Decreased production (marrow failure, nutritional)
  • Jaundice + dark urine? ‚Üí Hemolysis
  • Schistocytes on smear? ‚Üí Microangiopathic hemolysis (TTP, HUS, DIC)
TIER 3: MCV & Morphology
  • Microcytic (MCV <80) ‚Üí Iron deficiency, thalassemia, chronic disease
  • Normocytic (MCV 80-100) ‚Üí Acute bleed, hemolysis, chronic disease
  • Macrocytic (MCV >100) ‚Üí B12/folate deficiency, alcohol, hypothyroidism, MDS
  • Schistocytes ‚Üí TTP/HUS, DIC, MAHA
  • Spherocytes ‚Üí Hereditary spherocytosis, autoimmune hemolysis
  • Sickle cells ‚Üí Sickle cell disease
TIER 4: Pathophysiologic Category
Acute Blood Loss
  • #53 Upper GI Bleed - Hematemesis, melena, BUN/Cr >20
  • Lower GI Bleed - Hematochezia, diverticulosis, angiodysplasia
  • Retroperitoneal Bleed - Anticoagulation, flank pain, CT findings
  • Post-operative - Recent surgery, drain output, tachycardia
  • Traumatic - Obvious trauma, FAST positive
  • Ruptured ectopic pregnancy - Female, pelvic pain, positive Œ≤-hCG
Hemolytic Anemia
  • #77 Hemolytic Anemia - Elevated LDH, low haptoglobin, high retic, indirect bili
  • #76 Sickle Cell Crisis - Sickle cells on smear, pain crisis
  • #79 TTP - Pentad: thrombocytopenia, MAHA, renal failure, fever, neuro changes
  • #79 HUS - Triad: MAHA, thrombocytopenia, AKI (often post-diarrheal)
  • #78 DIC - Prolonged PT/PTT, low fibrinogen, bleeding + thrombosis
  • Autoimmune Hemolytic Anemia - Positive DAT (Coombs), spherocytes
  • G6PD Deficiency - Oxidative stress trigger (infection, fava beans)
  • #80 Transfusion Reaction - Recent transfusion, fever, hemoglobinuria
Decreased Production - Nutritional
  • Iron Deficiency - Low ferritin, low MCV, history of bleeding
  • B12 Deficiency - High MCV, neurologic symptoms (paresthesias)
  • Folate Deficiency - High MCV, alcoholism, malabsorption
Decreased Production - Marrow
  • Aplastic Anemia - Pancytopenia, bone marrow biopsy hypocellular
  • Leukemia/Lymphoma - Blasts on smear, lymphadenopathy
  • Myelodysplastic Syndrome - Elderly, dysplasia on smear
  • Bone Marrow Infiltration - Metastatic cancer, fibrosis
Chronic Disease
  • Anemia of Chronic Disease - Normal/low MCV, low iron but normal/high ferritin
  • #46 CKD - Low EPO, normocytic
  • Chronic Inflammation - Cancer, autoimmune disease
‚ö†Ô∏è MUST NOT MISS
  • #53 Massive GI Bleed - 2 large-bore IVs, transfuse, emergent endoscopy
  • #79 TTP - Plasmapheresis emergently, avoid platelets
  • #78 DIC - Treat underlying cause, replace factors/platelets as needed
  • #76 Sickle Cell Crisis - Hydration, pain control, transfusion if severe
  • Acute Leukemia - Blasts on smear, needs oncology consult
üî¨ INITIAL WORKUP
  • CBC with reticulocyte count - Assess severity and marrow response
  • Peripheral smear - Morphology (schistocytes, spherocytes, blasts)
  • BMP - Renal function (HUS/TTP)
  • LDH, indirect bilirubin, haptoglobin - Hemolysis markers
  • PT/INR, PTT, fibrinogen - DIC screen
  • Type and screen - Prepare for transfusion
  • Stool guaiac - Occult GI bleeding
  • Iron studies, B12, folate - If chronic or macrocytic
  • DAT (Coombs) if hemolysis suspected
  • Hemoglobin electrophoresis if sickle cell suspected
  • Consider: Bone marrow biopsy if unexplained cytopenias

Thrombocytopenia (Platelets <150k)

TIERED DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
TIER 1: Acuity/Severity Assessment
  • Severe (<20k)? ‚Üí High bleeding risk, consider platelet transfusion
  • Active bleeding (ICH, GI, etc.)? ‚Üí Emergent - transfuse platelets, find cause
  • Schistocytes + AKI + neuro changes? ‚Üí #79 TTP - plasmapheresis emergently
  • Recent heparin exposure + drop >50%? ‚Üí #79 HIT - stop heparin immediately
TIER 2: Isolated vs Multi-lineage
  • Isolated thrombocytopenia? ‚Üí ITP, HIT, TTP, drug-induced
  • Thrombocytopenia + anemia? ‚Üí TTP/HUS, DIC, bone marrow failure
  • Pancytopenia (low WBC/RBC/PLT)? ‚Üí Bone marrow failure, leukemia, hypersplenism
TIER 3: Associated Features
  • Schistocytes on smear ‚Üí TTP/HUS, DIC, mechanical valve
  • Bleeding/bruising ‚Üí Consumptive or severe thrombocytopenia
  • Prolonged PT/PTT + low fibrinogen ‚Üí DIC
  • Recent heparin ‚Üí HIT (usually days 5-10 of exposure)
  • Fever + diarrhea ‚Üí HUS (often E. coli O157:H7)
  • Splenomegaly ‚Üí Hypersplenism, lymphoma, cirrhosis
TIER 4: Pathophysiologic Category
Decreased Production
  • Bone Marrow Failure - Aplastic anemia, MDS, pancytopenia
  • Leukemia/Lymphoma - Blasts on smear, lymphadenopathy
  • Chemotherapy - Recent chemo, expected nadir
  • Nutritional - B12/folate deficiency (with macrocytic anemia)
  • Viral Suppression - HIV, EBV, CMV, parvovirus
Increased Destruction - Immune
  • #79 ITP (Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura) - Isolated low platelets, otherwise well
  • Drug-Induced - Heparin, quinine, sulfonamides, vancomycin, linezolid
  • SLE - ANA positive, other cytopenias
  • Post-Transfusion Purpura - 5-10 days after transfusion
Increased Destruction - Non-Immune
  • #79 HIT (Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia) - Drop >50%, thrombosis, 4Ts score
  • #79 TTP - Pentad: MAHA, thrombocytopenia, AKI, fever, neuro (need only 2)
  • #79 HUS - Triad: MAHA, thrombocytopenia, AKI, often post-diarrheal
  • #78 DIC - Prolonged PT/PTT, low fibrinogen, bleeding + clotting
  • HELLP Syndrome - Pregnancy, hemolysis, elevated LFTs, low platelets
  • Mechanical Destruction - Mechanical valve, ECMO, LVAD
Sequestration
  • Hypersplenism - Splenomegaly, #54 cirrhosis, portal hypertension
  • Hypothermia - Massive transfusion, trauma
Dilutional
  • Massive Transfusion - Trauma, surgery, >10 units PRBCs
Pseudothrombocytopenia
  • EDTA-induced clumping - Recheck in citrate or heparin tube
  • Platelet satellitism - Adherence to WBCs

  • #79 HIT - Stop ALL heparin (including flushes), start alternative anticoagulation

  • Acute Leukemia - Blasts on smear, oncology consult
